## Karl Linnaeus and the Classification of Humans

### 1. **Linnaeus as the Type Specimen for *Homo sapiens***

- In *Systema Naturae* (10th edition, 1758), Carl Linnaeus formally assigned the binomial name *Homo sapiens* to humans.
- He **did not designate a physical type specimen**, as modern taxonomy would later require.
- In 1959, the International Commission on Zoological Nomenclature (ICZN) ruled that **Linnaeus himself is the type specimen for *Homo sapiens***, based on the principle of *tautonymy* and lack of any other holotype.
- This makes Linnaeus **the official nomenclatural standard** for the human species under zoological classification.

---

### 2. **Human Subspecies in Linnaean Taxonomy**

In the same 1758 edition of *Systema Naturae*, Linnaeus subdivided *Homo sapiens* into four continental-based varieties, which he regarded as **subspecies**. These are not subspecies in the modern genetic sense, but more like *varietal forms* used in early taxonomic theory.

Each was described with brief physiological and behavioral characteristics, often influenced by 18th-century racial theories:

| Subspecies Name             | Continental Label     | Description (as per Linnaeus)                                |
|----------------------------|-----------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------|
| *Homo sapiens europaeus*   | European              | "Sanguine, muscular, hair flowing, blue eyes, gentle, acute" |
| *Homo sapiens asiaticus*   | Asian                 | "Melancholy, stiff, black hair, dark eyes, stern"            |
| *Homo sapiens americanus*  | American (Indigenous) | "Choleric, erect, black hair, reddish skin, obstinate"       |
| *Homo sapiens afer*        | African               | "Phlegmatic, relaxed, black skin, silky hair, crafty"        |

In the 1766 edition, he later added:

| Subspecies Name             | Description                       |
|----------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| *Homo sapiens monstrosus*  | Mythological or malformed humans  |
| *Homo sapiens ferus*       | Feral or "wild" humans (e.g. children raised by animals) |

These categories reflect early efforts at biological anthropology, but they are **now obsolete** in both taxonomic and anthropological sciences due to:
- Lack of genetic validity
- Overlap between populations
- Ethnocentric and non-scientific descriptors

---

### 3. **Status of Human Subspecies in Modern Biology**

- Today, **no subspecies** of *Homo sapiens* are formally recognized.
- All living humans belong to a **single species and subspecies**: *Homo sapiens sapiens*.
- Former hominins (like *Homo sapiens idaltu*, *Homo neanderthalensis*, *Homo erectus*) are treated as separate species or, in rare cases, archaic subspecies with unresolved taxonomic status.
- Genetic diversity among modern humans does not support clear biological subspecies divisions.
