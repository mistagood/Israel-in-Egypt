You raise a valid point. The idea of the Passover lamb being directly linked to atonement for sin doesn’t explicitly appear in Moses’ law as written. In fact, the scapegoat ritual in Leviticus 16, where one goat is sacrificed and another is sent into the wilderness bearing the sins of the people, is more closely tied to atonement in Moses’ writings. The Passover lamb, on the other hand, commemorates the Israelites' deliverance from Egypt (Exodus 12), not an atonement for sin. So, if we are to answer the question of what specific law Moses wrote that Jesus fulfilled, it requires a closer examination of Moses’ writings and how Jesus directly ties Himself to those laws.

The key here lies in the broader context of Moses’ law and prophecy. The two most concrete examples from the Torah that are often linked to Jesus’ fulfillment of the law are the sacrificial system and the prophecy of a coming prophet like Moses:

1. **Sacrificial System and Atonement**: Moses’ law in Leviticus 16 established the Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur), where a high priest would offer a sin offering for the people, and a scapegoat would symbolically carry away their sins. This system was an ongoing cycle, repeated year after year. In this sense, Jesus is seen as the fulfillment of this sacrificial law by offering Himself as a *once-for-all* sacrifice, as described in Hebrews 10:12-14. Jesus’ death is seen as the ultimate completion of this atonement ritual, where He not only takes on the role of the sacrificial goat but also the scapegoat, bearing away the sins of the people. While Moses didn’t directly say “the Messiah will be the atonement,” the framework of atonement in the law laid the foundation for the role Jesus claimed to fulfill.

2. **Deuteronomy 18:15-19**: Moses prophesied that God would raise up a prophet like him from among the people, and that this prophet would be the one the people must listen to. Jesus is often seen as the fulfillment of this prophecy because He leads the people out of a greater bondage, not just political or physical slavery, but the bondage of sin. This prophecy doesn’t directly say the prophet would also be a sacrifice, but the Jewish understanding of the Messiah evolved, especially in light of later prophecies (such as Isaiah 53) that spoke of a suffering servant.

3. **The Law of Righteousness**: The Torah sets a standard of righteousness that no one could fully meet. Moses’ law was designed to show that humanity is incapable of keeping God’s perfect standards on their own. In this way, Jesus fulfills the law by being the only one who perfectly kept the law’s demands (Matthew 5:17-18), not just in the letter, but in the spirit of the law. Jesus’ life and teachings centered around the deeper intent of the law, the heart behind it, as He showed in His Sermon on the Mount.

4. **Passover Lamb and Exodus**: While the Passover lamb itself in Moses' writings does not symbolize atonement for sin, Jesus’ death coincided with Passover, and early Christians saw His sacrifice as a new kind of deliverance, not from Egypt but from sin and death. Paul draws on this symbolism when he calls Jesus “our Passover lamb” in 1 Corinthians 5:7. Although this is a later interpretation, it illustrates how Jesus’ death was connected to the larger narrative of Moses’ writings on deliverance.

To summarize, Jesus fulfilled the law of Moses in these key ways:
- By becoming the ultimate fulfillment of the sacrificial system for atonement (Leviticus 16).
- By fulfilling the prophecy of the coming prophet (Deuteronomy 18:15-19).
- By perfectly keeping the law and embodying its true intent (Matthew 5:17-18).
- By recasting the deliverance narrative of Passover as a deeper spiritual deliverance from sin.

It is true that Moses doesn’t directly spell out a single law saying “the Messiah will come and atone for sins.” But Jesus’ fulfillment is seen in how He embodies, completes, and transcends the law’s systems, sacrifices, and prophecies.

### Levitical Atonement Ritual: A Quick Overview
In **Leviticus 16**, we have the Day of Atonement ritual, which centers around two goats:
1. **The Sin Offering (sacrificed goat):** One goat is sacrificed as a sin offering to atone for the sins of the people.
2. **The Scapegoat (Azazel):** The second goat is released into the wilderness, symbolically carrying away the sins of Israel. This scapegoat bears the burden of the people's sins and is sent away, never to return.

Now, if we look at the actions surrounding Jesus' supposed crucifixion, we can see several potential parallels to the Levitical atonement ritual as you outlined.

### Pilate as the High Priest: Casting Lots and the Two Figures

Pilate’s role as the one who casts lots between Jesus and Barabbas is indeed reminiscent of the high priest casting lots to determine which goat would be sacrificed and which would be released. According to **John 19:12**, Pilate attempted to release Jesus because he found Him innocent, much like the scapegoat is not sacrificed but is instead released to carry away the sins of the people.

- **Judas as the Sin Goat**: Judas, in your interpretation, acknowledges his guilt and returns the silver, confessing his sin in **Matthew 27:3-5**. - **Jesus as the Scapegoat**: According to your reading, Pilate’s attempt to release Jesus would cast Him as the scapegoat who is not sacrificed but sent away to bear the sins of the people. The key passage here is **Ezekiel 12**, where the "prince among them" is said to leave through the gate at twilight, carrying his burden. This would align with the scapegoat being sent into the wilderness to symbolically carry away the sins of the people.

### Ezekiel 12 and the “Prince Among Them”

**Ezekiel 12:12-13** describes the prince fleeing through the gate at twilight with a burden on his shoulder, hiding his face so he cannot be seen. This passage could be seen as a prophetic foreshadowing of the scapegoat ritual, where the sins of the people are carried out of sight.

### The Disappearance into the Wilderness

Your interpretation that Jesus did not die on the cross but instead disappeared into the wilderness, symbolically bearing the sins of the people, mirrors the role of the scapegoat in the Day of Atonement ritual. If this were the case, then the resurrection becomes unnecessary from a Levitical standpoint because the scapegoat doesn’t return—it’s gone, carrying away the sins forever. - Judas, who confessed his sin, is the sacrificial goat (the sin offering) who dies.

- Pilate, functioning as the high priest, casts the lots between Jesus and Barabbas, unknowingly participating in the fulfillment of the Day of Atonement ritual.

### Conclusion

This reinterpretation of the crucifixion aligns Jesus with the scapegoat rather than the sacrificial lamb. By connecting **Ezekiel 12** and **Leviticus 16**, your interpretation suggests that Jesus fulfilled Moses’ atonement law by carrying the sins of the people out of the public eye and disappearing into the wilderness, rather than being sacrificed as is traditionally understood.

In this framework, the crucifixion story becomes less about Jesus’ death and resurrection and more about the **completion of the atonement ritual**, where Judas takes on the role of the sacrificial sin goat, and Jesus embodies the scapegoat, fulfilling the law Moses established. This view raises intriguing theological implications and reimagines how Jesus could have fulfilled the law without adhering to the later Christian narratives of resurrection.

Deuteronomy 18 indeed presents an important framework for understanding prophecy and prophets. In verses 15-19, Moses speaks about the **prophet** whom God will raise up, and this is often interpreted as a reference to Jesus. However, in verses 20-22, it warns against **false prophets** who speak presumptuously in the name of the Lord, and provides a clear test: **if the prophet’s words do not come to pass**, the prophet has spoken presumptuously and should not be feared.

Let's break this down in the context of Jesus and Paul, particularly regarding the issue of unfulfilled prophecies.

### Deuteronomy 18:15-19 – The True Prophet
In these verses, Moses promises that God will raise up a prophet like himself from among the people. Many believe this prophet to be Jesus because:
- **Jesus fulfills the Mosaic role**: Both Moses and Jesus are lawgivers and leaders of their people. Jesus also emphasizes listening to His teachings, much like the prophet Moses.
- **Acts 3:22-23** and **Acts 7:37** explicitly reference this passage, identifying Jesus as the fulfillment of this prophecy.

### Deuteronomy 18:20-22 – The False Prophet
The passage warns about prophets who claim to speak in God’s name but make predictions that do not come true. A prophet like this is said to be speaking presumptuously and should be rejected. Here’s what the text says:

> “But the prophet who presumes to speak a word in my name that I have not commanded him to speak, or who speaks in the name of other gods, that prophet shall die. And if you say in your heart, ‘How may we know the word that the Lord has not spoken?’—when a prophet speaks in the name of the Lord, if the word does not come to pass or come true, that is a word that the Lord has not spoken; the prophet has spoken it presumptuously. You need not be afraid of him” (**Deuteronomy 18:20-22**).

### Paul’s Prophecies and Their Fulfillment
Paul, throughout his ministry, made several prophetic statements, some of which, as you point out, did not come to pass in the way he predicted. Let’s look at a few of these:

1. **The Imminent Return of Jesus**:
   - Paul strongly suggested that Jesus’ return was imminent and would happen during the lifetime of his followers. In **1 Thessalonians 4:15-17**, Paul writes:
     > “We who are alive, who are left until the coming of the Lord, will not precede those who have fallen asleep. For the Lord himself will descend from heaven…”
     
     Here, Paul seems to indicate that he expected Jesus to return within his lifetime, or at least within the generation of his listeners. This did not come to pass, leading many to question the accuracy of his prophetic statements.

2. **Change in Paul’s Messaging**:
   - As Paul grew older, his tone regarding Jesus’ return became more ambiguous. In **2 Timothy 4:6-8**, he writes as though he is preparing for death, acknowledging that he will not live to see Jesus’ return:
     > “For I am already being poured out like a drink offering, and the time for my departure is near. I have fought the good fight, I have finished the race, I have kept the faith.”
   
   This shift in Paul’s perspective shows a change from earlier statements about the imminent return of Christ.

### Paul and Deuteronomy 18’s Warning
If we take **Deuteronomy 18:22** as the standard for discerning false prophets, then Paul’s failed predictions about the return of Jesus could be seen as presumptuous. He prophesied an event that did not occur as he stated, which raises serious questions about his prophetic authority.

### Other Examples of Presumptuous Prophecies by Paul
- **Romans 11:26**: Paul says, “And so all Israel will be saved.” This prophecy remains problematic as it hasn't come to fruition in the way Paul envisioned. This is another instance where his prophetic declaration does not align with historical outcomes.
  
- **1 Corinthians 7:29**: Paul urges believers not to marry because “the time is short.” This advice was based on his belief in an impending apocalypse, which again, did not happen during that generation.

### Paul’s Evolving Theology
What’s striking about Paul is that as his early prophecies failed, he seemed to adapt his theology. In his earlier letters, Paul speaks with certainty about Jesus’ imminent return, but later, his focus shifts toward perseverance in faith and enduring trials, rather than expecting an immediate divine intervention.

This adaptability is often seen by critics as evidence that Paul was, in fact, speaking presumptuously at first. According to **Deuteronomy 18:22**, this makes him a false prophet in the biblical sense, as he made claims about God’s will that did not come to pass.

### Conclusion
