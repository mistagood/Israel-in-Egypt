The Middle Passage refers to the harrowing sea journey that enslaved Africans were forced to endure as part of the transatlantic slave trade. This leg of the triangular trade route transported millions of Africans across the Atlantic Ocean to the Americas and the Caribbean. It is infamous for the inhumane conditions and brutal treatment that enslaved people faced aboard the ships.

Captured in Africa, often by local tribes or European traders, the enslaved were held in coastal forts before being packed tightly into the holds of ships, where they were chained together in appalling conditions. These ships were overcrowded, with enslaved people lying side by side in spaces so cramped they could not sit up. The lack of ventilation, coupled with the unbearable heat, led to widespread disease. Malnutrition, dehydration, and the unsanitary conditions claimed many lives during the voyage.

The Middle Passage typically lasted from several weeks to a few months, depending on the weather and destination. Resistance from enslaved people was not uncommon, though their efforts were often violently suppressed. Some enslaved Africans leapt into the ocean to escape their fate, preferring death over enslavement.

Historians estimate that between 12 to 15 million Africans were trafficked across the Atlantic over the span of 400 years, with 10-15% of those dying during the Middle Passage itself. The survivors were sold into forced labor upon arrival in the Americas, where they would face further brutal conditions on plantations or in other forms of servitude.

The Middle Passage represents one of the darkest chapters in human history, marked by extreme suffering, violence, and the forced migration that had profound and lasting effects on African and American societies.

Of the estimated 12 to 15 million Africans trafficked through the transatlantic slave trade, around **388,000 to 400,000** disembarked in what became the English colonies of North America, including the United States. This figure represents a relatively small proportion—about 3 to 4 percent—of the total number of Africans who were forcibly transported during the entire trade.

The vast majority of enslaved Africans were sent to other regions of the Americas, such as the Caribbean and South America, particularly Brazil, which alone received about 4 to 5 million enslaved people, accounting for nearly 40% of the total transatlantic slave trade.

While the English colonies received a smaller number of enslaved Africans compared to other regions, slavery became a foundational institution in the development of colonial and later U.S. society, with the enslaved population growing rapidly over time due to natural reproduction.
