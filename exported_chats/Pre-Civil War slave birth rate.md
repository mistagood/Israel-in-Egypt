##  3. Why Such High Rates Under Dire Conditions—What Conditions Drive This?

Biologically and demographically, peak fertility emerges from a combination of factors:

### Conditions That Support High Fertility
1. **Balanced Sex Ratio and Early Marriage**  
   - Both critical for high rates of conception and successful births—prevalent in enslaved populations and frontier societies.

2. **High Infant and Child Survival**  
   - While mortality was high overall, some groups had survival rates that were sufficient for large numbers of births to survive into adulthood, thus encouraging reproduction.

3. **Economic or Sociocultural Pressures**  
   - In many agrarian societies, including the antebellum South and enslaved communities, children were valued as labor (economic utility) and continuation of lineage (social value).

4. **Absence of Family Planning Options**  
   - Limited or no access to contraception meant that fertility was largely uncontrolled—particularly among enslaved people, for whom sexual autonomy was denied.

### Why It Seems Paradoxical
- On the surface, it seems paradoxical: **severe oppression, harsh living conditions, malnutrition**, yet very high birth rates.
- The reality: biological capacity can be activated even under adversity if:
  - Marriages (or forced unions) are regular,
  - Cohorts are healthy enough to reach childbearing age,
  - No contraceptive is applied,
  - Cultural or forced expectations push for large families.

### Contrast with Modern Societies
- Today, developed societies experience **stage 3 of the demographic transition**: **low mortality, low fertility**—often due to women’s education, urbanization, economic factors, and access to contraception citeturn0search16.
- Thus, high fertility is not about optimal living conditions—rather, **about the absence of fertility control paired with demographic and social structures that allow repeated, early, and frequent childbirth**.
