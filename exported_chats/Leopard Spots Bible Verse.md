The verse you are referring to is Jeremiah 13:23. Here's the text from the King James Version (KJV):

"Can the Ethiopian change his skin, or the leopard his spots? Then may ye also do good, that are accustomed to do evil."

This verse is part of a larger passage in which the prophet Jeremiah is addressing the people of Judah, emphasizing the difficulty of changing one's inherent nature or deeply ingrained habits, much like an Ethiopian cannot change their skin color or a leopard its spots.

In Jeremiah 13, the specific passage about the Ethiopian changing his skin and the leopard his spots does not directly mention Ephraim. However, Jeremiah does address Ephraim and the northern kingdom of Israel in other parts of his writings.

For instance, in Jeremiah 31:18-20 (KJV), Jeremiah speaks about Ephraim:

"I have surely heard Ephraim bemoaning himself thus; Thou hast chastised me, and I was chastised, as a bullock unaccustomed to the yoke: turn thou me, and I shall be turned; for thou art the Lord my God. Surely after that I was turned, I repented; and after that I was instructed, I smote upon my thigh: I was ashamed, yea, even confounded, because I did bear the reproach of my youth. Is Ephraim my dear son? is he a pleasant child? for since I spake against him, I do earnestly remember him still: therefore my bowels are troubled for him; I will surely have mercy upon him, saith the Lord."

In this passage, God expresses his enduring love and mercy for Ephraim despite their transgressions. The message of repentance and redemption is central, showing a compassionate side of divine judgment and the hope for restoration.
