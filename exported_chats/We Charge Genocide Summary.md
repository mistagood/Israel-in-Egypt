*We Charge Genocide: The Crime of Government Against the Negro People* is a petition submitted to the United Nations in 1951 by the Civil Rights Congress (CRC), led by William L. Patterson. The document argues that the United States government systematically oppressed and committed acts of genocide against Black Americans, in violation of the UN Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide (adopted in 1948). 

### **Key Themes and Arguments:**

1. **Genocide Under International Law:** The petition applies the UN's definition of genocide—not only as mass killings but also as policies that inflict severe harm on a racial or ethnic group. It cites lynching, police brutality, economic oppression, mass incarceration, and systemic denial of basic human rights as mechanisms of genocide.

2. **Lynchings and Racial Terror:** The document presents cases of extrajudicial killings and mob violence, highlighting the role of local and federal authorities in allowing, condoning, or participating in these acts.

3. **Police Brutality:** The petition details numerous instances of Black people being murdered or brutalized by police, portraying law enforcement as an instrument of racial violence.

4. **Economic and Social Oppression:** It argues that racial discrimination in employment, housing, and education are deliberate policies designed to keep Black people in a subjugated state, which amounts to slow, systemic genocide.

5. **Forced Sterilization and Healthcare Disparities:** The document presents evidence of racist medical policies, including forced sterilization programs targeting Black women.

6. **Legal and Political Disenfranchisement:** The petition describes how Jim Crow laws, voter suppression, and the legal system itself contribute to an ongoing campaign of oppression.

7. **Historical and International Context:** The petition places U.S. racial violence in the context of global struggles against colonialism and fascism, comparing the treatment of Black Americans to the atrocities committed by the Nazis.

### **Impact and Legacy:**

- The document was signed by prominent Black intellectuals and activists, including W.E.B. Du Bois and Paul Robeson.
- It was submitted to the UN but largely ignored by the U.S. government and Western powers.
- The petition helped shape later civil rights activism and influenced international perspectives on American racism.
- It remains a foundational text in discussions about systemic racism, state violence, and the legal definition of genocide.

While the petition did not lead to immediate policy changes, its framing of racial oppression as genocide remains influential in contemporary movements against racial injustice, including those addressing mass incarceration and police violence today.

The *We Charge Genocide* petition was largely ignored by the U.S. government and the United Nations for several key reasons, rooted in Cold War politics, domestic racial dynamics, and international diplomacy.

### **1. Cold War Politics and Anti-Communism**
- The petition was submitted in 1951, at the height of McCarthyism, when the U.S. government aggressively targeted leftist and communist groups.
- The Civil Rights Congress (CRC), which organized the petition, was associated with the Communist Party USA. This connection allowed the U.S. government to dismiss the petition as Soviet propaganda rather than a legitimate human rights claim.
- The U.S. feared that acknowledging systemic racial violence as genocide could be used by the Soviet Union to undermine American credibility in the global ideological battle between democracy and communism.

### **2. U.S. Influence Over the United Nations**
- The U.S. held significant sway over the UN and was one of its founding members. It actively worked to suppress discussions that could embarrass or delegitimize its global image.
- The UN, still heavily influenced by Western powers, avoided direct challenges to its most powerful members, particularly on issues that could set legal precedents for international intervention.

### **3. Avoidance of International Accountability**
- Recognizing systemic racism as genocide could have opened the U.S. to international scrutiny and potential legal consequences under the 1948 UN Genocide Convention.
- Acknowledging the petition might have forced the U.S. to take concrete action against racial violence and systemic oppression, something the government was unwilling to do at the time.

### **4. The UN’s Limited Power on Internal Affairs**
- The UN, in its early years, was hesitant to interfere in the domestic policies of its most powerful member states.
- Western nations, many of which had their own colonial and racial issues, did not want to set a precedent for international intervention in domestic racial policies.

### **5. Racial Politics in the U.S.**
- The U.S. government dismissed the petition as an exaggeration and sought to maintain the narrative that it was making gradual progress on civil rights.
- The mainstream civil rights movement at the time, led by figures like the NAACP, distanced itself from radical claims of genocide to maintain access to political power and avoid government repression.

### **6. Suppression of Black Radical Voices**
- Figures associated with the petition, including Paul Robeson and W.E.B. Du Bois, faced government surveillance, persecution, and blacklisting.
- Robeson’s passport was revoked, preventing him from traveling to the UN or advocating internationally for the cause.
- The U.S. sought to suppress any movement that linked racial justice to anti-colonial struggles or critiqued capitalism.

### **Conclusion**
The *We Charge Genocide* petition was ignored not because of a lack of evidence, but because acknowledging it would have forced the U.S. to confront its own systemic racial oppression on an international stage. The Cold War, American political influence, and domestic racial politics ensured that the petition was marginalized, even though its arguments remain relevant in discussions of racial violence and human rights today.

Yes, there are established protocols and rules that govern how the **United Nations (UN)** and its associated bodies, such as the **International Court of Justice (ICJ)** or the **Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights (OHCHR)**, handle petitions and complaints. The reasons why the *We Charge Genocide* petition was not formally acknowledged by the UN likely stem from procedural, legal, and political considerations.

### **1. UN Rules and Procedures for Petitions in the 1950s**
At the time of the petition (1951), the UN had limited mechanisms for handling human rights complaints from non-state actors. Some key points:

- **State-Centric Model**: Under the UN Charter, only **member states** or UN-recognized international organizations could bring legal complaints directly to the UN. A petition submitted by a **non-governmental entity**, like the Civil Rights Congress, would not automatically trigger formal UN action unless a **member state sponsored it**.
- **The UN Genocide Convention (1948)**: The *Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide* required that **only member states or the UN itself** could formally accuse another state of genocide. Since the **United States had ratified the convention in 1948 but not its enforcement provisions**, this meant there was no clear **legal mechanism** at the time for the UN to prosecute the case.

### **2. The Complaint System at the Time**
- In 1947, the **ECOSOC Resolution 75(V)** laid the groundwork for what later became the **1503 Procedure**, but in the early 1950s, there was **no formal UN mechanism to handle individual human rights petitions**.
- The **Commission on Human Rights** (now part of the OHCHR) could receive complaints, but it lacked enforcement power and was politically constrained.
- Complaints about genocide would need to be **brought by a state or the UN General Assembly, Security Council, or International Court of Justice**.

### **3. Justification for Non-Acknowledgment**
Given these procedural rules, the UN likely did not act on *We Charge Genocide* because:
- **It was not submitted by a member state** (only states could formally charge another state with genocide).
- **The UN had no binding enforcement mechanism** at the time to prosecute genocide.
- **The U.S. was a dominant force within the UN** and could have pressured UN bodies to avoid discussion.
- **Political concerns**—The UN was still navigating Cold War tensions and did not want to openly challenge a major founding member like the U.S.

### **4. Later Developments**
By the **1970s**, the **1503 Procedure** and later the **1235 Procedure** allowed individuals and NGOs to submit complaints about human rights violations, though genocide cases still had to be handled through **state referrals or Security Council resolutions**.

### **Conclusion**
While the UN never officially stated why it ignored the petition, the most **likely explanation** is that:
1. It did not meet **formal legal requirements** for acknowledgment.
2. The **U.S. government influenced the process** behind the scenes.
3. The UN's **mechanisms for handling such complaints were still developing** in the 1950s.

Had the petition been submitted decades later, under different legal frameworks, it might have received more formal acknowledgment.
