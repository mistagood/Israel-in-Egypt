Stiff-necked? How so?

---

Yes—“stiff-necked” is one of the Bible’s favorite ways of describing something that **cannot be bent, guided, or assimilated** into the path God lays out.  

The Hebrew idiom comes from the image of an ox or donkey under a yoke. A teachable, obedient animal bends its neck so the yoke fits, allowing it to be steered. A stiff-necked one refuses to bow; the neck stays rigid, resisting direction. That stubborn refusal becomes a metaphor for a people who resist correction, won’t yield to instruction, and refuse to conform to God’s law.  

You see it in Exodus 32:9 and 33:3, where God calls Israel “a stiff-necked people” right after the golden calf episode. Moses picks up the phrase repeatedly (Deuteronomy 9:6, 13). It’s not simply about attitude, but about **a refusal to be joined or yoked**—they cannot be assimilated into the covenantal order because they resist submission.  

So, “stiff-necked” isn’t just a synonym for stubborn. It’s semiotic shorthand for **unyielding to assimilation or discipline**, refusing to bow. That’s how the Bible turns the physical into the symbolic.

You’re exactly right to push back. “Stiff-necked” and “unassimilable” are not the same category, and it would be slippery reasoning to collapse them. Let’s separate them cleanly.

Assimilation, as you said, means being absorbed into a larger body—taking on its customs, gods, language, and identity. The Torah is very explicit that Israel must *not* assimilate:  
- Leviticus 20:23 — “You shall not walk in the customs of the nations that I am driving out before you.”  
- Deuteronomy 7:3–4 — no intermarrying with the Canaanites, “for they would turn away your sons from following Me.”  

So, the biblical command is: *resist assimilation.* That’s a positive preservation of identity, not rebellion. Israel’s set-apartness (*qadosh*, holy) meant being **unassimilable by design**.

“Stiff-necked” (קְשֵׁה־עֹרֶף *qesheh-ʿoref*), by contrast, is not about cultural absorption but about refusing divine instruction. It’s rebellion against God’s *own* yoke, not against the nations. So yes—different spheres entirely.  

If we’re after a biblical word closer to “unassimilable,” it’s not “stiff-necked” but words like:  
- **Holy (qadosh)** — set apart, distinct, not blended.  
- **Peculiar/treasured possession (segullah)** — Deut. 7:6, meaning a people uniquely God’s.  
- **Unmixed (tamim)** — often translated “blameless” or “whole,” but literally unmixed, complete.  

So the logic is: *Israel’s calling was to be unassimilable into foreign nations but pliable toward God.* Stiff-necked is the inversion—pliable toward foreign gods but unyielding toward God.  

That’s where the incompatibility lies.

What is this slippery reasoning that you mentioned? Is that a real thing? Explain.

---

Yes—it’s a real thing, though “slippery reasoning” is me giving a shorthand. The more precise names are **equivocation** and sometimes **category error**, both of which live in the zoo of logical fallacies.  

Here’s what happened in the stiff-necked vs. unassimilable conflation:  

1. **Equivocation** — taking a word or concept that has one meaning (stiff-necked = obstinate against God’s guidance) and sliding it into another arena where the word seems superficially related (unassimilable = can’t be absorbed into another culture). The overlap is rhetorical, not real. It trades on similarity of “resisting” without acknowledging the different objects being resisted.  

2. **Category error** — treating two concepts that belong to different categories (moral rebellion vs. cultural distinctiveness) as though they are interchangeable. Israel’s holiness code *requires* being unassimilable, but their covenant faithfulness *forbids* being stiff-necked. Mixing those categories muddles the logic.  

So, “slippery reasoning” is the casual way to describe that move—when an argument slides from one sense of resistance to another, hoping the listener won’t notice the shift. It’s not always malicious; sometimes it’s just a lazy shortcut. But it creates a false equivalence.  

In debate or exegesis, catching these slides is crucial. Otherwise, we end up justifying disobedience (stiff-necked) by dressing it in the clothes of obedience (unassimilable), or vice versa.

Let’s build this carefully in a logical framework, using your premise of “logic should have guided Israel in the wilderness.”  

**1. Premises about logic and trust:**  
- P1: Logic is the use of consistent reasoning based on observed evidence.  
- P2: Israel had observed repeated phenomena beyond human control (plagues, Red Sea parting, manna, water from rock).  
- P3: These phenomena consistently aligned with what Moses reported God would do.  
- C1: It would be logically consistent to trust the power/source producing these events.  

**2. Contradictory behavior of Israel:**  
- P4: Despite the evidence, Israel frequently doubted, rebelled, or longed for Egypt.  
- P5: These actions contradict the logical conclusion of C1.  
- C2: Israel’s behavior was not guided by logic but by other conditions.  

**3. Conditions affecting their reasoning capacity:**  
- P6: Israel had lived under slavery and oppression for generations (Exodus 1:11–14).  
- P7: Oppression conditions often produce trauma, fear, and habituation to dependency.  
- P8: Sudden removal from familiar structures, even oppressive ones, generates anxiety and disorientation (seen in their repeated longing for Egypt: Exodus 16:3, Numbers 11:5).  
- C3: Israel’s psychological and social state impaired their ability to act consistently with logical evidence.  

**4. Synthesis:**  
- P9: To interpret the story logically, one must weigh both the rational evidence (miracles in their favor) and the irrational conditions (trauma, fear, loss of stability).  
- C4: Israel’s rebellion is not a contradiction of logic itself but a demonstration of how external conditions (slavery, displacement, trauma) distort human reasoning, leading them to act illogically in the face of overwhelming logical evidence.  

**Final conclusion:**  
Logic would have guided Israel to trust God’s deliverance. Their failure to do so was not due to lack of evidence, but due to the weight of psychological and social conditions that distorted their reasoning. Thus, the biblical narrative shows the tension between logical trust in God’s acts and the illogical pull of trauma and fear.  

This way we don’t just say “they were stiff-necked,” but instead identify the competing logical and illogical pressures at work.
