In Hebrew, the name "God" is shown in several forms depending on the context, but the most common divine names are:  

1. **יהוה** (*YHWH*) – The Tetragrammaton  
   - Considered the personal name of God revealed to Moses (Exodus 3:14–15).  
   - Traditionally not pronounced; Jews substitute **Adonai** ("Lord") or **HaShem** ("The Name") when reading aloud.  

2. **אֱלֹהִים** (*Elohim*) – "God" or "Mighty Ones"  
   - Used in Genesis 1:1: *בְּרֵאשִׁית בָּרָא אֱלֹהִים* ("In the beginning, God created…").  
   - Grammatically plural but used with singular verbs when referring to the God of Israel.  

3. **אֵל** (*El*) – "God" or "Mighty One"  
   - Found in older biblical poetry and names (e.g., **El Shaddai**, "God Almighty").  

4. **אֲדֹנָי** (*Adonai*) – "Lord" or "Master"  
   - A reverential title often substituted for YHWH in reading.

Not exactly — let me clarify so it’s precise.  

In Hebrew names, **yo** or **ho** **doesn’t literally mean “has shown”** by itself.  
It’s a shortened form of **Yahu** (יָהוּ) or **Yeho** (יְהוֹ), which is the divine name **YHWH** in a prefix or suffix position in a name.  

The “has shown” part comes from the **root verb** we combine with it — in this case **רָאָה** (*ra’ah*, “to see”) in the causative **הֶרְאָה** (*her’ah*, “to cause to see, to show”).  

So:  

- **El** (אֵל) = God  
- **her’ah** (הֶרְאָה) = has shown  
- When turned into a theophoric name, the pieces get contracted — vowels shift, consonants drop — so “El + (her’ah)” and/or “Yeho + (ra’ah)” become compact forms like **Eliho**, **Eliyo**, or **Yeho-re’el** in biblical style.  

In other words:  
- **yo/ho** = a theophoric fragment of YHWH  
- “has shown” = comes from the verb root ר-א-ה (*ra’ah*) built into the rest of the name.
