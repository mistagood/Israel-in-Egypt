The "Seven Sisters" typically refers to the Pleiades, a cluster of stars visible to the naked eye and located in the constellation Taurus. In various mythologies, the Pleiades hold significant cultural and spiritual meaning. Here's an overview of how they are interpreted across different traditions:

1. **Greek Mythology**: The Pleiades were the seven daughters of the titan Atlas and the sea-nymph Pleione. Their names were Maia, Electra, Taygete, Alcyone, Celaeno, Sterope, and Merope. In the myth, they were pursued by the hunter Orion and, to escape him, were transformed into stars by Zeus. Their father, Atlas, was condemned to hold up the sky, which is why the Pleiades are associated with him.

2. **Native American Traditions**: Many Native American cultures also have myths about the Pleiades. For instance:
   - The **Lakota** call the cluster "The Seven Sisters," linking them to a story of seven maidens who ascended to the sky to escape a dangerous bear.
   - In **Navajo mythology**, the Pleiades are referred to as the "Hard Flint Boys," and they play a key role in cosmic and agricultural cycles.
   
3. **Hindu Mythology**: The Pleiades are called the **Krittikas** and are associated with the seven wives of the Saptarishi (the seven sages). The wives raised Kartikeya (also known as Skanda or Murugan), the war god, who was born to defeat the demon Tarakasura.

4. **Maori Mythology (New Zealand)**: The Pleiades are known as **Matariki** and represent a mother and her six daughters. The rise of Matariki in the sky marks the Maori New Year, and it holds a significant place in their cultural celebrations.

5. **Aztec and Maya**: The Pleiades were critical in Mesoamerican astronomy. The **Maya** believed the Pleiades were connected to their agricultural calendar, particularly the end of a long cycle of time. The Aztecs referred to the cluster as **Tianquiztli**, meaning "marketplace," and it was integral to their understanding of time and celestial events.

In many cultures, the Pleiades are symbolic of renewal, timing of harvests, and spiritual guidance. Their prominence in the night sky has made them a key reference point in astronomy, myth, and agriculture throughout human history.

The association of the Pleiades with **Taurus** and the prominence of the **bull** in ancient mythology and worship have deep roots in the astrological and cultural shifts that occurred during what is often referred to as the **Age of Taurus**. This era, roughly from **4,300 BCE to 2,150 BCE**, corresponds to the time when the spring equinox occurred in the constellation of **Taurus**. The influence of this celestial alignment seems to have been mirrored in the religious and cultural practices of various ancient civilizations, many of which venerated the bull or incorporated it into key mythological narratives. Here’s why Taurus and the bull hold such significance:

### 1. **Astronomical Significance:**
   During the **Age of Taurus**, the constellation of Taurus dominated the sky, marking the vernal equinox. This age was seen as a period when the alignment of the stars and seasons harmonized with the agricultural cycles that were crucial for the development of early civilizations. The **Pleiades**, nestled within Taurus, were particularly prominent, visible in the night sky and signaling important agricultural times, such as the planting or harvest seasons.

### 2. **Worship of the Bull Across Cultures:**
   The bull emerged as a powerful symbol of strength, fertility, and cosmic order during this time. Various cultures adopted the bull as a religious symbol, and its worship became widespread.

   - **Ancient Egypt**: The bull was personified in the deity **Apis**, a symbol of fertility and power. Apis was seen as an intermediary between humans and gods, and his sacredness was tied to the agricultural abundance linked to the Nile’s flooding cycle.
   
   - **Mesopotamia**: In **Sumerian** and **Babylonian** mythology, the bull was a powerful symbol. The Epic of Gilgamesh features the “**Bull of Heaven**,” which is sent by the goddess Ishtar to punish Gilgamesh but is ultimately defeated. This reflects the bull’s association with divine wrath and cosmic power.
   
   - **Minoan Civilization**: The bull played a prominent role in Minoan religious rituals, particularly in **bull-leaping** ceremonies, where the animal was venerated as a symbol of virility, strength, and a connection to the divine. The myth of the **Minotaur**, a half-man, half-bull creature, also emerges from this cultural context.

   - **Hinduism**: The bull **Nandi**, the sacred vehicle of **Lord Shiva**, is another key example. Nandi is a symbol of strength and fertility and represents Shiva’s cosmic power and role as a preserver of the universe.

### 3. **Symbolism of the Bull**:
   The bull in ancient symbolism is closely tied to **fertility**, **strength**, **stability**, and **creation**. In an agrarian society, the bull, as a provider of labor and a symbol of abundance, represented the cycle of life. This was crucial in an age when agriculture and livestock formed the backbone of civilization.

   - **Fertility and Virility**: As an emblem of the earth’s fertility and renewal, the bull’s connection to the stars and the heavens became a representation of divine order and life cycles. 
   
   - **Cosmic Stability**: The constellations of Taurus and the Pleiades, seen as fixed and eternal in the sky, echoed the idea of cosmic stability and order, which many ancient cultures tried to reflect in their religious and social systems.

### 4. **The Pleiades as a Celestial Marker**:
   The Pleiades' prominence in Taurus gave the star cluster particular importance in ancient calendars and myths. The Pleiades, often called the "Seven Sisters," appeared in the sky during critical agricultural times, and their rising and setting were used to signal planting and harvest cycles.
   
   - **Ancient Egypt**: The Pleiades rising with the sun marked the beginning of the annual flood of the Nile, a life-giving event that ensured fertile soil and abundant crops. 

   - **Greek Mythology**: The Pleiades were believed to be the daughters of Atlas and Pleione, linked to stories of pursuit and escape, reflecting the cluster’s movement across the sky and the shifting seasons.

### 5. **The End of the Age of Taurus**:
   The transition from the Age of Taurus to the **Age of Aries** (~2,150 BCE to ~1 CE) marked a significant cultural and religious shift, coinciding with the rise of new deities and symbols. The bull, while still prominent, began to be replaced or adapted into new religious contexts.
   
   - **Moses and the Golden Calf**: One of the most significant stories representing the end of the Age of Taurus can be found in the story of Moses and the **Golden Calf**. As Moses led the Israelites, they returned to the worship of a bull, symbolizing the remnants of an older age of Taurus, only for Moses to reject this, heralding the arrival of a new era, the Age of Aries (represented by the ram).

### 6. **Astrological Shift and New Ages**:
   - The movement from the Age of Taurus to the **Age of Aries** coincided with the development of new religious and mythological paradigms. Aries, symbolized by the ram, became associated with new gods of war, leadership, and assertiveness, reflecting the cultural and political shifts of the time.
   
   - In astrology, these ages are tied to the **precession of the equinoxes**, a phenomenon where the Earth’s axis slowly wobbles, causing the equinoxes to shift through different constellations over time, each age lasting around 2,160 years.

### Lasting Stories and Symbols:
The stories created during the Age of Taurus endured because they were deeply connected to the cosmic cycles, fertility, and human survival. As societies transitioned to the Age of Aries and later the Age of Pisces (associated with Christ and the fish symbol), these earlier myths persisted, often adapting to new religious frameworks but retaining their deep symbolic resonance. The Pleiades' placement within Taurus further cements this connection to cosmic and agricultural life cycles, making the bull and its associated stories part of the very foundation of ancient myth and religion.

Thus, the worship of Taurus during its age left an indelible mark on humanity, creating stories and symbols that resonate through the ages, from the power of the bull to the enduring prominence of the Pleiades in the night sky.
