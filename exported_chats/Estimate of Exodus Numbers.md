This is a classic “deep mystery behind the text” question. There’s no consensus “real” number — the answer depends heavily on how one translates, interprets, and reconciles the biblical data with archaeology, demographics, and textual criticism. But I can walk you through the major estimates (and their strengths & flaws), then state my “best guess” as a working hypothesis for narrative purposes.

## What the Biblical Text Claims (Literally)

- **Exodus 12:37** says: “the children of Israel journeyed from Rameses to Succoth, about six hundred thousand men on foot, besides women and children.” (i.e. ~600,000 adult men)  
- **Numbers 1:46** gives a more detailed breakdown of the census in the wilderness: 603,550 men (ages 20 and up, presumably) from the tribes of Israel.  
- From those counts, many interpreters assume a multiplier (for women, children, the elderly) of about 3–5× (or more) to get the full population.

So, the *traditional* (face-value) number, using the biblical data, is often:

> **~2 to 3 million people** (men + women + children + “mixed multitude”) citeturn0search3turn0search1turn0search10turn0search15turn0search4

Some traditions push this even higher (e.g. 4 million or more) depending on their assumed ratio. citeturn0search11

## Why That Traditional Number Is Problematic (and Reasons Some Scholars Reduce It)

When you insist on “what really could have happened” rather than “what the text says if taken at face value,” major problems emerge:

1. **Demographic plausibility & carrying capacity**  
   Could Goshen, Egypt or the Sinai support 2–3 million people (plus livestock) moving through a desert region for 40 years? Many think not, given archaeological and ecological constraints. citeturn0search10turn0search15turn0search18turn0search4

2. **Archaeological silence**  
   If that many people left Egypt (and spent decades wandering), one might expect more traces (camps, material culture remnants, large-scale infrastructure, etc.). The archaeological record is largely silent on such a massive migration. citeturn0search18turn0search10turn0search4turn0search15

3. **Meaning of “eleph / ’elef”**  
   The Hebrew word often translated “thousand” (’elef) has a broader semantic range. It can sometimes mean “clan,” “unit,” or a military subdivision rather than strictly 1,000. Some scholars argue that the “600,000 men” might mean “600 military units / clans / clans of men” rather than 600,000 individuals. citeturn0search0turn0search10turn0search4turn0search15turn0search18  
   Under that view, the total population might shrink by an order of magnitude.

4. **Textual / literary exaggeration**  
   Ancient historiography often magnified numbers for rhetorical / theological effect. It’s possible the Exodus narrative uses large numbers as symbolic or theological devices. citeturn0search4turn0search15turn0search18

5. **Population growth constraints**  
   If only ~70 people (Jacob + 12 sons + households) went into Egypt (Genesis 46) and one projects their exponential growth over centuries, some argue it's hard to reach 2–3 million by the time of the Exodus without miraculous fertility, extended time, or exaggeration. citeturn0search4turn0search10turn0search18

Because of these issues, some more conservative or critical reconstructions propose **tens of thousands** or **a few hundred thousand** total (or even lower) as more realistic in non-miraculous terms. citeturn0search9turn0search18turn0search10turn0search15

## My “Best Guess” (for a Documentary Narrative)

Because your project is exploring hidden symbolism *and* connection to historical/prophetic layers, you’ll want a number that respects the power of the biblical claims without collapsing into pure fantasy. Here’s the working estimate I’d use (with caveats):

- Start from the text: ~600,000 “men-of-war” (or adult men) is the core anchor.
- Assume that “men” = fighting-age males (20–60, say). If you multiply that by 3 to 4 to account for women, children, elderly, helpers, etc., you reach **~1.8 to 2.4 million**.
- Then, adjust downward for “mixed multitude,” attrition, and maybe textual exaggeration. So a safe narrative window might be **~1 to 2 million total**.

Thus: **my working hypothesis** is **~1.5 million people** (give or take a few hundred thousand), counting men, women, children, and the mixed crowd.

I’d present it in the documentary as:

> “If the text’s 600,000 fighting men is our anchor, and we allow a multiplier for families and extras, a plausible estimate lands at 1 to 2 million people. A midpoint — ~1.5 million — serves as a disciplined, generous guess that balances the text’s force with demographic realism.”

You’d then note: if one instead takes a minimal view (interpreting “600,000” as “600 clans” or “600 units”), the number could collapse into **20,000–100,000** total. But that risks stripping the text of its epic scale.

Good — that’s exactly the kind of reasoning that stays faithful to the narrative logic rather than modern skepticism. Let’s walk it through cleanly, step by step, just using internal biblical reasoning and demographic math consistent with the worldview of Exodus.

---

### Step 1: Adult men (Exodus 12:37, Numbers 1:46)
Base number = **600,000 men on foot**, fighting age.

---

### Step 2: Wives
Sex ratios were roughly balanced, and the text assumes family units left together (“every man and his household”).  
→ **+600,000 women**  
Subtotal: **1,200,000 adults.**

---

### Step 3: Children per couple
Israelites were explicitly commanded to “be fruitful and multiply,” and Egyptian oppression centered on population control (Exodus 1:7–10). Even under hardship, fertility remained high.  
A conservative fertility rate in such conditions would be **2–3 surviving children per couple**, and some would have more.

Let’s take an average of **2.5 children per couple**.  
- 600,000 families × 2.5 = **1.5 million children.**

Subtotal: **1.2 million adults + 1.5 million children = 2.7 million Israelites.**

---

### Step 4: Mixed multitude (Exodus 12:38)
The text calls them *‘ʿerev rav’* — “a mixed multitude,” meaning non-Israelites who joined the flight.  
They could have included sympathetic Egyptians, slaves of other origins, and intermarried foreigners. The phrase *rav* (“great”) suggests a substantial number, not a handful.  

A modest estimate would be **10–25%** of the Israelite total.

- 10% of 2.7M = 270,000  
- 25% of 2.7M = 675,000  

Adding that gives a final range of roughly **3.0–3.4 million total persons** departing Egypt.

---

### Step 5: Reasoned midpoint
Using round numbers faithful to the text’s intent:

| Group | Estimated Count |
|-------|----------------|
| 600,000 men | 600,000 |
| 600,000 women | 600,000 |
| 2.5 children per family | 1,500,000 |
| Mixed multitude (~15%) | 400,000 |
| **Total** | **~3.1 million** |

---

### Step 6: Narrative summary
From the biblical perspective, around **three million souls** came out of Egypt — a nation within a nation. That number conveys the magnitude intended by the story: not a band of wanderers but a full-fledged people, organized by tribes, elders, and armies, capable of forming a new polity in the wilderness. The scale itself explains why Pharaoh’s pursuit was militarized and why divine intervention was portrayed as necessary to sustain them.

So, reasoning strictly from the internal logic of the text and normal demographic balance, the total Exodus population would stand at **approximately three million people** — give or take a few hundred thousand.
