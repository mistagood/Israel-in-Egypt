**Hosea's Wife: Gomer**

Hosea was a prophet in the Hebrew Bible whose story is told in the Book of Hosea. God commanded Hosea to marry Gomer, a woman described as "a wife of harlotry" (Hosea 1:2, various translations). This marriage was symbolic of Israel's unfaithfulness to God, as Gomer's infidelity mirrored the nation's idolatry and moral decline.

**Children of Hosea and Gomer**

Hosea and Gomer had three children, each given a name with prophetic significance:

1. **Jezreel** (son): The name "Jezreel" means "God sows." This name references the Valley of Jezreel, a place of past bloodshed. It symbolizes impending judgment against the house of Jehu and the kingdom of Israel for their violent actions.

2. **Lo-Ruhamah** (daughter): Meaning "Not Pitied" or "No Mercy," this name signifies that God would no longer show mercy to the house of Israel due to their continuous unfaithfulness.

3. **Lo-Ammi** (son): Translated as "Not My People," this name represents a severed relationship between God and Israel, indicating that they are no longer His people because of their abandonment of His commandments.

**Symbolism and Themes**

The marriage and the children's names serve as metaphors for the relationship between God and Israel. Hosea's personal life becomes a living prophecy, illustrating themes of infidelity, judgment, and ultimately, redemption. Despite the harsh judgments, the Book of Hosea also contains messages of hope, emphasizing God's enduring love and the possibility of restoration if the people return to righteousness.

### **1. The Northern Kingdom (Israel)**
These prophets primarily addressed the **ten northern tribes** before and during their downfall to Assyria in 722 BCE.

  - **Time Period**: Reign of Jeroboam II (Northern Kingdom at its peak).  
  - **Themes**: Social justice, condemnation of oppression of the poor, divine judgment.  
  - **Key Message**: God desires justice ("Let justice roll down like waters").

  - **Time Period**: Decline of Israel, leading up to its fall to Assyria.

  - **Key Message**: Israel’s idolatry is like adultery, but God’s love remains steadfast.

- **Jonah (c. 8th century BCE)**  
  - **Time Period**: Assyrian dominance.  
  - **Themes**: Universal mercy, repentance, God’s sovereignty.  
  - **Key Message**: Even Gentiles (like Nineveh) are within God’s reach of salvation.

### **2. The Southern Kingdom (Judah)**
These prophets addressed **Judah**, especially as it faced Babylonian threats and eventual exile (586 BCE).

- **Isaiah (c. 740–700 BCE)**  
  - **Time Period**: Reigns of Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah.  
  - **Themes**: Holiness of God, messianic hope, warnings about alliance with foreign powers.  
  - **Key Message**: Trust in God, not in political alliances or military strength.

- **Micah (c. 735–700 BCE)**  
  - **Time Period**: Overlaps with Isaiah; during Assyrian threats.  
  - **Themes**: Justice, mercy, humility, messianic prophecy.  
  - **Key Message**: God requires justice, mercy, and humility (Micah 6:8).

- **Jeremiah (c. 627–586 BCE)**  
  - **Time Period**: Final kings of Judah, fall of Jerusalem to Babylon.  
  - **Themes**: New covenant, impending judgment, lamentation over Judah’s sin.  
  - **Key Message**: Repentance could avert disaster, but Judah’s exile is inevitable.  

- **Zephaniah (c. 640–609 BCE)**  
  - **Time Period**: Reign of Josiah (before Babylon’s rise).  
  - **Themes**: The Day of the Lord, judgment, and restoration.  
  - **Key Message**: Judgment is near, but God’s remnant will be saved.

- **Habakkuk (c. 609–605 BCE)**  
  - **Time Period**: Babylon’s rise to power.  
  - **Themes**: Questioning God’s justice, faith amidst judgment.  
  - **Key Message**: The righteous live by faith.

  - **Themes**: Messianic hope, visions of restoration, God’s sovereignty.

- **Malachi (c. 450 BCE)**  
  - **Themes**: Renewing the covenant, awaiting the Messiah.  
  - **Key Message**: God desires sincere worship and faithfulness.

#### **2. Prophets to the Southern Kingdom (Judah)**  
These prophets warned Judah about Babylon’s rise and eventual exile (586 BCE).

##### **Before the Exile**

  - **Historical Context**: Reigns of Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, Hezekiah; Assyria looming.  
  - **Themes**: Holiness of God, trust in Him, messianic hope.

  - **Themes**: Justice, mercy, humility, and messianic prophecy.  
  - **Message**: God requires justice and humility, not empty rituals.  
  - **Key Verse**: "What does the Lord require of you? To act justly, love mercy, and walk humbly" (Micah 6:8).

  - **Historical Context**: Nineveh, after repentance in Jonah’s time, returns to wickedness.  
  - **Themes**: God’s justice, destruction of oppressors.  
  - **Message**: Assyria’s reign of terror will end with Nineveh’s destruction.  
  - **Key Verse**: "The Lord is good, a refuge in times of trouble" (Nahum 1:7).

  - **Historical Context**: Reign of Josiah; reforms begin but idolatry persists.

  - **Key Verse**: "The Lord your God is with you... He will rejoice over you with singing" (Zephaniah 3:17).

  - **Historical Context**: Babylon is rising as a dominant power.  
  - **Themes**: Questioning God’s justice, faith amidst chaos.  
  - **Message**: God will judge Babylon, but the righteous must live by faith.

  - **Themes**: Judgment, new covenant, lamentation.  
  - **Message**: Judah’s sin ensures exile, but hope lies in God’s future covenant.  
  - **Key Verse**: "I will put my law in their minds and write it on their hearts" (Jeremiah 31:33).

  - **Message**: God is still with His people in exile; He will restore them.
