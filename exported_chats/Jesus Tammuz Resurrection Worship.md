In the book of Ezekiel, chapter 8, the prophet is shown a vision of women weeping for Tammuz at the temple of Jerusalem. Tammuz was a Sumerian deity associated with fertility, life cycles, and agriculture. His myth involves a cycle of death and resurrection, symbolizing the seasonal changes in vegetation, where he dies in the heat of summer and is resurrected in the spring. This cycle of death and rebirth made Tammuz a symbol of resurrection in ancient Mesopotamian religions.

The weeping women, in Ezekiel’s vision, are mourning Tammuz’s death as part of a pagan ritual, which the prophet condemns as idolatrous worship within the very temple of the God of Israel. This scene is portrayed as one of the great abominations happening in Jerusalem, suggesting that the Israelites had turned from Yahweh to participate in these foreign rituals of mourning for a dying and rising god, like Tammuz.

When this narrative is linked to the worship of Jesus Christ, some interpret it as a continuation of this ancient practice, where the Israelites have replaced the worship of Yahweh with the worship of a new figure—Jesus, who is also said to have died and been resurrected. The key connection being drawn is that the worship of Jesus as the resurrected Son of God is, in essence, a reflection or continuation of the worship of Tammuz.

This interpretation suggests that the resurrection doctrine, as promoted by Paul and accepted by early Christians, mirrors the older pagan tradition of mourning and celebrating the resurrection of a deity. From this perspective, the figure of Jesus in Christian doctrine, particularly His resurrection, aligns closely with the ancient myth of Tammuz. The mourning for Tammuz is seen as analogous to Christian rituals like Good Friday, where believers mourn Jesus' death, followed by the celebration of His resurrection on Easter.

Thus, for those who adopt this view, the worship of Jesus Christ as the resurrected Son of God is considered a masked continuation of the ancient rites surrounding Tammuz, with Christians unknowingly engaging in a form of idolatry condemned in the Old Testament. The belief is that this form of worship is a perpetuation of the very practice Ezekiel condemned—the substitution of the true God of Israel for a false, resurrected god, represented in the figure of Tammuz.

This theory challenges traditional Christian beliefs, suggesting that the doctrine of Jesus' resurrection is not unique but borrowed from earlier religious traditions, redirecting worship from Yahweh to a figure who embodies the same patterns as Tammuz. Therefore, the "women weeping for Tammuz" becomes symbolic of a broader spiritual error: the Israelites, and by extension Christians, mistakenly worshiping Jesus in a manner similar to how their ancestors worshiped a pagan god.
