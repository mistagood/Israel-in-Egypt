Did Paul believe that Jesus was coming back in his lifetime?

---



---

Yes, it appears that Paul believed Jesus would return during his lifetime. Several passages in Paul's letters suggest that he had an expectation of Christ's imminent return. In 1 Thessalonians 4:15-17, Paul speaks of "we who are alive, who are left until the coming of the Lord," implying that he expected to be among those living when Jesus returned. Additionally, in 1 Corinthians 7:29-31, Paul advises believers to live as though the world is passing away, reinforcing the urgency of his message.

Paul’s writings reflect this early Christian belief that Jesus' return (the Parousia) was near. However, later in his ministry, Paul seems to adjust this expectation slightly, as seen in passages like Philippians 1:21-24, where he contemplates his own death and the possibility of departing to be with Christ. While his letters maintain the hope of Christ’s return, there’s a subtle shift acknowledging the possibility of his own death before that event.

Did Moses say that if a prophet prophesies something and it doesn't happen, then God hasn't spoken that?

---

Yes, Moses did say that if a prophet prophesies something and it does not come to pass, it is a sign that God has not spoken through that prophet. This is found in Deuteronomy 18:21-22, where Moses gives guidance to the Israelites on how to discern false prophets. The passage says:

*"And if you say in your heart, ‘How may we know the word that the LORD has not spoken?’— when a prophet speaks in the name of the LORD, if the word does not come to pass or come true, that is a word that the LORD has not spoken; the prophet has spoken it presumptuously. You need not be afraid of him."* 

This passage emphasizes that a true prophet of God will speak things that come to pass. If a prophecy fails, it is clear evidence that the message did not come from God.

---

It seems then that Paul is a false prophet.

---

Given the standard set by Deuteronomy 18:21-22, which states that if a prophecy does not come to pass, the prophet is speaking presumptuously, some might argue that Paul's expectations of Jesus' imminent return could be seen as problematic. In several of Paul's letters, he seems to anticipate that Jesus would return during his lifetime or shortly thereafter (e.g., 1 Thessalonians 4:15-17, 1 Corinthians 7:29-31). Since this expectation did not materialize, it raises the question of whether Paul’s predictions align with the criteria Moses outlined for a true prophet.

However, Paul's supporters might argue that his writings reflect the evolving understanding of God’s plan and that his prophetic insights were not necessarily bound to specific timelines but were rather expressions of hope for Christ’s return at any moment. Still, using the criterion from Deuteronomy, some critical perspectives could indeed conclude that Paul's prophecies concerning the timing of Christ's return did not meet the standard of prophetic accuracy that Moses established.

This leads to a broader discussion on how to interpret Paul's role and message within early Christianity, particularly given the context of his influence on the developing doctrine. His teachings, especially concerning resurrection and the Gentiles' inclusion, continue to provoke debate on whether he should be seen as a prophet in alignment with Moses’ criteria or as someone whose message diverged from traditional Israelite expectations.
