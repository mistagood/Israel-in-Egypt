The Western Zodiac, as we know it today, has its roots in ancient Babylonian and later Greek astrology. Here's a brief history:

1. **Babylonian Origins**: The origins of the zodiac can be traced back to the Babylonians, around the 5th century BCE. They identified patterns of stars (constellations) and associated them with myths and stories. These constellations marked out the path that the Sun takes through the sky over the course of a year, which is known as the ecliptic.

2. **Greek Adaptation**: The Greeks adopted the Babylonian system and made significant contributions to it. By the 4th century BCE, the Greeks had developed the zodiac of 12 signs, which is familiar to us today. Each sign corresponds to 30 degrees of the 360-degree circle of the zodiac.

3. **Egyptian Influence**: There's evidence of some cross-influence between Babylonian astrology and ancient Egyptian star lore. The Dendera Zodiac, a bas-relief from the ceiling of the portico of a chapel dedicated to Osiris in the Hathor temple at Dendera, is one of the most studied artifacts related to the zodiac from Egypt.

4. **Roman Adoption**: The Romans adopted the Greek zodiac system, and it became integrated into their culture and daily life. The names of the zodiac signs in English (e.g., Aries, Taurus) are derived from the Latin names used by the Romans.

5. **Age in Comparison to the Torah**: The origins of the Torah are a subject of scholarly debate. Traditionally, the Torah is believed to have been written by Moses, which would place its composition around the 13th century BCE. However, modern scholarly consensus often places the compilation of the Torah's current form during the Babylonian Exile or shortly thereafter, in the 6th-5th centuries BCE. This would mean that the earliest forms of the zodiac and the final compilation of the Torah are roughly contemporaneous. However, the traditions, stories, and laws contained within the Torah are certainly older and could date back many centuries prior to its final compilation.

6. **Comparison to Sumer and Akkad**: The civilizations of Sumer and Akkad predate the Babylonian Empire and were established around the 4th millennium BCE. While these civilizations had their own star lore and constellations, the zodiac system as we know it was not yet developed.

In summary, the Western Zodiac system, as recognized today, originated with the Babylonians around the 5th century BCE and was refined by the Greeks and Romans in subsequent centuries. Its development is roughly contemporaneous with the compilation of the Torah, though the traditions within the Torah are older. The zodiac system is younger than the civilizations of Sumer and Akkad.

However, the stories in Genesis, including those of the patriarchs, are believed to have been passed down orally for generations before being committed to writing. The traditions and stories of the patriarchs could date back to the 2nd millennium BCE or even earlier.

2. **Zodiac's Age**: As mentioned previously, the Western Zodiac system as we know it, with its twelve signs, originated with the Babylonians around the 5th century BCE.

5. **Conclusion**: While there are intriguing parallels between the twelve tribes and the zodiac's twelve signs, direct evidence linking the two is scant. The narrative of Jacob and his sons likely predates the Babylonian zodiac system. However, it's essential to approach any potential connections with caution, recognizing the differences and the cultural contexts from which each system arose. The Bible and the zodiac serve different purposes: one is a religious and historical text, while the other is an astrological system. While cultural exchanges undoubtedly occurred, drawing direct lines between the two requires further evidence and scholarly consensus.
