The name **Africa** was *not* originally the name of the entire continent, nor was it named after a white man named "Africa." That is a myth or an oversimplification. The true origin is more complex and has roots in ancient language, geography, and conquest.

Here’s a breakdown of the most credible theories:

### 1. **Afri People (Berber origin)**
The Romans referred to a group of people living near modern-day Tunisia as the **Afri** or **Aourigha**, possibly of Berber origin. The name **Africa** came from the Latin *"Africa terra"*, meaning “land of the Afri.” This referred specifically to **North Africa**, not the whole continent.

### 2. **Roman Naming**
After defeating Carthage in the Punic Wars, the Romans established the province of **Africa** (roughly Tunisia today). This was a Roman name for the territory and eventually expanded in usage through Latin texts and maps.

### 3. **Arabic or Phoenician Origins**
Some scholars believe the word might come from the Phoenician word *"afar"*, meaning dust, or from the Arabic *"Ifriqiya"*, which also became a name for a region in North Africa during Islamic rule.

### 4. **Not Named After a Person**
There is *no historical evidence* that Africa was named after a white European man named “Leo Africanus” or “Scipio Africanus.” Scipio Africanus was a Roman general who fought in Africa, and the title “Africanus” means “conqueror of Africa,” not the other way around. Africa already had a name before him.

This is Paul's foundational claim. He states that his gospel did not come from the apostles, the Torah, or eyewitness instruction—but from a *revelation* he alone received. There were no witnesses. It was not delivered at Sinai, nor confirmed by signs to the public.

---

### **2. The “Damascus Road” Vision**

**Acts 9, 22, and 26** each give slightly different versions of Paul’s vision.

In **Acts 9:3–7**, the men with Paul **hear a voice but see no one**.  
In **Acts 22:9**, they **see the light but hear not the voice**.  
In **Acts 26**, Paul adds more dramatic details: a “light above the brightness of the sun” and words in Hebrew from Jesus himself.

These contradictions raise legitimate questions about the event’s verifiability.

---

### **3. Contrast with Biblical Standards of Prophetic Authority**

According to Torah, a prophet or leader must be tested:

- **Deuteronomy 13:1–3**

- **Deuteronomy 18:20–22**  
> The prophet must speak in God’s name, and what he speaks must come to pass.

Paul did **not** perform signs publicly to confirm his apostleship the way Moses, Elijah, or Jesus did. Nor did he bring people back to obedience to the Torah. In fact, many of his teachings—particularly on the Law, circumcision, and Israel’s role—*depart* from Moses' words.

## **Breakdown & Hidden References**

### **Verse 20: “Where is the flock that was given thee?”**  
- This is a **judgment speech**, accusing a leader or group entrusted with the flock—God's people—who has lost or **scattered them**.
- In context with earlier chapters (esp. Jeremiah 6:1), this likely points to **Benjamin**, the tribe that bordered Jerusalem and often acted as a **gatekeeper** or **buffer** for Judah.
- **Paul**, a Benjamite (Phil. 3:5), later becomes the symbolic figure who scatters through doctrine (Romans 10:4), not physical exile, but theological disinheritance.

---

### **Verse 21: “What wilt thou say when he shall punish thee?...”**  
- This refers to judgment upon the **Benjamite leadership**, now elevated above the people through **false instruction**.
- *“Thou hast taught them to be captains, and as chief over thee”* implies they trained others to rule, perhaps referencing **Gentile converts** or **Roman sympathizers**, now holding **spiritual authority** over the covenant people—a reversal of order.
- This evokes **Deuteronomy 28:43-44**, where the stranger rises above Israel as a sign of curse.
- *“Shall not sorrows take thee, as a woman in travail?”* — This directly echoes **Rachel’s travail** in Genesis 35:18, when she dies giving birth to **Benjamin**, calling him **Ben-Oni**, "son of my sorrow."

---

### **Genesis 35:18:**  
> “And it came to pass, as her soul was in departing... she called his name **Benoni**: but his father called him **Benjamin**.”

- Jeremiah’s mention of **travail** and **sorrows** is a direct allusion to **Benjamin’s origin**, rooted in death, pain, and mourning.
- The word **sorrows** here is not generic—it’s pregnant with **ancestral and prophetic memory**.
- And **Jacob’s own lamentation** when he believed Joseph was dead (“I will go down to the grave unto my son mourning” - Gen. 37:35) links sorrow not just to Benjamin’s birth, but also to **Joseph’s separation**—the prophetic pattern of division.

---

### **Verse 24: “I will scatter them as stubble…”**
- The language of **scattering** is both agricultural and prophetic.
- This aligns perfectly with **Jacob’s prophecy in Genesis 49:27**:
  
> “Benjamin shall ravin as a wolf: in the morning he shall devour the prey, and at night he shall divide the spoil.”

- *“Divide the spoil”* is an ancient metaphor for **scattering**, **splitting**, or **fragmenting** unity. Paul, as a theological innovator, introduces **division** in the law (Romans 6:14), the people (Romans 9–11), and the concept of Israel (Galatians 3:28).

- The **wind of the wilderness** symbolizes divine judgment and exile—a reversal of the Exodus. Rather than leading people *out* of wilderness, this Benjamite figure drives them *back in*.

---

### **Verse 25: “This is thy lot… because thou hast forgotten me, and trusted in falsehood.”**
- *“Thy lot”* echoes the prophetic allocation of fate or destiny, often tied to divine judgment.
- It also recalls **the portion or inheritance** of the tribes. Benjamin’s **literal land allotment** was near Jerusalem—but here his **spiritual inheritance** is judgment, not favor.
- The phrase *“trusted in falsehood”* echoes **Jeremiah 7:8** (“Behold, ye trust in lying words, that cannot profit”)—a direct rebuke to those who follow doctrines not rooted in the covenant.
