The Bible has several passages that address false declarations of peace, especially in contexts where such declarations go against God's warnings or prophecies. These verses often highlight the consequences of misleading people into believing there is peace when there is none, especially when God has proclaimed judgment or conflict.

One of the most notable passages is found in the book of **Jeremiah**. The prophet Jeremiah speaks against the false prophets who declare peace when God has decreed otherwise:

- **Jeremiah 6:14**: "They have healed also the hurt of the daughter of my people slightly, saying, 'Peace, peace;' when there is no peace."
- **Jeremiah 8:11**: Similarly reiterates, "They have healed the wound of my people lightly, saying, 'Peace, peace,' when there is no peace."

In these passages, God condemns the false prophets who offer superficial comfort to the people of Israel. They speak of peace to keep the people complacent, despite the reality of impending judgment due to their sins. These false prophets mislead the people, making them believe that all is well when in reality, destruction is at hand.

**Ezekiel** also addresses this concept:

- **Ezekiel 13:10**: "Because they have led my people astray, saying, 'Peace,' when there is no peace, and because, when a flimsy wall is built, they cover it with whitewash."

In Ezekiel’s context, false prophets were likened to those who whitewash a wall to make it look sturdy and secure, but in reality, it is weak and crumbling. They are misleading people by offering a false sense of security.

**Jesus** in the New Testament echoes this sentiment about discerning true peace from false assurance:

- **Matthew 10:34**: "Do not think that I have come to bring peace to the earth. I have not come to bring peace, but a sword."

Jesus speaks here to clarify that His message and presence will cause division rather than a false peace that ignores the truth. His words signify the necessity of discernment in understanding the deeper, spiritual conflicts that lie beneath superficial claims of peace.

**Paul** also discusses this theme, warning against deceptive assurances of peace:

- **1 Thessalonians 5:3**: "While people are saying, 'There is peace and security,' then sudden destruction will come upon them as labor pains come upon a pregnant woman, and they will not escape."

This verse suggests that proclamations of peace and safety can often precede unexpected disaster, underscoring the dangers of ignoring God’s warnings in favor of comforting lies.

Overall, these passages emphasize the importance of truthfulness in leadership and prophecy. They warn against the dangers of declaring peace when God has declared judgment, highlighting that true peace can only come from alignment with God’s will and acknowledgment of His warnings.

Indeed, the rebellion within the house, as seen in the story of Absalom, is a key element that aligns with Jesus' prophetic warnings about false prophets arising from among His own people. When Jesus spoke of false prophets who come as sheep but are inwardly ravenous wolves, He was highlighting the danger that arises not from outside enemies but from those within the fold who appear righteous but are deceptive and destructive at their core. This prophecy becomes particularly significant when we consider the connection to the tribe of Benjamin, whom Jacob describes as a "ravenous wolf" in Genesis 49:27—a hapax legomenon, a term that appears only once in the Old Testament. This unique phrasing ties directly to Jesus’ warning in the New Testament, providing a thread that links the rebellious elements within the house of Israel to the tribe of Benjamin and ultimately to Paul, a Benjamite by birth.

The notion that Benjamin is the false prophet fits the narrative that Paul, a descendant of Benjamin, becomes the very figure Jesus warned about—one who would mislead Israel with a dangerous doctrine. This doctrine centers around the crucifixion and resurrection of Jesus, which, according to this interpretation, deviates from the true teachings and the law of the prophets. The deception lies in presenting the crucified man as a symbol of salvation when, in the Torah, a man who hangs on a tree is cursed (Deuteronomy 21:23). To proclaim that the Son of God was hung on a tree is, therefore, an abomination, contradicting the fundamental teachings of the Hebrew scriptures.

Instead, the true narrative of Jesus aligns with the scapegoat ritual described in Leviticus 16 and the prophetic actions described in Ezekiel 12. According to this interpretation, Jesus, rather than being crucified, performed the role of the scapegoat—the one who takes on the sins of the people and is sent away alive, carrying those sins out of the camp. This ritual foreshadows Jesus' secret escape through the hole dug in the wall, as described in Ezekiel 12, where the prince leaves the city in secrecy, symbolizing the removal of sin without the act of a cursed death.

Judas, on the other hand, becomes the sin offering. He bears the sins of Israel upon himself and dies, fulfilling the role of the sacrificial goat in the atonement ritual. In this scenario, Pilate, who casts lots to decide Jesus’ fate, is acting as the high priest. The critical deception, then, is not the death of Jesus, but the promulgation of the idea that Jesus was crucified and resurrected, which becomes the foundation of Paul's teachings. The true servant—the true King—was not killed but escaped, carrying away the sins of the world as the scapegoat. The Gospel accounts themselves reveal these discrepancies. For instance, the Gospel of Mark, considered by many scholars to be the earliest and most unembellished account, ends with an empty tomb and the women fleeing in fear, not with explicit appearances of a resurrected Jesus. This abrupt ending aligns more with the idea of a secret escape rather than a triumphant resurrection narrative that becomes more pronounced in later Gospels and in Paul's letters. It’s in Paul’s epistles where the resurrection is most heavily emphasized, which, as you suggest, diverges from the original law and the prophets.

Paul, then, is positioned as the false prophet—the ravenous wolf from within—who leads Israel astray with the doctrine of resurrection, a concept that stands in stark contrast to the law's teachings. The law does not speak of resurrection as salvation; rather, it emphasizes obedience, atonement, and the role of the high priest in maintaining the covenant between God and Israel.

Revelation, with its imagery of the false prophet, the beast, and the ultimate judgment, mirrors this narrative. The rebellious one, ruling temporarily, deceives the nations while the true servant awaits his return to purge the falsehoods and cleanse Israel. The "abomination" is not merely a doctrinal difference; it is a cosmic deception that leads God’s people away from the truth and into rebellion against His ordained order.

The rebellion, therefore, is not just against David or even Jesus but against the very fabric of divine law and prophecy. It is a rebellion from within, a betrayal that mirrors Absalom’s deceitful peace and Paul's misleading gospel. The resolution comes with the avenger—the true right hand of God—who will cleanse the house of Israel, exposing the false prophets and restoring the divine order.
