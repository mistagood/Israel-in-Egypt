The Gospel of John, when analyzed in reverse order, presents a nuanced counternarrative to the Synoptic Gospels—Matthew, Mark, and Luke—that provides layers of symbolism and theological implications. In this interpretive framework, the narrative arc of Jesus in the Gospel of John can be likened to the ritualistic processes surrounding the scapegoat and sin offering, as articulated in the Hebrew Scriptures.

### Scapegoat and High Priest: Pilate's Role

Pilate's interaction with Jesus in the later chapters can be seen as an inversion of the high priest's role in the Yom Kippur ritual described in Leviticus 16. Traditionally, the high priest would present two goats; one would be sacrificed as a sin offering, and the other, the scapegoat, would be released into the wilderness. Pilate, in offering to release Jesus or Barabbas (John 18:39-40), unwittingly replicates this ancient ritual. The crowd's choice of Barabbas echoes the selection of the scapegoat, while Jesus' subsequent crucifixion parallels the sin offering.

### Judas and Lazarus: The Sin Offering

Intriguingly, the identification of Judas as Lazarus provides a compelling layer to the narrative. Judas, often vilified as the betrayer, takes on a different role as the 'sin offering,' symbolically taking on the sins of the people. This resonates with the role Lazarus plays in John 11 when he is resurrected by Jesus. The act of raising Lazarus from the dead could be seen as a symbolic prelude to the general resurrection and the forgiveness of sins, akin to the purification achieved through the sin offering.

### The Fourth and Third Days: Resurrection and Reunion

The resurrection of Lazarus on the 'fourth day' could be an inverted reflection of Jesus' own resurrection narrative. Typically, Jesus is said to have risen on the third day, but by placing this event on the fourth day, the text may be emphasizing the completeness of the redemptive act (cf. Genesis 1:14-19, where the fourth day marks the completion of the celestial bodies).

### The Samaritan Woman: The Bride as Israel

The encounter with the Samaritan woman in John 4 gains additional significance in this framework. By characterizing her as the 'bride,' she becomes a metaphor for Israel, echoing the prophetic tradition where Israel is often portrayed as the bride of Yahweh (e.g., Isaiah 54:5; Hosea 2:19). The 'wedding' thus signifies not just a personal union but the reunification and cleansing of Israel.

### Miracles: Signs of the Kingdom

The miracles performed by Jesus in the Gospel of John can be interpreted as 'signs' pointing toward the inauguration of the Kingdom of God and the impending resurrection. This is consistent with John's unique focus on 'signs' rather than 'miracles,' emphasizing their revelatory function (John 20:30-31).

### John the Baptist: The Culmination

John the Baptist's declaration, "Behold the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!" (John 1:29), serves as a fitting culmination in this reverse reading. It brings the narrative full circle, affirming Jesus' role in the cosmic plan of redemption, and aligns well with the scapegoat and sin offering typologies.

### Cross-Referencing and Linguistic Nuances

This interpretation enables us to cross-reference various biblical texts, such as Leviticus 16 for the Yom Kippur ritual, Ezekiel 12:5 for the prophetic 'hole in the wall,' and Old Testament prophecies concerning Israel as the bride. Moreover, it calls attention to the special use of language, such as the term "fourth day" which is a hapax legomenon, appearing only in John 11:39.

### Connections to African American History

The concept of a scapegoat and sin offering, as well as the theme of liberation and reunification, deeply resonates with the history and conditions of African Americans. The redemptive arc symbolized by Jesus can be seen as a powerful metaphor for the struggles and hopes for liberation and equality.

In summary, reading the Gospel of John in reverse not only offers a novel interpretation but also enriches our understanding of its theological, historical, and cultural layers. It brings to light how word choices and narrative structure can both reveal and conceal key teachings, and provides a multifaceted lens through which to view the text.
