Yes, we do.

The English word **"logic"** comes from the Greek word **_logos_ (λόγος)**. In ancient Greek, _logos_ had a broad range of meanings, including:

- word  
- speech  
- reason  
- account  
- principle  
- argument  

It was used by philosophers like **Heraclitus**, **Plato**, and **Aristotle** to signify **reasoned discourse** or **the rational principle that governs the universe**.

When **Aristotle** formalized the study of reasoning, it was under the umbrella of **"logikē" (λογική)** — the art or science of _logos_ — which became **logic** in Latin (_logica_) and then entered Old French and English as **logic**.

So yes — **logic is the formalized offspring of logos**, narrowed into the systematized art of reasoning.

And in biblical texts, especially John 1:1 — _"In the beginning was the Logos..."_ — it’s a powerful metaphysical term meaning both **divine reason** and **creative word**, tying reason, speech, and being into one.

### 1. **Was the word "logos" changed to "verbum"?**

Yes, but not arbitrarily — here's how it happened:

- **In Greek:**  
  John 1:1 originally says:  
  > *Ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ Λόγος* — “In the beginning was the **Logos**.”

- **In Latin (Vulgate):**  
  Jerome, translating the Bible into Latin (late 4th century), renders this:  
  > *In principio erat **Verbum*** — “In the beginning was the **Word**.”

So **“logos”** was translated to **“verbum”**, which literally means “word” in Latin — just like “logos” can. But here's the twist:

> **Logos is much deeper than “word.”**  
> It means **reason, logic, discourse, principle**, and even **the mind of God** in Greek philosophy.

By translating **logos → verbum**, the **Latin Church** downplayed some of that **philosophical weight**. “Verbum” is more like *spoken or written word*, not the full concept of **divine reasoning** or **cosmic intelligence** that “logos” carried for Greek thinkers.

---

### 2. **So where does “logica” come in?**

- **"Logica"** is the Latin form of **logic**, derived directly from **logos**.
- While **verbum** became the standard word in *biblical* Latin for **logos**, the word **logica** was retained in **philosophical** and **scholastic** Latin.

By the **Middle Ages**, thinkers like **Boethius**, **Aquinas**, and **Peter Abelard** were distinguishing between:
- **Verbum Dei** — “Word of God” (the scriptural, spiritual expression)
- **Ratio / Logica** — “Reason / Logic” (the philosophical structure and method)

---

### 3. **So what’s the connection between *verbum* and *logica*?**

Think of them like **two sides of the same coin**:

- **Verbum** = the *spoken expression* of the thought  
- **Logica** = the *structure and reason* behind the thought

In **John 1:1**, Logos is both. It is the **uttered Word** and the **rational force**. But Latin chose to emphasize **verbum** — the communicative act — while **logica** survived as a discipline in philosophy and theology.
