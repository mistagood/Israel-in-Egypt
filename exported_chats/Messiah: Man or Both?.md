whats the difference between the modern definition of scapegoat and the levitical scapegoat tradition?

---

The term "scapegoat" has its origins in the religious rituals described in the Old Testament of the Bible, specifically in the book of Leviticus, but its modern usage in English has evolved to take on a different meaning.

In the Levitical tradition, a scapegoat was a literal goat that was ritually burdened with the sins of the people and then sent into the wilderness as part of the Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement) ceremonies. Two goats were selected: one was sacrificed, and the other, the scapegoat, had all the sins of the people symbolically placed upon it by the high priest. This scapegoat was then released into the wilderness, symbolically carrying away the sins of the community.

The modern definition of "scapegoat," however, has lost its literal and religious connotations. Today, a scapegoat refers to a person or group that is unjustly blamed for problems. The scapegoat bears the blame for others, often as a way of deflecting responsibility or accountability. For example, if a project at work fails and one person is singled out and blamed for the failure, even though many factors or people may have contributed to it, that person could be considered a scapegoat.

So, while both the Levitical and modern definitions involve the idea of bearing blame, the context, connotation, and usage have changed significantly. In Levitical tradition, the scapegoat is a part of a religious ritual to symbolically cleanse the people of their sins. In contrast, the modern usage usually refers to an unjust situation where blame is assigned to an innocent party or is disproportionately assigned to one party among many that may share responsibility.

### Historical Context

The 1st century was a melting pot of cultures, religions, and philosophies. The Roman Empire's vast expanse allowed for a plethora of religious ideas to intermingle and influence one another. While Rome's paganism was dominant, Judaism held a significant presence, particularly in the eastern Mediterranean regions.

#### Judaism and the Roman Empire:

By the time Jesus began his ministry, Judaism was already a well-established religion, deeply rooted in its traditions, laws, and rituals. The Jewish people, despite their religious resilience, had experienced a series of subjugations— from the Babylonians to the Seleucids and now, the Romans. The presence of Roman rule was a constant reminder of their lack of political freedom, often leading to tensions.

This societal tension played a foundational role in the Jewish expectation of a Messiah— a savior who would liberate them from Roman rule and reestablish the Davidic kingdom. These eschatological hopes were deeply ingrained in the Jewish psyche, heavily influencing their interpretation of Jesus' message and mission.

#### Emergence of Christianity:

Jesus of Nazareth, while born into this turbulent milieu, presented teachings that often deviated from traditional Jewish thought. His message of love, forgiveness, and the imminent Kingdom of God found followers, but also created adversaries. Crucially, the movement that emerged post his crucifixion was primarily led by his disciples, with Peter taking a central role.

However, the early Christian message soon found a new proponent: Saul of Tarsus, later known as Paul. A Pharisee turned Christian post a profound conversion experience, Paul's interpretation of Jesus' teachings and his mission to the Gentiles dramatically influenced the trajectory of early Christianity.

#### The Pharisees:

As a Pharisee, Paul belonged to a Jewish sect known for its strict adherence to the Mosaic Law and its belief in the resurrection. Pharisees were often at loggerheads with other Jewish groups, like the Sadducees, due to their differing theological stances. Jesus himself had confrontations with the Pharisees, accusing them of hypocrisy and legalism. It's within this framework that we need to understand Paul's pre-conversion background and its implications on his Christian teachings.

### The Role of Peter and Paul

#### Peter: The "Rock" of the Church

Simon Peter, often simply called Peter, was one of Jesus' closest disciples. In the Gospel accounts, Peter is depicted as passionate, impulsive, yet deeply devoted to Christ. A series of interactions highlight his central role in the early Christian movement:

1. **Confession of Faith**: In Matthew 16:15-18, when Jesus asks his disciples, "Who do you say that I am?", Peter's response, "You are the Messiah, the Son of the living God," earns him Jesus' commendation. Jesus goes on to say, "You are Peter, and on this rock I will build my church." Here, Peter is established as foundational to the future Church.
  
2. **The Transfiguration**: Peter is one of the three disciples (alongside James and John) who witnesses the transfiguration of Jesus, further solidifying his role in Jesus' inner circle (Matthew 17:1-9).
  
3. **Denial and Restoration**: Peter's threefold denial of Jesus during His trial and subsequent restoration by the risen Christ (John 21:15-19) depicts a disciple's weakness but also emphasizes the depth of divine forgiveness and restoration.

However, Peter's leadership was not without its controversies. His vision of the unclean animals, as recorded in Acts 10, shows him grappling with the implications of the Gospel for Gentiles. By the time of the Jerusalem Council (Acts 15), the tension between the Jewish law and the Gospel's universality becomes even clearer.

#### Paul: The Apostle to the Gentiles

Paul's introduction in the Christian narrative is as a persecutor, one who zealously hunted down followers of "The Way." However, his dramatic conversion on the road to Damascus (Acts 9) transformed him from a staunch Pharisee to one of Christianity's most fervent advocates.

1. **Paul's Theology**: Influenced by his Pharisaic background and his profound conversion experience, Paul emphasized faith in Christ over works of the law (Galatians 2:15-21). His letters often explore the relationship between the Mosaic Law and Christ's redemptive work.

2. **Missionary Journeys**: Paul undertook several missionary journeys, establishing churches across the Roman Empire. His letters to these churches form a significant portion of the New Testament and offer insights into early Christian theology and practice.

3. **Clash with Peter**: In Galatians 2:11-14, Paul recounts a confrontation with Peter over the latter's withdrawal from Gentile believers. This incident highlights the ongoing debate within early Christianity about the role of Mosaic Law.

4. **Paul's Roman Citizenship**: As a Roman citizen, Paul had rights and privileges that aided his missionary work. His Roman identity allowed him access to diverse audiences, eventually leading him to Rome, the heart of the empire.

---

Despite their different backgrounds and approaches, both Peter and Paul were instrumental in shaping early Christianity. Their combined efforts, though occasionally at odds, were crucial in transitioning the Christian faith from a small Jewish sect to a global movement.
