Several Hebrew names carry the meaning “God is my strength” or “God strengthens.” Here are the most direct ones:

- **Uzziel (עֻזִּיאֵל)** — literally *“God is my strength.”* From *ʿuzz* (strength, might) + *El* (God). Found in Exodus 6:18.  
- **Azriel (עַזְרִיאֵל)** — *“God is my help/strength.”* From *ʿezer* (help/aid) + *El* (God).  
- **Ezekiel (יְחֶזְקֵאל / Yeḥezqel)** — *“God strengthens.”* From *ḥazaq* (to strengthen) + *El.*  
- **Hezekiah (חִזְקִיָּה / Ḥizqiyyah)** — *“Yahweh is my strength.”* Same root as Ezekiel, with the divine name Yah.  

If you want the most literal match to “God is my strength,” **Uzziel** is the exact one. If you want “God strengthens,” **Ezekiel** is the classic form. If you want “Yahweh is my strength,” **Hezekiah** carries that.

Yes, you’ve spotted a deep overlap. Both names come from the same Hebrew root **ḥ-z-q (חזק)**, which means “to be strong, to strengthen.”  

- **Ezekiel (Yeḥezqel)** = “God strengthens.”  
- **Hezekiah (Ḥizqiyyahu)** = “Yahweh is my strength.”  

It’s the same idea: divine empowerment, just expressed with slightly different grammar and divine names (*El* vs. *Yahu*).  

Now, about the visions. Ezekiel’s great vision of the dry bones (Ezekiel 37) is often applied to the story of Hezekiah. Why? Because Hezekiah lived during the Assyrian crisis, when Jerusalem was nearly destroyed. Rabbinic commentary and later Christian interpreters sometimes retroactively connected Ezekiel’s vision to that desperate moment—seeing the “revival of bones” as an allegory for Judah’s survival under Hezekiah.  

But in the text itself, Ezekiel is exiled in Babylon, more than a century after Hezekiah. His vision is about the “whole house of Israel” being restored, not about a past king. The confusion comes from the fact that:  

1. Their names both mean “God strengthens.”  
2. Hezekiah’s reign was seen as a miraculous survival.  
3. Ezekiel’s prophecy was seen as the ultimate metaphor for national survival.  

So later traditions blurred the lines, reading Ezekiel’s imagery *as if* it were about Hezekiah, since both names and both contexts point to God’s strength reviving His people.

Ah, now that’s even more pointed. Ezekiel 12 is the “acted-out vision” of **exile**. God tells Ezekiel to pack his bags in the sight of the people, dig through the wall, and go out at twilight, carrying his bundle on his shoulder (Ezekiel 12:3–7). It’s a sign that the prince in Jerusalem—and the people with him—will go into captivity.  

Here’s the twist:  

- **Hezekiah’s name (Ḥizqiyyahu = Yah strengthens)** suggests endurance and divine support. His reign was remembered for survival against overwhelming odds (the Assyrian siege, the shadow on the sundial).  
- **Ezekiel (Yeḥezqel = God strengthens)** acts out weakness—blindness, exile, hunger, trembling—yet in the background, the same root idea is present: only God can supply strength when human kings collapse.  

Some Jewish interpreters actually link Ezekiel 12 with Hezekiah because of the *“digging through the wall”* detail. In 2 Kings 20:20, Hezekiah is said to have made a conduit and brought water into the city. In Ezekiel 12, the prince breaks through a wall to escape. It’s an ironic reversal: Hezekiah strengthened the city by digging a channel in; Ezekiel shows a weakened prince digging out to flee.  

So, when people say Ezekiel’s vision “was about Hezekiah,” it’s not that Ezekiel 12 literally describes him, but that the **name-theme and wall-imagery** naturally echo back to Hezekiah’s story. Both men embody the tension between human frailty and divine strengthening:  

- **Hezekiah:** strengthened to resist Assyria.  
- **Ezekiel’s vision:** judgment and exile, with strength only possible through God’s future restoration.  

It’s almost like Ezekiel is dramatizing what would have happened *if God had not strengthened Hezekiah.*
