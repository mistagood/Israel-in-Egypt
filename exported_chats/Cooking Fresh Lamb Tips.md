Great! You’re in for a treat—fresh halal lamb, especially marinated, can be incredibly flavorful and tender. Since you’ve already done the important step of marinating, here are a few tried-and-true ways to cook it, depending on what you’re working with and what tools you have:

---

### **Option 1: Pan-Searing and Simmering (for Stew-Style or Bone-In Cuts)**
**Best for:** Mixed cuts with bones, especially if you want a tender, fall-apart texture.

**Steps:**
1. **Sear First:** Heat a heavy pan (cast iron or Dutch oven ideal) with a bit of oil until hot. Add the lamb pieces in batches (don’t overcrowd) and sear them until browned on all sides. This locks in flavor.
2. **Simmer:** Once browned, add some water or broth (chicken or beef), maybe a chopped onion or garlic, and cover the pan. Let it simmer on **low** for 1.5 to 2 hours. Check occasionally and add liquid if needed.
3. **Finish:** Uncover to reduce the sauce a bit if you want it thicker. Taste and adjust seasoning.

---

### **Option 2: Oven-Roasting (if bone-in or large chunks)**
**Best for:** Larger chunks or meaty cuts, less ideal for stew meat.

**Steps:**
1. Preheat oven to **375°F (190°C)**.
2. Place marinated lamb in a roasting dish or on a rack in a baking pan.
3. Roast for 30–40 minutes (for boneless chunks) or up to 1 hour (for bone-in), until browned and cooked through. Internal temp should hit **145°F (medium-rare)** or **160°F (medium)** if using a thermometer.

---

### **Option 3: Grilling or Broiling (if you have skewers or want charred flavor)**
**Best for:** Boneless pieces, kebab-style cuts.

**Steps:**
1. Thread lamb onto skewers if desired.
2. Grill or broil on high heat for **4–5 minutes per side**, until nicely charred and cooked through.
3. Let rest a few minutes before serving.

---

### **Quick Notes:**
- **If you’re unsure about doneness**, cut into a thicker piece. Lamb can be pink in the middle, but should be hot all the way through.
- **Marinade bonus:** If it has yogurt, lemon, vinegar, or spices, that’ll tenderize the meat and add depth. You can use the leftover marinade in the pan sauce if it was never exposed to raw meat for too long.
