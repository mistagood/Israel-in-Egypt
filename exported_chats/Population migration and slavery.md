---

## 2. Why that matters
If a distinctive regional origin (say, East African or Bantu coastal populations) was disproportionately represented in this pulse, then the genetic “founder effect” would be very strong. Descendants of that group would show signals amplified relative to their overall numbers. That’s why African-American genetic data often shows strong clustering around particular regions (West-Central Africa, Gold Coast, Bight of Benin).

---

## 3. East African vs. West African
Shipping data shows the **United States’ late imports came mainly from West and West-Central Africa** (Congo/Angola, Bight of Benin, Senegambia).  
Large-scale East African (Mozambique, Madagascar) exports went mostly to **Brazil, Cuba, Indian Ocean islands**—not North America. U.S. arrivals from East Africa are documented but relatively rare.

So, if an East African signal shows up in African Americans today, the parsimonious explanation is either:
- Smaller direct imports from East Africa during that 1790–1810 spike, or  
- Secondary imports (captives redistributed via the Caribbean).

- **General U.S. population (c. 1800):** ~55‰ CBR.  
- **Enslaved population (1810–30):** ~53‰ CBR.  

That’s not a “one report” cherry-pick; multiple demographers working from different record sets converge on the enslaved population having a CBR in the low-50s.  

---

## Why growth was higher for slaves despite “lower” CBR
Here’s the important distinction:

- **CBR** tells you raw births per 1,000 each year.  
- **Growth rate** is **(births – deaths) / population**.  

So, if two groups have similar birth rates but one has *different mortality*, the growth rates diverge.  

The U.S. white population had CBR ~55, CDR ~25 → net natural increase ~30 per 1,000 → growth ~3%/yr.  
The U.S. enslaved population had CBR ~53, CDR ~28–30 → net ~23–25 per 1,000 → growth ~2.3–2.5%/yr.  

So even with a slightly lower birth rate than the general population, they still grew at nearly the same pace because their mortality was in a range that made that arithmetic work out.

You’re right: it can’t be both. Here are the actual like-for-like comparisons.

## 1) Birth rate (CBR) vs. growth rate (r)
- **CBR** = births per 1,000 population per year.  
- **Growth rate** \(r\) = \((\text{births} - \text{deaths})/\text{population}\) (ignoring migration for now).

A population can have a **slightly lower CBR** than another group and still **grow rapidly** if deaths aren’t so high as to cancel births. But it **cannot** simultaneously “grow faster” than the other group unless its **\(r\)** is higher.

## 2) What the U.S. numbers actually imply (1790→1860)
- **Enslaved population:** 697,624 → 3,953,760  
  \(\Rightarrow r_{\text{enslaved}} \approx 2.509\%\) per year (about **25.09‰**).
- **Total U.S. population (all people):** ~3.93M → ~31.4M  
  \(\Rightarrow r_{\text{US total}} \approx 3.013\%\) per year (about **30.13‰**).

**Conclusion:** the enslaved population grew **slower** than the **total** U.S. population over 1790–1860. So any statement that “slaves grew faster than the U.S. population” is **false**. What is true is that the enslaved population in the U.S. grew **much faster** than enslaved populations in many Caribbean/South-American sugar/mining zones (different comparison).

## 3) Where the 53‰ point fits
Reconstructed enslaved **CBR ≈ 53‰** (low-50s) is **slightly below** the free U.S. CBR (often ~55‰ around 1800). Pair that with an average enslaved **CDR** in the upper-20s/low-30s‰ and you get the observed enslaved **r ≈ 25‰**. That delivers **rapid growth**, yet still **slower** than the overall U.S. \(r ≈ 30‰\).

## Bottom line
- **No contradiction:** enslaved CBR ≈ 53‰ can be a bit **lower** than the general U.S. CBR and still yield **rapid** growth—just not **faster** than the total U.S. growth.  
- The often-quoted “faster growth” point properly applies to **U.S. enslaved vs. other slave societies**, not **vs. the whole U.S. population**.

Exactly — you’ve landed on the crux of the problem: the **numbers and the narrative don’t align cleanly**.  

Let’s disentangle it step by step.

---

## 1. The reconstructed numbers
- **General U.S. population CDR (1800s):** ~20–25‰.  
- **Enslaved CDR (reconstructed averages):** ~28–32‰.  

That gap is not huge. It suggests that, statistically, enslaved people were only a little “sicker” or shorter-lived than free Americans. If you just read those numbers, slavery doesn’t look like the catastrophic mortality trap the narrative describes.

---

## 2. Why the numbers might not capture the reality
Several issues explain the mismatch:

1. **Averages smooth out extremes**  
   – If half of slave children died young, but adult survivors lived into their 40s, then the *average crude death rate* can still hover around 30 per 1,000.  
   – High infant mortality inflates the rate, but adults surviving long enough to bear children keeps it from exploding like Brazil’s.

2. **Underreporting and data bias**  
   – Most U.S. slave “vital statistics” come from plantation records kept by owners. They had motives to misreport, especially under laws discouraging importation after 1808. “Born here” was more useful than “smuggled in.”  
   – Infant and child deaths in slave quarters often went unrecorded or misclassified, biasing CDR *downward*.  

3. **Selection effects**  
   – By the 1790s, the U.S. slave population was already heavily American-born. Newly arrived Africans (who suffered catastrophic “seasoning” mortality like Brazil’s) were fewer. Survivors represented a “hardened” population — biasing death rates downward relative to the first generation.

4. **Narrative vs. statistics**  
   – Enslaved narratives emphasize whippings, killings, deprivation, and the daily brutality of slavery. Those don’t always translate into elevated *crude death rates* when averaged over millions.  
   – Systematic cruelty can coexist with “statistical survival” if fertility is coerced and mortality, while high, is not catastrophic enough to sink the population curve.

---

## 3. The infant survival problem
You’re right: if “half of slave children died,” that alone should push the CDR sky-high. But what’s actually documented in plantation registers is **infant mortality ~150–200 per 1,000 births** (15–20%), not 50%. That is much higher than white infants (~100 per 1,000), but not half. Add in childhood mortality, and still, about half of enslaved children reached adulthood. That’s bad compared to whites, but not so extreme that it blows the whole population apart.

So:  
- Child survival = worse than whites.  
- Adult survival into 30s–40s = possible.  
- Result = a population that could reproduce itself and grow, even under constant brutality.

---

## 4. The logical tension
You’re right: if the CDR was truly 40–60‰ (like Brazil’s), then the U.S. census curve can’t be explained without **hidden inflows or erased populations**.  
But if we accept the reconstructed 28–32‰, then slavery in the U.S. statistically looks “only moderately worse” than general pre-modern life — which doesn’t line up with the lived horror documented in testimony.  

That is where the suspicion of **fabricated or massaged numbers** creeps in. Planters had every incentive to underreport deaths and overreport births. The “natural increase” narrative conveniently made the U.S. look less dependent on smuggling and more “self-sustaining.”

---

## 5. So where do the narratives of deadly slavery come from?
- **Firsthand accounts** (Douglass, Jacobs, WPA interviews) describe the social death, violence, arbitrary killings, hunger, disease, sexual coercion.  
- **Demographic records** are biased and smoothed, not showing local spikes (cholera, smallpox, plantation massacres).  
- **Comparisons to whites** still show worse infant survival, worse adult mortality, shorter life spans — but the numbers stop short of the catastrophic mortality of Brazil.  

---

### Bottom line
The official demographic reconstructions give U.S. slaves a **CDR only ~5–10‰ higher** than whites. That’s too low to match the narrative of wholesale premature death unless:  
1. Records were manipulated to hide smuggling and deaths, **or**  
2. Survivors of the Middle Passage + first generations were “selected” for endurance, depressing long-run averages, **or**  
3. The horror of slavery lay less in statistical mortality than in **social, psychological, and violent oppression**, which raw CDRs don’t measure.
