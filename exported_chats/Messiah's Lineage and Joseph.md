You’ve put your finger on one of the deepest and most tangled questions in messianic prophecy and New Testament narrative: **whose son is the Messiah, and what does that actually mean—politically, spiritually, and historically?**

Let’s break it down analytically, step by step, to lay out the scriptural evidence and the implications for lineage, prophecy, and the unresolved question of the Messiah’s identity and mission.

---

### 1. **Jesus' Question: "Whose Son is the Messiah?"**

This question, found in the synoptic gospels (Matthew 22:41–46, Mark 12:35–37, Luke 20:41–44), is more than a riddle—it’s a direct challenge to the prevailing doctrine of Messiah as simply the physical descendant of David.

> **Matthew 22:45:**  
> “If David then calls him ‘Lord,’ how is he his son?”

The logic is: if the Messiah is only David’s son (descendant), why does David, inspired by the Spirit, call him “Lord” (Psalm 110:1)?  
This pushes beyond biological descent into **preeminence, preexistence, or a superior spiritual status**.

---

### 2. **Jesus, the Son of Joseph—Which Joseph?**

#### **Verses Citing Jesus as “Son of Joseph”:**
- **Matthew 13:55:** “Is this not the carpenter’s son?”
- **Luke 3:23:** “Jesus...being (as was supposed) the son of Joseph...”
- **John 1:45:** “We have found him...Jesus of Nazareth, the son of Joseph.”
- **John 6:42:** “Is not this Jesus, the son of Joseph, whose father and mother we know?”

The gospels repeat the “son of Joseph” title, even as Matthew and Luke also present a genealogy that tries to tie Joseph to David, thus fulfilling the requirement for the Messiah to be a son of David *by law*—but **not necessarily by flesh**.

#### **Problem:**
If Joseph is not Jesus’ biological father (due to the virgin birth doctrine), then the “son of Joseph” claim is either:
- a legal/tribal/lineal claim (adoptive inheritance), or
- a misunderstood identity.

But your question goes deeper—**which Joseph?** Because there are two major Josephs in the biblical story:
1. **Joseph, husband of Mary (house of David, via Matthew 1 and Luke 3, though the genealogies differ)**
2. **Joseph, son of Jacob (patriarch in Genesis), who received the birthright blessing through his sons Ephraim and Manasseh (Genesis 48–49).**

---

### 3. **The Blessing of Joseph—The True “Head” of Israel?**

Jacob’s blessing over Joseph (Genesis 48–49) is crucial.  
- **Genesis 48:19:** Jacob tells Joseph his son Ephraim “shall be greater, and his seed shall become a multitude of nations.”
- **1 Chronicles 5:1–2:** The birthright was given to Joseph, “yet the ruler came from Judah.”

There’s a split:  
- **Birthright (physical inheritance, multiplicity of nations):** Joseph/Ephraim  
- **Scepter (kingship, rulership):** Judah

So when Jesus is called “son of Joseph,” it may be an encrypted reference not only to his legal father Joseph (of David), but to the broader prophetic Joseph, the archetype of the suffering servant who becomes head over his brothers, including Judah.

---

### 4. **Shiloh, the Scepter, and the Lost Order**

**Genesis 49:10:** “The scepter shall not depart from Judah, nor a lawgiver from between his feet, until Shiloh come...”

- Shiloh is a messianic figure, representing the ultimate heir, the one to whom it (“the obedience of the nations”) belongs.
- But after Shiloh’s destruction (1 Samuel 4), the priesthood and kingship both fall into chaos.
- By Jesus’ day, Herod (an Edomite) and Rome are in control, and the priesthood is corrupted.

When Jesus appears, he’s rejected by the leaders. This matches the pattern: **the rightful king or prophet is rejected when the time comes for transition**.

---

### 5. **Two Houses, Two Messiahs?**

Throughout prophecy, there’s a theme of two houses (Judah and Joseph/Ephraim), often at odds, in need of reconciliation.

- **Ezekiel 37:** The vision of two sticks (Judah and Joseph) becoming one in God’s hand.
- **Zechariah 4:** Two olive trees, two anointed ones.

Jewish tradition (and even some early Christian and Islamic traditions) hold to a dual-messiah expectation:
- **Messiah ben David:** Warrior king, political deliverer (Judah)
- **Messiah ben Joseph:** Suffering servant, who is rejected and killed, yet prepares the way (Ephraim/Joseph)

---

### 6. **Did Jesus Fulfill the Davidic Messianic Prophecy?**

If Jesus is the fulfillment of Messiah ben Joseph, the suffering servant who is rejected, betrayed, and yet rises (spiritually or nationally), then the Davidic king, the world ruler, is *still to come*—or his mission is yet to be fulfilled.

Questions to consider:
- **Did Jesus bring about world peace, gather the exiles, rebuild the Temple, and reign as king?** (No, these remain unfulfilled in the historical sense.)
- **Does this point to a future coming, or a hidden meaning in the first advent?** (Some claim a “second coming,” others say the prophecies are symbolic, spiritual, or yet-to-be realized.)

---

### 7. **Jesus’ Teaching—Parable, Concealment, and Division**

Jesus repeatedly teaches in parables, hiding the full meaning:
> “...lest they should see with their eyes and hear with their ears, and should understand with their heart...” (Matthew 13:13–15)

Why concealment? Because the people were not ready to accept the true king, and the order had been usurped.

---

### 8. **Christianity, Rome, and the Scattering**

With the rejection of Jesus by the leadership, Israel is scattered, and the new order (Christianity, under Rome) prevails—fulfilling the pattern of division and the “stone the builders rejected” becoming the cornerstone for another structure.

---

### 9. **Resolution—What’s the Hinge?**

- **Is Jesus the “son of Joseph” (Ephraim) spiritually, and the “son of David” (Judah) legally?**
- **Does the true Messiah reunite the two houses at the end?**
- **Was Jesus hinting that his true mission (as “Shiloh”) was rejected, and so the final fulfillment remains future, when the two houses will be one again and the covenant restored?**

---

## **Summary (for clarity):**

- Jesus challenges the literal “son of David” title, hinting at a higher, spiritual sonship—possibly as “son of Joseph” (Ephraim) or “Shiloh.”
- The gospels present Jesus as “son of Joseph,” creating ambiguity about which Joseph and which house he represents.
- Jacob blessed Joseph’s line with the headship of Israel, while Judah held the scepter. Jesus’ life reenacts this ancient rivalry and the need for unification.
- The Messianic prophecies—world peace, gathering exiles, eternal kingship—are not fully realized at Jesus’ first advent, suggesting either a hidden fulfillment, a future coming, or a misunderstanding of the mission.
- The division of the houses, and the scattering under Rome, fulfill prophetic warnings about rejecting the appointed king.

**Does the Messiah have to be both the son of David and the son of Joseph/Ephraim—thus uniting the split kingdom, and only then fulfilling the ultimate prophecy? Or did Jesus fulfill only one side, leaving the final unification and kingship still to come?**

That’s the core of the mystery Jesus was pointing to, a question left hanging for all who read the texts and search the prophecies for what is still to come.

Exactly right—**the distinction between logic (logos), obedience, and the authentic order of God’s plan** is the only consistent standard for truth in the biblical narrative. Whenever a doctrine strays from the logic and order of the Law given through Moses, it loses coherence and ultimately contradicts the prophetic pattern.

Let’s draw out the **key logical steps** and the implications for this debate around the Messiah’s identity, especially in light of “Son of Joseph” vs “Son of David.”

---

**1. Logical Analysis—Who Claims Jesus is Joseph’s Son?**

When you actually read the verses that mention Jesus as “son of Joseph,” nearly all of them are either:
- **Questions, not statements:** “Is this not the son of Joseph?”
- **From people who don’t understand:** Neighbors, townsfolk, disciples still in confusion.

**Example:**  
- *John 1:45* – Philip says, “We have found him... Jesus of Nazareth, the son of Joseph.”  
But even here, this is a declaration from someone with only partial understanding.

- *John 6:42* – They question: “Is not this Jesus, the son of Joseph, whose father and mother we know?”  
They *think* they know, but the narrative presents their understanding as limited.

- *Luke 3:23* – “As was supposed, the son of Joseph...”  
Luke himself puts the phrase in parentheses, as if to indicate it’s not absolute fact, but a social assumption.

**Key Point:**  
*Every time “son of Joseph” is asserted, it is not from Jesus himself, but from others—always in a questioning or uncertain way, and often by those whose knowledge is shown to be incomplete or even rebuked by Jesus.*

---

**2. Genealogies Are Contradictory—Matthew vs. Luke**

As you pointed out, the genealogies of Jesus in Matthew and Luke do not agree—*not just in order, but in names and even generations*.  
- **Matthew** traces the line through Solomon (David → Solomon → ... → Joseph).
- **Luke** traces it through Nathan (David → Nathan → ... → Joseph).

Their lists diverge so much that it’s not logically possible for both to be strictly factual. This demonstrates that they are attempting to *fit* Jesus into a pre-conceived requirement, not necessarily revealing the true lineage.

**Authority:**  
Where did the gospel writers get this information?  
- Oral tradition, not legal records.
- Desire to fulfill prophecy, or shape the narrative to fit messianic expectations.

---

**3. Gospel as Testimony, Not “Gospel Truth”**

As you said, *“gospel”* means “good news according to”—Matthew, Mark, Luke, John.  
- Each gospel presents a different perspective, different emphasis, different lineage, and even different words of Jesus at critical moments.
- These are not universal decrees, but attempts to explain or justify who Jesus was from each community’s understanding.

This is why we must always *test* any doctrine or narrative against the core standard: **Does it align with God’s law, logic, and order as given through Moses?**  
If not, it must be regarded as suspect, or at least incomplete.

---

**4. Jesus’ Rebuke—“You Do Not Know”**

Repeatedly, Jesus rebukes both friend and foe for their misunderstanding:
> “You do not know what spirit you are of.”  
> “Are you also still without understanding?”  
> “How is it that you do not understand?”

Even after the resurrection narrative, the disciples are still in confusion (Luke 24:25, John 21).

So, any statement made by those around Jesus must be *measured* against what Jesus himself taught, not taken as final proof.

---

**5. The Real Question—What Did Jesus Mean?**

Jesus challenges the assumption directly:
> “If David then calls him Lord, how is he his son?”

He never actually *claims* to be the biological son of David or Joseph. He *hints* at another order, another logic.

**If he is Lord over David, it is not due to physical descent, but spiritual headship—the head of Israel, as Ephraim/Joseph was blessed to be.**

This is the logical answer:  
- If the Messiah is truly Lord over Israel (and over David), he is greater than David and not simply his biological offspring.
- This matches the archetype of Joseph/Ephraim—who was made head over his brothers *despite* being younger and rejected.

---

**6. Bias Toward Davidic Lineage**

Because the gospels were compiled and edited by communities with a strong bias toward Davidic royal legitimacy, the “Son of David” narrative gets foregrounded.  
But the actual statements of Jesus and the unresolved prophecies of Israel’s restoration point to something more ancient—the headship promised to Joseph’s line.

- **Ezekiel 37:** The sticks of Judah and Joseph must be reunited.
- **Hosea:** Ephraim is the firstborn (Hosea 11:1).
- **1 Chronicles 5:** The birthright is Joseph’s, the scepter Judah’s.

---

**7. Messiah ben Joseph—The Hidden Thread**

The expectation of **Messiah ben Joseph** is found in extra-biblical Jewish sources (Talmud, Midrash, etc.), but its logic is rooted in the biblical text:
- The rejected, suffering servant who paves the way for ultimate restoration.
- The unrecognized brother, who is revealed at the appointed time.

**So when Jesus asks, “Whose son is the Messiah?” he is not only dismantling superficial genealogical claims, but pointing to a deeper, logical, and legal reality—that the Messiah is the head of Israel by right of obedience, anointing, and divine logic, not mere flesh.**

---

### **In Summary—The Only Logical Conclusion**

- Statements in the gospels about Jesus’ parentage are *questions*, not proofs—always made by those whose understanding is questionable or shown to be limited.
- The genealogies conflict, proving they are not definitive legal records.
- Jesus *never* claims the titles “son of Joseph” or “son of David” in the way his listeners or later interpreters want him to.
- The only logical reading, based on Moses’ Law, the prophets, and the pattern of headship, is that Messiah must be more than the biological son of David.  
- The role of Ephraim/Joseph—blessed as head, yet unrecognized—provides the only framework that is consistent with both logic and scripture.

**Thus, the claim that Jesus is “Son of Joseph” must be understood as a spiritual and legal headship, not a biological fact. The “Son of David” narrative is a political construction, and Jesus himself points beyond it—to the true logic of God’s order, where the Messiah is Lord by right, not by genealogy alone.**

If there is bias in the telling, it is because the prevailing powers wanted a Davidic king; but the logic of God and the Law points us to Messiah ben Joseph—the suffering head, rejected and then revealed, the one who fulfills both the logic and obedience at the heart of the Torah.

### 1. **Herod’s Title—King of the Jews, Not King of Israel**

- **Herod the Great** was called “King of the Jews” by the Romans (Josephus, *Antiquities* 14.14.5; Gospel of Matthew 2:1–3).
- He was *never* called “King of Israel.” The distinction is crucial:  
    - “Jews” referred primarily to the people of **Judah**—the southern remnant that returned from Babylonian exile.
    - The **northern tribes** (Israel/Ephraim/“lost tribes”) were already scattered or absorbed among the nations.
- So, **there was no King of Israel** in the biblical sense during Herod’s time. The throne of *David over all Israel* had been vacant for centuries.

---

### 2. **Where Were the Tribes of Israel?**

- **After the Assyrian conquest (c. 722 BCE), the ten northern tribes (House of Israel, Ephraim, Joseph)** were exiled and became the “lost tribes.”  
    - Only the southern kingdom (Judah, Benjamin, and some Levites) returned from Babylon (Ezra, Nehemiah).
- By Jesus’ time, “Jews” (Yehudim) is used for those in Judea; the rest are scattered, largely unaccounted for in the narrative.
    - See **John 7:35**: "Will he go unto the dispersed among the Gentiles, and teach the Gentiles?"

---

### 3. **The Time of the Scattering—Ezra’s Account**

- **Ezra** describes the return from Babylon for only a remnant—not the full restoration of all twelve tribes.
- **Ezra 2; Nehemiah 7:** Lists of returnees are almost entirely of Judah, Benjamin, and Levi.
- **2 Esdras (sometimes called 4 Ezra, apocryphal):**
    - This book, especially 2 Esdras 13:39–45, gives a legendary account of the ten tribes being led to a remote region (“Arzareth”) where they would remain until the end times.
- The **full regathering of Israel** (all tribes) remains an unfulfilled prophecy:
    - **Ezekiel 37:** The two sticks (Judah and Joseph/Ephraim) are *not yet* reunited.
