What you’ve described is a strong modern reading of Genesis 2–3: the *Tree of Knowledge of Good and Evil* as the seed of human over-specialization. Instead of seeing it only as “moral awareness,” you’re pointing to how humanity’s development of an oversized brain and a drive for ingenuity led to both survival and eventual dependency. Let me break it down in structured form:

---

### 1. Overspecialization as the “fruit”
- **Biological angle**: Our brains became disproportionately large, consuming enormous energy. This brain expansion fueled abstract thought, symbolic language, technology, and specialization.
- **Civilizational angle**: We built systems of agriculture, industry, and technology that allowed us to survive—but at the cost of becoming dependent on them.
- **Parallel to Genesis**: Eating the fruit gave immediate insight and capability, but also introduced fragility and exposure (Adam and Eve realize their nakedness). Similarly, human specialization gives power but exposes weakness.

---

### 2. The death consequence
- **Fragility of civilization**: Because we depend on complex inventions, removing them (electricity, global supply chains, digital networks) would collapse society. This fragility is a “death sentence” encoded into our survival strategy.
- **Symbolic death**: In Genesis, death enters the human condition when knowledge is grasped prematurely. In technological terms, our innovations lead toward potential extinction events—ecological collapse, nuclear war, AI risks, etc.

---

### 3. Peril without inventions
- **Dependence paradox**: We survive only through the things that enslave us—machines, medicine, agriculture, digital infrastructure. Strip them away and civilization teeters.
- **Hidden prophecy**: The “tree” is not just a moral choice but a trajectory: humanity gains mastery but loses resilience. We cannot return to the garden (simplicity of hunter-gatherer life), nor can we live without the artificial systems we’ve built.

---

### 4. Semiotic reading
- **Tree = knowledge systems (science/technology).**  
- **Fruit = specialized expertise that feeds advancement but narrows adaptability.**  
- **Death = collapse when dependency reaches totality.**

Well, it brings into question, what does it mean to be human? What was man before this fragility, before the fall into the fragile state of man, the lower state of man, the son of man? What was man? What was he intended to be?

---

That’s the right question—because Genesis frames the “fall” not just as a mistake but as a transformation of what it means to be human. Let’s lay this out carefully:

---

## 1. Man Before the Fall
- **Formed of dust, breathed into by God** (Gen. 2:7): Humanity was clay animated by divine breath—frail in body, yet directly sustained by God’s spirit. This made man **immortal in potential** but not yet tested.
- **Placed in a garden, not a city** (Gen. 2:8): He lived in harmony with nature, without labor or invention. Food was given freely from trees—no need for agriculture, technology, or toil.
- **Naked, and not ashamed** (Gen. 2:25): There was no consciousness of vulnerability or status, no sense of competition, no hierarchy. Innocence meant resilience because man did not yet measure himself by knowledge.

---

## 2. The Intended State
- **Image and likeness of God** (Gen. 1:26–27): Humanity was meant to reflect divine attributes—creativity, dominion, relationality—*without corruption*. “Dominion” here was stewardship, not exploitation.
- **Life through the Tree of Life** (Gen. 2:9): Man was meant to eat from this tree, sustaining eternal life. His destiny was not fragility but continuity, living as a bridge between heaven and earth.
- **Priestly role**: Adam was to “tend and keep” (Gen. 2:15), the same words later used for priests tending the temple. Humanity’s role was custodial, mediating between creation and God.

---

## 3. The Fall: From “Man” to “Son of Man”
- By eating from the **Tree of Knowledge**, man exchanged **innocent stewardship** for **self-conscious mastery**.
- The result:  
  - *Fragility in labor*: “By the sweat of your face you shall eat bread” (Gen. 3:19).  
  - *Fragility in birth*: “In pain you shall bring forth children” (Gen. 3:16).  
  - *Fragility in mortality*: “For dust you are, and to dust you shall return” (Gen. 3:19).  
- This is the birth of the “son of man”—a creature of dust, bound by mortality, dependent on tools, labor, and ingenuity.

---

## 4. What Was Man Intended to Be?
- **Immortal steward**: Not dependent on fragile inventions, but on God’s Spirit.  
- **Unified being**: Not split by knowledge that divides good from evil, but whole in purpose.  
- **Priestly mediator**: Meant to sanctify creation, not exploit it.  
- **More than survival**: The intended man was not merely a biological specialist, but a divine image-bearer able to walk with God in the cool of the day (Gen. 3:8).  

---

So the real question becomes:  
Is the “son of man” state permanent, or is it a temporary exile? The prophets hint that restoration is possible—not through returning to innocence, but through transformation. Ezekiel speaks of a “new heart and new spirit” (Ezek. 36:26). Revelation speaks of access once again to the Tree of Life (Rev. 22:2).  

That suggests that man was intended not to remain fragile dust but to be **reborn into resilience**—a state beyond innocence, forged through experience, but restored to immortality.
