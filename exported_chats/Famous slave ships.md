Yes, there are several **famous slave ships** historically known for their voyages to what is now the United States. While the majority of enslaved Africans brought to North America arrived through indirect routes or were re-exported from the Caribbean, a few vessels stand out for their direct role, their historical documentation, or their notoriety:

---

### 1. **Clotilda (also called Clotilde)**
- **Notoriety**: Last known slave ship to bring enslaved Africans directly to the U.S.
- **Date**: 1860 (52 years after the transatlantic slave trade was outlawed by the U.S. in 1808)
- **Captain**: William Foster
- **Route**: From Dahomey (modern-day Benin) to Mobile, Alabama
- **Cargo**: Roughly 110 enslaved Africans
- **Historical Significance**:
  - The smuggling was illegal and conducted in secrecy.
  - Survivors founded the community of **Africatown**, near Mobile.
  - In 2019, the wreck of the *Clotilda* was identified in the Mobile River.

---

### 2. **La Amistad**
- **Not technically a slave ship**, but a coastal cargo schooner that became infamous after a revolt.
- **Date**: 1839
- **Voyage**: Captured Africans staged a mutiny off the coast of Cuba.
- **Outcome**: Ship was seized off the U.S. coast, leading to a landmark U.S. Supreme Court case.
- **Significance**:
  - The Africans won their freedom in court.
  - The case helped fuel abolitionist movements.

---

### 3. **The Wanderer**
- **Date**: 1858
- **Cargo**: Illegally smuggled 409 Africans to Jekyll Island, Georgia.
- **Original Purpose**: A luxury yacht converted into a slave ship.
- **Outcome**: Despite the illegal nature, no one was successfully prosecuted.
- **Significance**: One of the last confirmed illegal slave ships to reach the U.S.

---

### 4. **The Henrietta Marie**
- **Date**: Sank in 1700 off the coast of Florida.
- **Route**: Triangular trade shipâ€”brought slaves to the Caribbean, but also part of early slaving voyages to North America.
- **Significance**: Wreck discovered in 1972; a vital archaeological site with over 7,000 artifacts recovered.

---

### 5. **Brooks (or "Brookes")**
- **Not a U.S.-specific ship**, but crucial for its diagram.
- **Significance**: Famous British slave ship used in abolitionist propaganda.
- **Note**: The *Brooks diagram* was widely circulated in the U.S. and Britain to expose the inhumane conditions aboard slave vessels.
