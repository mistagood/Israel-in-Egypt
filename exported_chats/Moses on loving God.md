Here is the straight-forward answer drawn strictly from Moses’ own words.

There is only **one place** in the Torah where Moses explicitly states that **loving God with all the heart, soul, and might** *is itself the keeping of the Law*. Moses directly connects love to obedience, and obedience to love, treating them as one action.

### **Deuteronomy 6:4–6 — the core statement**
**Deuteronomy 6:4–6**  
*“Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God is one LORD.  
And thou shalt love the LORD thy God with all thine heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy might.  
And these words, which I command thee this day, shall be in thine heart.”*

Moses immediately binds love to commandment-keeping by saying that **the commandments must be in the heart**.  
This is the first layer.

### **Deuteronomy 11:1 — Moses defines love as obedience**
**Deuteronomy 11:1**  
*“Therefore thou shalt love the LORD thy God, and keep his charge, and his statutes, and his judgments, and his commandments, alway.”*

Here Moses does not give two instructions; he defines one through the other.  
**To love God = to keep His commandments.**

### **Deuteronomy 30:6–10 — Moses makes it explicit**
This is where Moses ties everything together with no ambiguity.

**Deuteronomy 30:6**  
*“And the LORD thy God will circumcise thine heart… to love the LORD thy God with all thine heart, and with all thy soul, that thou mayest live.”*

**Deuteronomy 30:8–10**  
*“And thou shalt return and obey the voice of the LORD…  
If thou shalt hearken unto the voice of the LORD thy God, to keep his commandments and his statutes… with all thine heart and with all thy soul.”*

Moses uses the same phrase—**“all thy heart and all thy soul”**—and directly applies it to **keeping the commandments**.

### **Synthesis — Moses’ own construction**
1. **Deut 6:5**: Love God with all your heart, soul, might.  
2. **Deut 6:6**: These commandments *must be* in your heart.  
3. **Deut 11:1**: Loving God *is* keeping His commandments.  
4. **Deut 30:6–10**: Keeping the commandments with “all your heart and all your soul” *is what Moses calls loving God*.

This is why Jesus, when asked the greatest commandment, simply quotes Moses. He isn’t inventing anything; He is pointing back to Moses’ definition that love of God and keeping the Law are not two separate acts but **the same act described from two sides**.

## 1. First, establish the standard: Moses’ test for a false prophet

### 1A. Deuteronomy 13 – Same God, different Law

**Deut 13:1–5** (summary – read it in full):

- If a prophet or dreamer:
  - gives a sign or wonder, **and it even comes to pass** (so the sign “works”),
  - **but says, “Let us go after other gods… and let us serve them,”**
- You **must not** listen.
- That prophet **must die**,  
  *“because he hath spoken to turn you away from the LORD your God, which brought you out of the land of Egypt…”* (Deut 13:5).

Key points:

- The sign **working** does not validate the prophet.
- The issue is: does he **turn you away** from the God who brought you out of Egypt and from **His commandments**?

Even if he still uses the name of Israel’s God, if his teaching **turns you from the covenant** given through Moses, he’s false.

### 1B. Deuteronomy 18 – Speaking in God’s Name what God did not command

**Deut 18:20–22**:

- A prophet who **presumes to speak** in God’s name what God **has not commanded** him to speak shall **die** (v. 20).
- Also, if he speaks in God’s name and the word **does not come to pass**, you shall **not be afraid of him** (v. 22).

So Moses’ standard boils down to:

1. Fidelity to **the covenant**, statutes, and judgments.  
2. No **self-authorized speech** in God’s name.
