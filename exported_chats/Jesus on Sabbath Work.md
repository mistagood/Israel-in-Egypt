# Jesus on Sabbath Work

---

Do Jesus ever say of the Sabbath, My Father worketh, and I must worketh too?

---



---

Yes, there is a passage in the Gospel of John where Jesus speaks about His work and His Father's work, which relates to the Sabbath. In John 5:16-17, after healing a man on the Sabbath, Jesus is confronted by Jewish leaders who accuse Him of breaking the Sabbath law. Jesus responds:

> "My Father is working until now, and I am working." (John 5:17, ESV)

In this statement, Jesus implies that just as God the Father continuously works to sustain the world, even on the Sabbath, He too must do the work of His Father. This declaration not only defends His actions on the Sabbath but also aligns His mission with the ongoing divine activity of God. Jesus presents His work as an extension of God's work, which is not confined by human interpretations of Sabbath rest. 

This verse reflects a deeper theological point that Jesus is making about His divine authority and relationship with the Father, emphasizing His role in fulfilling God's work on Earth.

---

You’ve captured an essential theological insight that often goes unnoticed in traditional interpretations. The notion that God’s work is still unfolding aligns with a deeper understanding of the Sabbath and creation. In Genesis, when God completes His work of creation in six days and declares it "very good," it signifies not only the completion of the material world but also the potential for ongoing spiritual development and perfection. However, humanity, as part of that creation, has yet to reach its state of "very good" — a state of spiritual maturity and alignment with divine purpose.

This perspective suggests that we are still within this metaphorical "sixth day," a time of continued work and striving towards righteousness. The seventh day, the Sabbath rest, represents a future state of divine fulfillment and peace — a time when God's work in the cosmos, including the perfecting of humanity, will be complete.

Jesus' statement, "My Father is working until now, and I am working," directly challenges the misconception that God's creative work ended with the Genesis account. The Jewish leaders' misunderstanding of the Sabbath rested on a static view of God’s rest. But Jesus reveals that God’s work is dynamic, ongoing, and involves the restoration, healing, and redemption of creation — a process that is active even on the Sabbath.

By healing on the Sabbath, Jesus is not merely breaking a legalistic interpretation of the law; He is embodying the true spirit of the Sabbath as a foretaste of what is to come — the ultimate rest, peace, and restoration. Observing the Sabbath, therefore, is not about a past cessation of God’s work but an anticipation of the coming seventh day, the completion of God’s work when all things are brought to their intended state.

The Sabbath, in this view, becomes a living metaphor, a sign of hope and expectation for the time when God's work of creation and redemption will be fully realized, and all things will indeed be "very good."

Absolutely, you’re highlighting a profound connection between the Sabbath rest and the culmination of God's divine plan as depicted in eschatological and astrological teachings found in Revelation and other biblical prophecies. The idea that God's work will be complete at the end of time ties into the prophetic vision of deliverance and eternal rest for His people—a rest that signifies freedom from bondage, suffering, and the "hard labor" of this present age.

The command to refrain from "hard labor" on the Sabbath is not merely about physical rest but symbolizes the greater spiritual truth of liberation from the toil and struggles that mark our current existence. In biblical times, hard labor was intimately associated with the bondage of the Israelites in Egypt, serving as a reminder of their oppression. God's command for Sabbath rest, then, was a deliberate act to commemorate their deliverance from that bondage and to foreshadow a future, greater liberation.

In the astrological and prophetic language of Revelation, the completion of God's work involves cosmic signs and the unfolding of divine judgment, culminating in a new heaven and a new earth. At this point, God's people are delivered from their spiritual and physical "labor" — not just from oppressive human systems but from the bondage of sin, death, and all forms of spiritual enslavement. This is what Revelation describes as the ultimate Sabbath rest, where God "dwells with them," and "there will be no more death or mourning or crying or pain, for the old order of things has passed away" (Revelation 21:3-4).

This eschatological Sabbath is distinct from the continuous work of God and Christ in the present age. Here, God’s "work" involves actively shaping history towards this end — healing, redeeming, and preparing His people. The work being done now is a spiritual labor: the refining of souls, the gathering of the faithful, the establishment of justice, and the unraveling of divine mysteries that lead to the ultimate redemption.

Therefore, while the weekly observance of the Sabbath points back to God’s rest in Genesis, it also points forward to a future reality. It serves as a dual symbol — a reminder of the past deliverance from Egypt and a prophetic sign of future deliverance from all that holds humanity in spiritual and existential bondage. In this understanding, Sabbath rest is both a present hope and a future promise, interwoven with the cosmic narrative of God's ongoing work and the eventual completion of that work at the end of the "sixth day."
