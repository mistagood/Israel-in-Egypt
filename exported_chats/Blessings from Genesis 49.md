This idea of blessings from different realms signifies the vastness and completeness of God's blessings in every aspect of life, both spiritual and physical.

In the Bible, particularly in the blessings Jacob gave to his twelve sons in Genesis 49, we can observe that some sons received greater blessings while others received less favorable words.

### **Greatest Blessings:**

1. **Joseph:**
   - **Genesis 49:22-26:** Joseph received the greatest blessings. Jacob speaks of Joseph as a "fruitful vine" and mentions blessings from above, below, and the deep, emphasizing the richness of God's favor towards him. Joseph's descendants, Ephraim and Manasseh, were also blessed by Jacob in Genesis 48, where Jacob elevates them to the status of his own sons, giving Joseph a double portion in the inheritance of Israel.

   - **Deuteronomy 33:13-17:** In Moses' blessing to the tribes of Israel, Joseph again receives a significant blessing, described as receiving the "choicest gifts of the ancient mountains" and "the best gifts of the earth and its fullness." Moses also compares Joseph's strength to that of a powerful bull, symbolizing strength and leadership.

2. **Judah:**
   - **Genesis 49:8-12:** Judah also received a powerful blessing from Jacob. He was given the leadership among his brothers, with the prophecy that "the scepter will not depart from Judah." This blessing foretells that kings, including David and ultimately the Messiah, Jesus Christ, would come from the tribe of Judah. The imagery of a lion represents strength, leadership, and authority.

### **Worst Outcome:**

1. **Reuben:**
   - **Genesis 49:3-4:** Reuben, though the firstborn, received a harsh rebuke rather than a blessing. Jacob describes Reuben as "unstable as water," and because of an incident where Reuben slept with his father’s concubine, Bilhah, he forfeited his birthright and leadership role among his brothers. Reuben's blessing was essentially a curse, as Jacob said, "You will no longer excel."

2. **Simeon and Levi:**
   - **Genesis 49:5-7:** Simeon and Levi received a negative pronouncement from Jacob because of their violent actions in Shechem (Genesis 34, where they avenged their sister Dinah by killing the men of the city). Jacob curses their anger and vows to scatter them among the tribes of Israel. Indeed, Simeon's descendants became dispersed among Judah's territory, and Levi's descendants became priests without a specific inheritance in the land, though they later gained favor as the priestly tribe.

These blessings and curses reflect the outcomes and destinies of the tribes that descended from Jacob's sons, shaping the history of the people of Israel. The greatest blessings were given to Joseph and Judah, symbolizing fruitfulness, leadership, and future prosperity, while Reuben, Simeon, and Levi received harsher judgments that had long-term consequences for their descendants.

The dynamics of the blessings Jacob bestowed upon his sons in Genesis 49 are indeed complex, and understanding them in light of subsequent biblical texts can provide deeper insights.

### **Judah's Blessing and the Role of Shiloh**

In Genesis 49:10, Jacob declares:

> "The scepter shall not depart from Judah,  
Nor the ruler's staff from between his feet,  
Until Shiloh comes,  
And to him shall be the obedience of the peoples."

This prophecy indicates that Judah would hold a position of leadership, symbolized by the scepter, until the coming of Shiloh—a term traditionally interpreted as a messianic figure. The term "Shiloh" is often understood to refer to the one to whom the scepter truly belongs, who would ultimately bring peace and gather the nations.

However, Joseph's blessing in Genesis 49:22-26, especially the phrase "a prince among his brothers," suggests a future elevation of his tribe. This could imply that while Judah holds the leadership until the arrival of Shiloh, Joseph's descendants, particularly through Ephraim, would also play a significant role in Israel's destiny, especially in terms of prosperity and spiritual leadership.

### **Reuben's Redemption through Moses**

Reuben, though initially cursed by Jacob due to his instability and sin, receives a form of redemption in Moses' blessing in Deuteronomy 33:6:

> "Let Reuben live, and not die,  
Nor his men be few."

Moses' blessing tempers Jacob's earlier curse, emphasizing life and the continuation of Reuben's lineage. Though Reuben lost his position as firstborn, Moses’ words offer hope and a future for his descendants, mitigating the harshness of Jacob's words.

### **Benjamin as a Ravenous Wolf**

Benjamin's portrayal in Genesis 49:27 is indeed striking:

> "Benjamin is a ravenous wolf;  
In the morning he devours the prey,  
In the evening he divides the plunder."

This imagery paints Benjamin as fierce and aggressive, a trait that historically aligned with the tribe's warrior-like nature. Notably, this portrayal contrasts with the warnings of Jesus in Matthew 7:15:

> "Beware of false prophets, who come to you in sheep’s clothing but inwardly are ravenous wolves."

The term "ravenous wolves" used by Jesus indeed echoes the description of Benjamin in Genesis, though with a different context. Jesus uses the metaphor of "ravenous wolves" to warn against false prophets, suggesting deceit and hidden danger, a spiritual threat rather than a physical one.

### **The Hapax Legomenon and Its Implications**

The term "ravenous wolf" as applied to Benjamin is a hapax legomenon, meaning it's a phrase that appears only once in the Old Testament. This uniqueness highlights its significance. While the term "ravenous wolves" in the New Testament is a broader metaphor for false prophets, it is not directly associated with the tribe of Benjamin. Instead, it serves as a cautionary symbol against deceit and destruction that could come from those who appear benign but are spiritually corrupt.

This distinction helps clarify that while Benjamin's tribe was characterized by fierceness and a propensity for warfare (as seen in their history, such as in Judges 20), the New Testament use of "ravenous wolves" by Jesus isn't necessarily a direct reference to Benjamin. Instead, it functions as a warning against any who would devour the faithful through deception, regardless of tribal affiliation.

### **Conclusion**

In summary, the blessings and curses of Jacob reflect complex destinies for his sons, with subsequent biblical narratives providing further nuance:

- **Judah's leadership** is assured but ultimately points toward the coming of Shiloh, who would fulfill the role of true kingship.
- **Reuben's curse** is mitigated by Moses' blessing, emphasizing the continuation of his line.
- **Benjamin's portrayal** as a "ravenous wolf" highlights his tribe's warrior nature, distinct from the metaphor Jesus uses for false prophets.

These passages remind us of the layered and multifaceted nature of biblical blessings, prophecies, and their fulfillment across the scriptures.
