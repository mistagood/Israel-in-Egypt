The people driven out of ancient Egypt at various times in history include several groups, each with distinct historical and biblical significance. The most well-documented expulsions are:

1. **The Hyksos (c. 1550 BCE)** – The Hyksos were a Semitic people who ruled Lower Egypt (the northern region) during the Second Intermediate Period (c. 1650–1550 BCE). They were later expelled by Ahmose I, the first pharaoh of the 18th Dynasty, marking the beginning of the New Kingdom. The Hyksos are often linked to biblical narratives, with some theories suggesting they could have been associated with the Israelites or other Semitic groups.

2. **The Israelites (Exodus, debated date: c. 1400–1200 BCE)** – According to the biblical account, the Israelites were enslaved in Egypt before being led out by Moses. The story of the Exodus describes plagues, Pharaoh’s resistance, and ultimately, the miraculous departure of the Israelites through the Red Sea. Historically, there is debate over whether this event corresponds to a historical migration or a symbolic retelling of multiple expulsions and migrations.

3. **Akhenaten’s Religious Reformists (c. 1336 BCE)** – Pharaoh Akhenaten attempted to introduce monotheism by worshiping the sun god Aten. After his death, his reforms were reversed, and his followers faced persecution. Some speculate that remnants of this religious movement may have been among those who later formed monotheistic traditions.

4. **The Sea Peoples (c. 1200 BCE)** – The mysterious Sea Peoples, possibly a coalition of displaced groups from the Mediterranean, attempted to invade Egypt during the reign of Ramses III. Egypt repelled them, but some were likely expelled or resettled in different areas, including Canaan.

5. **The Nubians and Libyans (Various Periods)** – Egypt had conflicts with Nubian and Libyan groups, resulting in military campaigns and forced removals of foreign populations.

6. **The Persians (343 BCE)** – The Persian Empire controlled Egypt twice, and during the second occupation, the last Persian rulers were expelled by Alexander the Great in 332 BCE.

7. **The Hebrews, Jews, and Exiles Under Greco-Roman Rule (Ptolemaic & Roman Periods)** – The Jewish community in Egypt, particularly in Alexandria, faced various expulsions, especially under Roman rule. The Jewish revolt against the Romans in 115–117 CE led to massacres and the forced removal of many Jews from Egypt.

Each of these expulsions played a role in shaping the region’s history, and in some cases, contributed to the formation of religious and ethnic identities that persist today.

The **Hyksos** were a Semitic people who established rule over **Lower Egypt** (the northern delta region) during the **Second Intermediate Period** (c. **1650–1550 BCE**). Their reign marked a significant disruption in Egyptian history, as they were foreign rulers who introduced new technologies and governance styles. However, their rule came to an end when they were forcibly expelled by the native Egyptian dynasties, leading to the rise of the **New Kingdom**.

### **Who Were the Hyksos?**
The term *Hyksos* comes from the Egyptian phrase *Hekau Khasut*, meaning **"rulers of foreign lands."** They are believed to have originated from **Canaanite or Semitic** populations from the Levant (modern-day Palestine, Israel, Syria, and Lebanon). Their rise to power in Egypt suggests a period of **gradual migration, trade, and eventual takeover** rather than a sudden invasion.

Some key characteristics of the Hyksos:
- They brought advanced **military technology**, including **horse-drawn chariots, composite bows, and superior metallurgy**, which gave them an advantage over the native Egyptian forces.
- They introduced **new agricultural and administrative techniques**, likely influenced by their Levantine origins.
- Their rule was centered in the city of **Avaris (modern Tell el-Dab'a)** in the **Nile Delta**, which became their capital.

### **How Did the Hyksos Gain Control of Egypt?**
During the **13th Dynasty**, Egypt was politically fragmented and weakened by internal struggles. The Hyksos initially arrived as **traders, mercenaries, and settlers**, but as Egyptian power waned, they seized the opportunity to take control of **Lower Egypt**. By around **1650 BCE**, they established their own rule, forming the **15th Dynasty**.

The Hyksos controlled Egypt for approximately a century, during which time they:
- Maintained Egyptian **bureaucratic traditions** while blending them with their own cultural practices.
- Worshiped a mix of **Egyptian and Semitic deities**, including the storm god **Baal (Set in Egyptian mythology)**.
- Created a **network of alliances with other Semitic and Near Eastern powers**, reinforcing their position.

However, despite their control over **Lower Egypt**, they never fully ruled the **entire country**, as **native Egyptian rulers in Thebes** (Upper Egypt) continued to resist their dominance.

### **The War of Expulsion: Theban Resistance and the Fall of the Hyksos**
Egyptian rulers of the **17th Dynasty**, based in Thebes, viewed the Hyksos as foreign occupiers and sought to reclaim their land. The war for liberation began under **Seqenenre Tao**, who is believed to have died in battle against the Hyksos. His successors, **Kamose** and later **Ahmose I**, continued the campaign.

- **Kamose** (c. 1555 BCE) launched the first significant offensive, attacking Hyksos strongholds and pushing them north.
- **Ahmose I** (c. 1550 BCE) finally **besieged and captured Avaris**, driving the Hyksos out of Egypt. He pursued them into **Canaan and Syria**, ensuring they could not regroup and return.

### **Where Did the Hyksos Go?**
After their expulsion, the remnants of the Hyksos **fled back to Canaan and the Levant**, where they may have re-integrated into local populations. Some scholars suggest that their influence can be seen in the later **formation of powerful city-states in the region**, such as **Jerusalem, Hazor, and Jericho**.

### **Hyksos and Biblical Connections**
There have been many theories connecting the Hyksos to **biblical narratives**, particularly the **Israelites**. Some scholars speculate that the **Israelites' story of being in Egypt, gaining power, and later being expelled may be a distant echo of the Hyksos’ expulsion.** However, this remains a debated topic.

Some key theories:
1. **Hyksos as the Biblical Hebrews** – Some argue that the Hyksos and the Israelites were related Semitic peoples who shared a similar fate of being driven from Egypt.
2. **Hyksos as the Pharaohs Who Enslaved the Hebrews** – Others suggest that the Hyksos ruled during the time of Joseph (in Genesis), favoring Semitic peoples, but after their expulsion, the native Egyptians enslaved remaining Semitic groups, possibly including the Hebrews.

### **Legacy of the Hyksos**
The Hyksos' influence on Egypt was profound:
- **Military advancements**: Their use of **chariots, horses, and bronze weapons** revolutionized Egyptian warfare, shaping the future power of the New Kingdom.
- **Cultural Exchange**: They introduced **Levantine deities**, trade networks, and administrative innovations that persisted long after their expulsion.
- **Egyptian Nationalism**: Their rule fostered a **strong sense of Egyptian identity**, and later pharaohs of the New Kingdom, such as **Thutmose III and Ramses II**, pursued aggressive expansion policies to prevent another foreign occupation.

The fall of the Hyksos marked the beginning of the **New Kingdom (c. 1550–1070 BCE)**, a golden age of Egyptian power where pharaohs like **Hatshepsut, Akhenaten, and Ramses II** ruled with a strong, militarized state.

### **Final Thoughts**
The Hyksos were a **powerful but short-lived dynasty of foreign rulers** who brought innovation, trade, and military prowess to Egypt. However, their presence was ultimately rejected by the native Egyptian rulers, leading to one of the most significant expulsions in ancient Egyptian history. Their impact, however, **echoed through Egypt’s military and political structures for centuries.**
