Osceola (circa 1804–1838) was a prominent Seminole leader and warrior known for his resistance against the United States during the Second Seminole War (1835–1842). Born as Billy Powell, he adopted the name Osceola, which means "black drink crier" in the Creek language, reflecting his role in leading his people against American encroachment.

Osceola's resistance was marked by his strategic acumen and fierce leadership. He was instrumental in several key battles, using guerrilla tactics and leveraging the Seminole's deep knowledge of the Florida swamps to their advantage. His leadership was pivotal in the Seminole efforts to resist removal from their lands, part of the broader Trail of Tears that forcibly relocated Native American tribes.

In 1837, Osceola was captured under a flag of truce, a controversial act that further fueled the Seminole resistance. Despite his imprisonment, his legacy lived on as a symbol of resistance and resilience. His influence on Seminole history and culture remains significant to this day.
