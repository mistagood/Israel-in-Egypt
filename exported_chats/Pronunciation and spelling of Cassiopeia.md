The correct spelling is **Cassiopeiae** — that’s the **genitive** (possessive) form of *Cassiopeia*, used in star names to mean “of Cassiopeia.”  

### Pronunciation
It’s pronounced:  
**/ka-see-oh-PEE-eye/**  
or phonetically:  
**cass-ee-oh-PEE-eye**

Here’s the breakdown:  
- **Cass** – like “cassette”  
- **ee-oh** – as in “Leo”  
- **PEE** – strong emphasis here  
- **eye** – rhymes with “sky”

So, when you see something like *Alpha Cassiopeiae*, it literally means “Alpha of Cassiopeia.”

so what does it mean

---

The name **Cassiopeia** comes from Greek mythology. It means **“she whose words excel”** or more loosely **“she of the fine voice.”**  

Etymologically, it likely combines:  
- **kassio-** (Κασσι-) — possibly from *kekasmai* (“to excel, shine, boast”),  
- **-opeia** (ὄψ / ὄψις) — meaning “voice,” “face,” or “appearance.”  

So the name can be read as **“she who excels in beauty or speech.”**

In myth, **Cassiopeia** was the vain queen of Aethiopia (yes, that’s the ancient spelling — not the modern nation but a broad region south of Egypt). She boasted that she or her daughter Andromeda was more beautiful than the Nereids, sea nymphs beloved of Poseidon. As punishment, Poseidon sent a sea monster, and later, the gods placed Cassiopeia among the stars — **upside down on her throne**, circling the pole as a warning against vanity.  

The constellation **Cassiopeia** thus literally means *“the queen (Cassiopeia) in the heavens.”*  

When you see **Cassiopeiae** in a star name (like *Alpha Cassiopeiae*), it means **“of Cassiopeia”** — “the brightest star of the constellation of the queen.”

You’re right to flag the mismatch. Two different things were getting blended:

1) **What the name “Cassiopeia” means (etymology).**  
- Greek: **Κασσιόπεια** (*Kassiópeia*).  
- Likely built on **-op(e)- / ὄψ** (“voice,” “face/appearance”) + a first element related (possibly) to **κέκασμαι** (*kekasmai*, “to excel, outshine, boast”).  
- Hence common scholarly glosses like **“she who excels (in) voice/appearance”** or **“she of excellent speech/beauty.”**  
- Important: this is **not certain**; many ancient proper names are partly opaque. But “excellent/outshining” + “voice/appearance” is the standard, cautious reading.

2) **What the constellation is (reference/identity).**  
- The figure in the sky is **the mythic queen Cassiopeia** placed among the stars.  
- Calling it “the **queen in the heavens**” describes **who** the constellation represents, **not** the lexical meaning of the name.

So:  
- **Literal name-meaning (probable):** “she who excels/outshines in voice/appearance.”  
- **Constellation identity (not a translation):** the queen (Cassiopeia) set in the sky.  

No contradiction—just etymology vs. depiction.
