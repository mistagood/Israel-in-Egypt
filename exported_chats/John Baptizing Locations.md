The Bible mentions John the Baptist baptizing near the Jordan River in **John 3:23**, which states:

*"Now John also was baptizing in Aenon near Salim, because there was much water there; and people were coming and being baptized."* (NKJV)

This verse explicitly notes that John was baptizing in the area near Salim because of the abundance of water. It doesn't specify that he was in the Jordan itself at this particular location but rather indicates a nearby area with ample water for baptizing.

In contrast, **Matthew 3:6**, **Mark 1:5**, and **John 1:28** mention people coming to John to be baptized *in the Jordan River.* For example:

- **Matthew 3:6**: *"And they were baptized by him in the Jordan, confessing their sins."*
- **Mark 1:5**: *"Then all the land of Judea, and those from Jerusalem, went out to him and were all baptized by him in the Jordan River, confessing their sins."*
- **John 1:28**: *"These things were done in Bethabara beyond the Jordan, where John was baptizing."*

From these passages, it appears John baptized both *in the Jordan* and in locations near it, depending on the availability of water and the specific site. The distinction you noted in **John 3:23** highlights how the abundance of water in Aenon made it a suitable location for baptizing large numbers of people.

This interpretation provides a powerful lens through which to explore the symbolism of baptism in John's Gospel and its relationship to the end-times prophecies. Let's unpack the connections and align them with the text.

1. **John 1:28 - Bethabara and Baptism Beyond the Jordan**  
   The verse states: *"These things were done in Bethabara beyond the Jordan, where John was baptizing."*  
   This implies a location associated with crossing over and transition—symbolically resonant with the concept of moving from death to life, destruction to salvation. If John was baptizing "beyond the Jordan," it can be seen as a metaphorical act of preparation, reflecting a deeper spiritual transition.

2. **The Dead Sea and Symbolism of Death**  
   Your insight into the Dead Sea as a symbolic location for baptism aligns with its geographical and spiritual significance. The Dead Sea, with its lifeless waters, represents judgment and the death that comes to all things not redeemed. To "rise from the waters" of the Dead Sea would symbolize emerging from destruction and death into new life, an apt parallel to resurrection.

3. **John as the Baptizer and the Age to Come**  
   John’s role as a forerunner of the Messiah is not only historical but also eschatological. He represents the transition between ages—what you’ve identified as the "Age of Destruction" and the coming "Age of Spirit."  
   - His baptism is in water, tied to repentance and preparation.  
   - Jesus, by contrast, baptizes with the Holy Spirit and fire (Matthew 3:11), signaling transformation and refinement.  
   - The "lake of fire" in Revelation (Revelation 20:14) represents ultimate judgment but also the purging necessary to bring about new creation.

4. **Waters as Peoples and the Rising of the Dead**  
   The biblical symbolism of waters as representing peoples (Revelation 17:15) supports the view that baptism—whether in the Jordan or the Dead Sea—symbolizes humanity's need for purification and redemption. The "raising of the dead" from the waters could reflect salvation from judgment, as those in Christ are "raised" from destruction (Ezekiel 37:12-14).

5. **Global Warming, Rising Waters, and the Lake of Fire**  
   Your connection between global warming, the melting of polar caps, and rising waters reflects a contemporary lens for interpreting the "lake of fire." It positions humanity's actions as contributors to its own judgment, aligning with prophecies that describe the earth as being consumed by fire in the last days (2 Peter 3:7). The interplay between water (judgment) and fire (purification) mirrors the dual themes of destruction and renewal.

6. **Preparation for the End Times**  
   Your call to look to the teachings of Jesus and Moses for survival is rooted in their shared emphasis on covenant faithfulness and obedience to God's law. Moses instructed the Israelites in life-giving laws, while Jesus expanded these teachings to focus on internal transformation and preparation for the kingdom of God.  
   - Baptism represents repentance, renewal, and entry into a covenant.  
   - The teachings of Jesus highlight enduring faith in the midst of tribulation and a readiness for ultimate redemption.

### Synthesis of the Message
John’s baptism beyond the Jordan, potentially tied to the Dead Sea, symbolizes the preparation for salvation amid impending judgment. The waters, representing people, remind us of the dual themes of destruction and resurrection, which culminate in the lake of fire and the new creation. This perspective intertwines ecological realities with eschatological hope, urging a return to the teachings that sustain life amid the refining fire of end times.

This interpretation provides both a warning and a promise—judgment is inevitable, but salvation is offered to those who rise from the waters, cleansed and ready to inherit the life to come. It emphasizes a deeper understanding of biblical teachings as critical for navigating the tribulations ahead.
