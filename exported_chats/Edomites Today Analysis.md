**The Edomite people**—descendants of Esau, Jacob’s twin brother—are a key mystery in biblical and historical analysis. In the Bible, Edom is portrayed as a nation southeast of ancient Israel, with its heartland in the territory south of the Dead Sea, encompassing the region called Mount Seir (Genesis 36:8; Obadiah 1:8-9). The Edomites are described as the “red people,” their name meaning “red,” echoing Esau’s nickname and the red stew he traded for his birthright (Genesis 25:30).

### What happened to the Edomites?
By the first century CE, the Edomites—known by then as **Idumaeans**—had been absorbed into the broader populations of the Near East, especially after being conquered by the Nabateans and then forcibly converted to Judaism by John Hyrcanus (Hasmonean ruler, c. 129 BCE). By the time of Jesus and the Second Temple period, Edomites/Idumaeans were considered Judean citizens. Herod the Great, the Roman-appointed “King of the Jews,” was an Idumaean by descent.

After the Roman destruction of Judea in 70 CE, the distinct identity of Edomites disappears from mainstream historical records. Most academic sources conclude that they were gradually assimilated into the surrounding peoples, losing their unique identity through intermarriage, conversion, and migration.

## **Who Wrote Second Esdras?**

Excellent—this is the heart of **textual history, canon formation, and transmission**. Let’s clarify exactly **how biblical and non-biblical Jewish writings from Ezra’s era and after the exile were preserved, translated, or lost**, and why some became globally spread while others faded or survived only in certain communities.

---

## 1. **The Era in Question: Ezra’s Time and After (Post-Exile, c. 500–400 BCE and Later)**

**Key context:**  
- **Ezra’s era** is after the Babylonian exile, during the Persian period, when the Second Temple was built and the foundations of “Judaism” as we know it were being laid.
- This is when the Torah and many other biblical books were being compiled, edited, and canonized.

---

## 2. **Preservation of the Hebrew Bible (Tanakh)**

- **Continuous Copying in Hebrew:**  
  The books that became the Hebrew Bible (Old Testament)—including Torah, Prophets, Writings—were copied, recopied, and standardized *within the Jewish community*, especially by priests and scribes.
- **Canonization:**  
  The process of deciding which books were “scripture” was **gradual** (most likely from Ezra’s time through 2nd century CE).  
  - **Ezra-Nehemiah** is *traditionally* credited with promoting Torah-reading and scripture preservation (Nehemiah 8:1-8).
- **Spread and Translation:**  
  - The **Septuagint** (LXX), a Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible, was made by and for Greek-speaking Jews in Egypt (3rd–2nd century BCE).  
    - Because of Christianity’s adoption of the Septuagint, **these texts spread throughout the Roman world** in translation.

---

## 3. **Why Did Some Books (like 2 Esdras/4 Ezra) Not Make the Cut?**

- **Canon Boundaries:**  
  - The books that became “canonical” were those **most widely accepted** and used in worship and teaching in the main Jewish communities of Judea and Babylonia.
  - **Ezra-Nehemiah** made it into the canon; **apocalyptic or pseudepigraphal books** (like 2 Esdras/4 Ezra, Enoch, Jubilees) were more marginal.
- **Rabbinic Judaism’s Priorities:**  
  - After the Second Temple’s destruction, rabbinic authorities focused on Torah, Prophets, Writings, and emerging Talmud—**apocalyptic literature was often sidelined**.
  - Books like 4 Ezra/2 Esdras may have seemed too radical, political, or speculative for the developing mainstream, especially as the Jewish community sought stability.
- **Preservation by Christians:**  
  - Early Christians were interested in apocalyptic texts (which they saw as foreshadowing Christ’s return), so they **translated, copied, and preserved** many works that Jews stopped copying.
  - **Thus, these works survived mostly in Greek, Latin, Ethiopic, etc.,** often in “Apocrypha” or “Pseudepigrapha” collections.

---

## 4. **Transmission Patterns: Canonical vs. Apocryphal**

| Type                    | Preservation Path    | Spread Worldwide?        | Example              |
|-------------------------|---------------------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| **Canon (Tanakh/OT)**   | Copied in Hebrew, later Greek (LXX), then other languages; adopted by both Jews and Christians | Yes—Septuagint, Vulgate, KJV, etc. | Genesis, Isaiah, Psalms |
| **Non-canon (2 Esdras, Enoch, etc.)** | Marginal in Judaism, but copied and translated by Christians (Greek, Latin, Ethiopic, Slavonic) | Often, but mostly in Christian circles | 2 Esdras, Enoch, Jubilees |

---

## 5. **Why the Difference?**

- **Community Value and Use:**  
  - Books used in public worship, law, and identity-building became central and were copied relentlessly.
  - Books valued more in mystical, apocalyptic, or sectarian circles often faded in Jewish copying but survived in Christian translation.
- **Historical Happenstance:**  
  - Some books simply got lucky—adopted by a major community, translated, and copied; others did not.
  - The process was *not* always systematic or deliberate exclusion—sometimes just a matter of local popularity or neglect.

---

## 6. **To Your Specific Questions:**

- **Did books like 2 Esdras slip through the cracks, or were they ever widely accepted?**
  - 2 Esdras (4 Ezra) was valued in some Jewish circles (e.g., cited in some Midrashim, used by some apocalyptic groups), but was not universally accepted.
  - It gained much broader survival thanks to **Christian interest and copying**.
- **Is there evidence of early Hebrew copies?**
  - Only fragments or indirect references—no full ancient Hebrew manuscript has survived, but this is true for many works outside the main canon.
- **Why do some books “go global” and others don’t?**
  - It’s a mix of **community acceptance, canon decisions, translation efforts, and historical luck**.

## **Summary**
- **Biblical (canonical) books** were preserved through continuous Hebrew copying and translation, becoming central to both Jewish and Christian traditions.
- **Non-canonical books** (like 2 Esdras) were less consistently preserved by Jews, but often picked up, translated, and spread by Christians.
- **Preservation and transmission depended on religious priorities, community decisions, and historical events**—not always on deliberate exclusion or loss of value.
