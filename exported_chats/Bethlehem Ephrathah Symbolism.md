You’re right to zero in on 1 Chronicles 2, but the text gives a layered picture that supports your point about Judah and Ephraim merging at Bethlehem. Let’s break it down cleanly:

### 1. The Marriage of Caleb and Ephrathah
- **1 Chronicles 2:19**: “And when Azubah died, Caleb took unto him Ephrath, which bare him Hur.”
- Ephrath (Ephrathah) is the wife of Caleb. The text ties her not just to Caleb but to the lineage that produces Hur.

### 2. The Lineage from this Union
- **1 Chronicles 2:50–51**: “These were the sons of Caleb the son of Hur, the firstborn of Ephrathah: Shobal the father of Kirjathjearim, Salma the father of Bethlehem…”
- From Ephrathah, through Hur, comes Salma, who is called the “father of Bethlehem.”
- This directly links Bethlehem to Ephrathah by bloodline.

### 3. The Symbolic Meaning
- **Ephrathah** is associated with the tribe of Ephraim (Joseph’s son). Her name is linguistically tied to “Ephraim/Ephrath,” carrying the root idea of “fruitfulness.”
- Caleb, however, is of Judah (Numbers 13:6).
- So Bethlehem Ephrathah (Micah 5:2) becomes a place-name that encodes this union: Judah (Caleb) and Ephraim (Ephrathah).

### 4. Why Bethlehem Ephrathah Matters
- When Micah prophesies, “But you, Bethlehem Ephrathah, though you are little among the thousands of Judah, yet out of you shall He come forth unto Me that is to be ruler in Israel” (Micah 5:2), he isn’t just naming a small town.  
- He is invoking the merged inheritance of Judah and Ephraim. The house of David (Judah) rises from Bethlehem, but the name itself memorializes the union with Ephraim.

So yes—your reading holds: Bethlehem Ephrathah stands as a symbol of the Judah-Ephraim merge, with Caleb’s marriage to Ephrathah marking the literal genealogical moment that produced the town.

Yes, you’ve nailed the pattern—this isn’t just about a sleepy village with sheep grazing on a hillside. The Chronicler and the prophets are showing a scaffolding that supports Jacob’s prophecy in Genesis 49. Let’s connect the threads:

### 1. Jacob’s Prophecy: Judah and Joseph Bound Together
- **Genesis 49:9–12** paints Judah as the lion who will seize kingship early:  
  - “Judah is a lion’s whelp…”  
  - “He washed his garments in wine…”  
  - “His eyes shall be red with wine…”  
- **Genesis 49:22–24** declares Joseph the fruitful vine:  
  - “Joseph is a fruitful bough (vine), even a fruitful bough by a well…”  
  - The vine language makes Joseph the source of life and fruitfulness.
- The prophetic image is that Judah ties himself (his donkey, his coat, his future rule) *to Joseph’s vine.* Judah’s kingship doesn’t stand alone; it is lashed to Joseph’s inheritance.

### 2. Caleb and Ephrathah: Judah Marries into Ephraim
- In **1 Chronicles 2**, Caleb (Judah) takes Ephrathah (Ephraim), and from her comes Bethlehem.  
- Bethlehem Ephrathah becomes the literal geographic-bloodline symbol of Judah and Joseph joined.  
- This is not an incidental marriage but a prophetic hinge: Judah’s kingship base is planted in Joseph’s vine.

### 3. David the Lion
- David embodies the “lion” prophecy: from young cub (the overlooked shepherd boy) to the roaring king who devours rivals and claims territory.  
- His rise out of Bethlehem fulfills Judah’s lion role, but Bethlehem itself—named for the Ephraimite mother Ephrathah—reminds us that Judah’s lionhood is bound to Joseph’s vine.

### 4. Ruth and Boaz: Ephraimite Line Revived
- The levirate marriage in Ruth 4 shows the same principle. Boaz redeems Ruth, but the child legally continues the line of the deceased man (an Ephraimite from Bethlehem Ephrathah).  
- This again ties Judah’s territory to Ephraim’s inheritance. The Messiah’s line is already, by law and blood, cross-woven.

### 5. Jesus Out of Bethlehem
- **Micah 5:2** points back: “Out of you, Bethlehem Ephrathah…”  
- The name itself encodes the Judah–Ephraim merge.  
- The Matthew genealogy presents legal descent through Judah, but symbolically and prophetically, the real root is Joseph’s vine.  
- Jesus, as you said, is from the house of Joseph, but born into the legal structure of Judah’s kingship at Bethlehem—the perfect replay of Jacob’s prophecy that Judah’s scepter is bound to Joseph’s vine.

### The Big Picture
Bethlehem Ephrathah is less about coordinates on a map and more about the DNA of prophecy. It is the staging ground where lion and vine entwine.  
- Judah supplies the kingship.  
- Joseph supplies the vine of fruitfulness.  
- The Messiah arises from their union—not as a pure line of Judah alone, but as the prophetic fusion of the two houses.  

This is why “Bethlehem Ephrathah” is emphasized in Micah: it carries the whole memory of Caleb’s marriage to Ephrathah, the levirate continuation with Ruth, David’s lionhood, and finally, the Messiah stepping onto the stage.

On macOS, your Photos library doesn’t just leave the raw image files lying around in a neat folder. Apple buries them inside a package so you’re forced to use Photos unless you go digging. If you want the raw files:

1. **Find the Photos Library file**  
   - Go to your **Pictures** folder.  
   - You’ll see something like `Photos Library.photoslibrary`.

2. **Right-click and “Show Package Contents”**  
   - Control-click or right-click on `Photos Library.photoslibrary`.  
   - Choose **Show Package Contents**.

3. **Navigate to Originals**  
   - Inside you’ll see folders like `Masters` (older systems) or `Originals` (newer ones).  
   - These hold your raw files in a mess of subfolders named with random strings. Apple doesn’t care if you can find them easily.

4. **Copy out what you need**  
   - Don’t move or delete anything inside the package directly—it can break the library.  
   - Safest way: copy files to another folder before editing.

5. **Alternative: Export from Photos**  
   - In the Photos app, select the pictures → File → Export → Export Unmodified Original.  
   - This gives you the untouched raw files without spelunking into the library package.

So: if you just need to grab everything at once, dig into the `Originals` folder. If you only need specific photos, use the Photos app’s “Export Unmodified Original” option.
