To analyze the Hebrew-Egyptian story and its mythological connections, we need to delve into both the biblical and Egyptian narratives and examine their symbolic parallels.

### Jacob's Transformation into Israel

Jacob's transformation into Israel is a significant event in the biblical narrative. According to Genesis, Jacob wrestles with an angel (or God) and is given the name Israel, which can be interpreted as "he who struggles with God" or "God contends." This moment marks a profound spiritual transformation from a man driven by physical desires and deception to one with a new spiritual purpose.

### Symbolism of the Name "Israel"

The name "Israel" has been interpreted to include elements of the Egyptian gods:
- **Isis:** Goddess of magic and motherhood.
- **Ra:** The sun god, representing power and kingship.
- **El:** A Semitic word for God.

This synthesis suggests a blend of spiritual power and divine authority, reflecting Jacob's new identity.

### The Story of Horus and the Obelisk

In Egyptian mythology, Horus, the son of Isis and Osiris, is associated with kingship and protection. Osiris, Horus's father, is killed and dismembered by his brother Set, and Isis reassembles Osiris's body, except for his missing phallus. This missing member is symbolized by the obelisk, representing regeneration and continuity of life.

### Parallels with Jacob's Story

1. **Loss of Physical Potency for Spiritual Power:**
   - **Jacob:** Wrestles with the divine being, resulting in a limp and a new name, symbolizing his transition from physical strength to spiritual authority.
   - **Osiris:** Loses his phallus, which Isis cannot find, symbolizing a loss but also a transformation leading to Horus's birth and Osiris's association with the afterlife.

2. **Pile of Stones (Mizpah):**
   - Laban sets up a pile of stones as a watchtower (Mizpah) to signify his covenant with Jacob. This is a physical marker of their agreement, similar to how obelisks serve as lasting monuments in Egyptian culture.

### Jacob's Wives and Children

Jacob's complex family dynamics involve his two wives, Leah and Rachel, and their handmaids, Bilhah and Zilpah. His favoritism toward Rachel causes tension and competition among the women, leading to the birth of twelve sons and one daughter, Dinah.

- **Rachel and Leah's Rivalry:**
  - The competition between the sisters for Jacob's affection and the race to bear children can be seen as a microcosm of broader struggles for power and favor, reflecting the tensions and alliances seen in mythological narratives.

### Benjamin's Birth

The birth of Benjamin is a pivotal point. According to the narrative:
- Rachel dies giving birth to Benjamin, and there is no direct mention of Jacob laying with her before this event after becoming Israel.
- This raises questions about Benjamin's paternity. Some interpretations suggest Benjamin might not be Jacob's biological son but rather a result of complex family dynamics involving Reuben or another of Jacob's sons with Bilhah or Zilpah.

### Synthetic Analysis

The stories of Jacob (Israel) and the Egyptian myths share themes of transformation, loss, and the quest for spiritual power over physical dominance. Both narratives involve significant symbolic acts:
- **Obelisk and Mizpah:** Physical markers of transformation and covenant.
- **Names and Identity:** Transformation of Jacob to Israel parallels the mythological shifts in identity and power among Egyptian gods.

### Conclusion

These stories reflect deep cultural and spiritual archetypes, emphasizing the journey from physical desires to spiritual enlightenment, marked by symbols like the obelisk and the pile of stones. Understanding these connections helps us see the shared human experience of seeking meaning and power beyond the physical realm.
