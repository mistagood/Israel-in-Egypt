In the Mazzaroth framework, the watery quadrant—home to D’li (Aquarius/Reuben), Cassiopeia, and Andromeda—forms a narrative of emotional rescue, repentance, and restoration.  

Reuben, as D’li (דְּלִי), is “the water drawer,” from *dalah*, “to draw up water.” This directly recalls Genesis 49:4, where Jacob calls him *“unstable as water.”* Reuben’s defining act was to rescue Joseph from death by drawing him up from the pit—he was the first to “raise from the waters,” a kind of proto-baptizer. His constellation sits beside the great celestial river systems (Eridanus, Cetus’ sea, and Pisces’ stream), the very region mythic Greeks saw as the domain of Poseidon’s trials and salvations.

Cassiopeia, the boastful queen chained to her throne, stands as the mirror of Reuben’s pride and regret. In myth she offends the sea-gods by exalting her beauty above the Nereids and must watch her daughter Andromeda bound to a rock as sacrifice to a sea monster. In Israel’s story this echoes Reuben’s overreach—“he went up to his father’s couch” (Genesis 35:22)—a violation born of impulse that brought judgment on his house. Cassiopeia’s inverted throne, visible as the W-shaped constellation rotating around Polaris, evokes that fall: the queen “overturned,” yet still visible in repentance, chained but not destroyed. She is the sorrow of Reuben’s line, his longing to reclaim honor.

Andromeda, chained to the rock beside the sea and later freed by Perseus, reflects the second half of Reuben’s arc: redemption and deliverance from the consequence of passion. She is “drawn up” (*dalah* again) from peril, mirroring Reuben’s attempt to draw Joseph from the pit. Perseus, striking down Cetus (the sea monster), plays the saving counterpart Reuben could not fully become—an image of the messianic rescuer completing what the firstborn began. Thus the Andromeda–Cetus–Perseus tableau turns the story into a cosmic moral drama: water, guilt, repentance, and rescue intertwined.

So within this watery quadrant of the heavens we read:
- **D’li (Aquarius/Reuben)** — the repentant rescuer, unstable yet life-giving;
- **Cassiopeia** — the proud yet sorrowful mother figure, bound to repentance;
- **Andromeda** — the captive daughter, the rescued remnant;
- **Perseus** — the redeeming deliverer, the restoration of what Reuben sought.

Together they compose a celestial reflection of Genesis 49’s verdict and hope: the instability of water transfigured into the act of drawing up life from its depths. The quadrant tells the old story twice—once in the book of the sky, once in the book of Genesis—each reminding that instability can be turned into salvation when the waters are rightly drawn.

This Greek-language Jewish romance (probably 2nd century BCE – 1st century CE) elaborates Asenath’s conversion story. While it doesn’t name Dinah explicitly, later rabbis identified its heroine with the Dinah-born child from the Midrash above, merging the strands.

**3. Rabbinic purpose.**  
The genealogy solves an ethical problem: how could Joseph, a Hebrew of strict covenant, marry an Egyptian priest’s daughter?  The Midrash turns her into an adopted Israelite, preserving tribal purity.  Thematically it reunites Leah’s line (Dinah) with Rachel’s through Joseph—your “recombination of the houses.”

So your usage is historically grounded in **Jewish Midrash and early folklore**, not a modern reconstruction.  
In your framework that means Asenath (the Dinah-child) embodies the *bridge of captivity*: born from violation, raised in Egypt, yet restoring covenantal blood when she joins Joseph.  It’s exactly the kind of mythic recursion you’re tracing through Cassiopeia and Andromeda—the estranged daughter who becomes the key to reunification.
