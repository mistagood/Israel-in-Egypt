---

Yes, several ancient cultures held the rabbit or hare in sacred or symbolic regard, often associated with fertility, rebirth, and the moon. While few directly **worshipped** the rabbit as a *god*, many mythologies and cultic systems revered it symbolically, and some personified rabbit deities or spiritual beings.

Here are a few notable examples:

---

### **1. Egyptian Mythology – Unut (Wenet)**
- **Who**: Unut, also called Wenet, was an Egyptian goddess whose totem animal was the hare.
- **Cult Center**: Worshipped in the 15th Upper Egyptian nome, especially Hermopolis.
- **Symbolism**: Associated with speed, fertility, and the underworld. She was later absorbed into the cult of Horus or Thoth.
- **Significance**: The hare was considered a messenger between worlds — a psychopomp — likely because of its connection to the moon and cycles.

---

### **2. Chinese Mythology – Moon Rabbit (Yutu)**
- **Who**: The Moon Rabbit or Jade Rabbit (*Yùtù*) lives on the moon and is a companion to the moon goddess Chang'e.
- **Role**: He mixes the elixir of immortality or pounds medicine in a mortar.
- **Symbolism**: Immortality, lunar cycles, enlightenment.
- **Worship**: Not worshipped directly, but celebrated during moon festivals (Mid-Autumn Festival) and symbolic in Taoist and Buddhist traditions.

---

### **3. Mesoamerican Mythology – Aztec and Mayan Moon Rabbit**
- **Aztec**: In Aztec legend, Quetzalcoatl once threw a rabbit into the moon, creating the image of a rabbit's silhouette there.
- **Maya**: The moon goddess Ix Chel was often depicted with a rabbit.
- **Symbolism**: Fertility, the lunar cycle, creation stories.
- **Spiritual Role**: Rabbits were seen as sacred and lunar beings, with their likeness etched in celestial mythology.

---

### **4. Celtic Mythology – Eostre and the Hare**
- **Eostre/Ostara**: A Germanic/Celtic spring goddess associated with renewal, fertility, and dawn.
- **Animal**: The hare (not a rabbit per se, but closely related) was sacred to her.
- **Festival**: Ostara's celebration in spring involved symbols of hares and eggs—precursors to Easter traditions.
- **Symbolism**: Fertility, rebirth, springtime.

---

### **5. Native American Beliefs – Great Hare (Nanabozho / Mahtigwess)**
- **Algonquian Tribes**: Nanabozho is a cultural hero and trickster figure, often portrayed as a giant hare.
- **Role**: Creator, teacher, shapeshifter, and spiritual guide.
- **Spiritual Status**: Though not worshipped as a "god," he is revered in oral traditions, akin to Coyote in other tribes.

---

### **6. Buddhist Symbolism – The Self-Sacrificing Rabbit**
- **Story**: In Jataka tales (stories of Buddha's past lives), the Buddha was once a rabbit who sacrificed himself in a fire to feed a hungry traveler (actually a god in disguise).
- **Result**: The god honored the rabbit by placing his image on the moon.
- **Moral**: Compassion, selflessness, and dharma.
- **Cultural Legacy**: Popular in East and Southeast Asia, influencing moon festivals and art.

---

### Summary:
While direct **rabbit worship cults** were rare, **the rabbit/hare held sacred significance** across multiple ancient cultures:
- **Moon** and **fertility** symbolism dominate.
- In some, they are **divine messengers**, **sacrificial figures**, or even **cosmic creators**.
- Their **reverence**, especially in **lunar and springtime rituals**, suggests a quiet but persistent spiritual importance.
