The concept of fornication in the Bible is complex, and different terms were used in the original Hebrew and Greek texts, each with nuanced meanings.

1. **Hebrew Bible (Old Testament)**: In the Torah and other Hebrew scriptures, words such as **זָנָה** (*zanah*) are used. This word broadly refers to unfaithfulness, often metaphorically describing Israel's relationship with God, rather than just sexual immorality. *Zanah* can imply prostitution, idolatry, and spiritual adultery, illustrating a breakdown of covenant faithfulness.

   - **Leviticus 18 and 20**: These chapters address various forms of sexual immorality, though the word "fornication" itself doesn’t appear in translation. The behaviors condemned here include adultery, incest, and relations with other forbidden partners, often tied to maintaining purity within the community and covenant.

   - **Ezekiel 16 and 23**: The prophets, particularly Ezekiel, use *zanah* to metaphorically criticize Israel’s spiritual infidelity through alliances with other nations and idolatry, which was viewed as “fornicating” with foreign gods.

2. **Greek New Testament**: The Greek word **πορνεία** (*porneia*) is frequently used for "fornication." *Porneia* refers broadly to sexual immorality and includes adultery, prostitution, and various forms of sexual behavior that were considered outside the sanctity of marriage. It appears in several contexts:

   - **1 Corinthians 6:18**: Paul speaks directly to avoiding *porneia*, emphasizing that it is a sin against one's body, a temple of the Holy Spirit.
   
   - **Matthew 5:32 and 19:9**: Jesus uses *porneia* when he talks about grounds for divorce, which some interpret as unfaithfulness or sexual immorality that breaks the marital bond.

3. **The Cultural and Linguistic Context**: *Zanah* and *porneia* reflect a range of meanings beyond individual acts. In the Hebrew scriptures, "fornication" often symbolizes Israel's betrayal of the covenant with God, as unfaithfulness in a marital sense mirrors spiritual unfaithfulness. In the Greek New Testament, *porneia* becomes more expansive, relating to all forms of sexual immorality within Greco-Roman cultural contexts where promiscuity was common and accepted.

This exploration suggests that "fornication" in the Bible goes beyond just physical acts and extends to a metaphorical level, especially in Hebrew thought, where it is often linked with idolatry and betrayal of covenant relationships. In the Greek context, it more explicitly includes a broader category of sexual immorality, reflecting a fusion of cultural views on moral purity.

Your inquiry touches on a key point: whether the concept of *fornication* as we understand it today — specifically premarital sex or sexual relations outside of marriage — exists explicitly in the Bible, or if it’s more of a modern interpretation applied to ancient texts.

1. **The Hebrew Bible and Moses’ Law**: You’re right that the Hebrew Bible doesn’t directly address premarital sex in the way we might expect by today’s standards. There are specific laws concerning sexual relations, but they tend to focus on adultery (extramarital sex involving a married person), incest, and cases where an unmarried woman is taken, especially regarding issues of honor and familial rights.

   - **Exodus 22:16-17** and **Deuteronomy 22:28-29**: These passages address what happens if a man has relations with an unmarried woman (sometimes translated as “seduces” or “violates” her), generally requiring him to marry her or pay her family compensation. This suggests a concern with protecting familial honor and stability rather than a strict prohibition of premarital sex.

2. **Greek New Testament - *Porneia***: By the time of the New Testament, *porneia* becomes a more commonly used term. However, *porneia* does not precisely align with our modern definition of "fornication" as premarital sex. Instead, it broadly encompasses what was seen as any sexual activity outside the bounds of acceptable social and religious standards, including adultery, incest, and prostitution. 

   - In **1 Corinthians 7**, Paul advises that if one cannot exercise self-control, they should marry rather than “burn with passion.” But Paul’s writings don’t present a clear command against premarital sex; rather, they reflect an encouragement toward marriage as a form of moral guidance, given the perceived risks of *porneia*.

3. **Interpretive Differences - Modern vs. Ancient Context**: The modern idea that any sex outside marriage is inherently sinful (as “fornication”) reflects centuries of evolving interpretation, influenced heavily by church teachings and societal shifts, especially in the medieval and early modern periods. The Bible itself lacks a clear directive banning all premarital sex. Instead, it addresses sexual ethics in terms of protecting marriage covenants, family lineage, and communal harmony.

4. **Translation Choices and Cultural Shifts**: The English translation “fornication” was chosen based on the translators' cultural context, imposing a broad interpretation on ancient terms like *porneia* (Greek) or *zanah* (Hebrew). By translating these as “fornication,” which in English has come to mean any premarital sexual act, translators may have inadvertently imposed a more rigid rule than the original texts indicate.

In summary, there’s no clear biblical mandate that singles out all premarital sex as a moral transgression in the sense we consider it today. Ancient laws and texts were primarily concerned with social consequences — protecting family honor, preventing exploitation, and maintaining purity within marriage. Modern interpretations of *fornication* as “premarital sex” likely reflect later cultural and theological shifts, rather than an original biblical standard.
