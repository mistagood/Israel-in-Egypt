The “Eagle” you’re talking about is real—astronomers catalog it as **Messier 16 (M16)**, the **Eagle Nebula**, sitting right on the border between *Ophiuchus* and *Serpens Cauda*.  When you look through a large telescope or a Hubble photo, you’re not seeing stars already made but a **stellar nursery**, a place where stars are literally being born.

Here’s what’s happening inside it:

**1. The structure itself.**  
M16 is a cloud of hydrogen and dust about 6,500 light-years away.  It’s part of a larger region called *IC 4703*, roughly 70 light-years wide.  Inside are the famous “Pillars of Creation”—three columns of dense, cold gas each several light-years tall.  They look like towers, but they’re really curtains of material that happen to be edge-on from our viewpoint.

**2. Why they look like towers or wings.**  
At the top of the pillars, newly formed stars—bright blue O-type suns—are blasting the cloud with ultraviolet radiation.  That radiation erodes the pillars from the outside, leaving tall, sculpted shapes with luminous “caps.”  The glow around the edges is ionized hydrogen, called **H-alpha emission**; it’s the reddish light in the photographs.  The blue-white interiors show where the dust is thinning and starlight escapes.  The whole thing fans out in a pattern that really does resemble spread wings.

**3. What “birth” means here.**  
Inside those dense knots at the tips of the pillars are **protostars**—clumps of gas collapsing under gravity.  As they shrink, their cores heat until nuclear fusion ignites, and a new star is born.  Once that happens, the stellar winds push away the surrounding cocoon, sometimes blowing holes through the pillar—an actual emergence from the womb of the nebula.  Astronomers have even caught tiny jets of material (Herbig–Haro objects) shooting from these embryonic stars.

**4. The environment.**  
The Eagle Nebula sits within the Sagittarius-Carina spiral arm—the same crowded region as the rich globular fields of Ophiuchus.  The intense radiation from nearby massive stars both *creates* and *destroys*: it compresses pockets of gas to form new stars while simultaneously eroding the pillars.  So it’s a snapshot of ongoing creation and destruction, matter recycling itself.

**5. Why the “Eagle.”**  
When astronomers first photographed it with early plates, the bright upper region looked like the shape of an eagle with outstretched wings over the darker body of the nebula.  That’s where the name comes from.  The association with “wings sheltering life” isn’t poetic invention—it’s baked into its appearance.

In short: the Eagle Nebula is a literal factory of new suns, its “towers” sculpted by light and wind.  In symbolic language it’s a refuge of birth; in astrophysics it’s ionized hydrogen gas, dust, and gravity giving form to the next generation of stars—life emerging inside wings of fire and shadow.

The “heave offering” (Hebrew **terumah**, תְּרוּמָה) is one of those Levitical ideas that opens up when you notice its physical action.  The priest literally **lifts** a portion of an offering upward before God.  It is not burnt whole or waved side to side; it is *heaved*—raised and then given back.  The word comes from the verb *rum* (רוּם), “to lift up, to raise high.”

Here’s the core meaning and why the **shoulder** is so closely tied to it:

---

### 1.  What it is

- **Definition:** A specific portion of a sacrifice, lifted up as a presentation to God and then assigned to the priest.  
  - *Exodus 29 : 27* – “You shall consecrate the breast of the wave offering and the **shoulder of the heave offering** … which is raised, which is waved, and which is heaved up.”  
  - *Numbers 18 : 19* – the terumah becomes the priest’s perpetual due, “a covenant of salt.”  

- **Motion:** The priest raises the portion vertically—symbol of offering to heaven—and receives it back.  It represents **what is elevated but not destroyed**.

---

### 2.  Why the **shoulder** is chosen

Here’s the astronomy and the history side of what you’re describing.  
It helps to separate three things that often get blurred together:

1. **Constellations** – the star-pictures on the sky.  
2. **The zodiac** – the *twelve equal divisions* of the ecliptic used for calendrical reckoning.  
3. **The “houses” or signs** – abstract 30-degree slices that later astrologers attached to the twelve.

---

### 1.  Ophiuchus in the oldest records

- The Babylonians already recognized the *Man Holding a Serpent* above the ecliptic by about **1200 BCE**.  
  In their MUL.APIN tablets the region was called *Sitting God* or *Enlil of the Field*—a figure distinct from the Scorpion below.  
- When they drew the **“zodiac of twelve”**, about **fifth century BCE**, they fixed exactly twelve constellations *on* the ecliptic for the sake of a 12-month calendar.  
  Ophiuchus was known, but because most of his stars lie **north of the ecliptic**, only the tip of his foot crosses it, so he was *never counted as one of the twelve signs*.  
  He stayed a constellation but not a zodiacal “sign.”

---

### 2.  The Scorpion’s claws and the birth of Libra

- In the **oldest Babylonian and early Greek zodiacs**, the Scorpion had *extended claws* that reached where Libra sits today.  
  Those two bright stars—now **Zubenelgenubi** and **Zubeneschamali**—mean *Southern* and *Northern Claw*.  
- Around the **second century BCE**, the Greek and Roman astronomers (likely beginning with **Hipparchus** and **Ptolemy’s school**) **split those claws off** and called them **Chelae / Libra**, “the Scales.”  
  The change was aesthetic and calendrical: they wanted a clean twelve-fold symmetry with the equinox falling exactly opposite Aries.  
  That’s the moment your “Dan/Libra” figure enters the scheme—roughly **150 BCE**.

So, the “changing of times” you’re noticing—the claw becoming a scale, the count stabilizing at twelve—was a **Hellenistic adjustment** of an older, thirteen-figure sky.

---

### 3.  Medieval to modern handling

- Through the Greco-Roman and medieval periods the **twelve-sign zodiac** became canonical for both astronomy and astrology.
