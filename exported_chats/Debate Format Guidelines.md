## üìå PURPOSE OF DEBATE

1. **Clarify Competing Interpretations**  
   To bring forward different viewpoints on biblical, historical, or cultural topics, allowing audiences to hear well-reasoned positions.

2. **Restore Order to Controversial Discourse**  
   Provide a controlled forum for discussing ideas that are typically suppressed, misrepresented, or scattered.

3. **Educate, Not Just Entertain**  
   Debates should illuminate the text or history in question, not just be verbal combat. Clarity and evidence win, not theatrics.

4. **Create a Historical Record**  
   Debates contribute to a public archive of competing views, helping researchers, students, and the public see where lines of disagreement lie.

| **Phase**                      | **Time**    | **Purpose**                                                                 |
|-------------------------------|-------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Opening Statements            | 5 mins each | Frame the issue, introduce key terms, and lay out the thesis.              |

## üìú FORMAT RULES

### 1. **Topic Must Be a Statement, Not a Question**
   - Example: *‚ÄúPaul is the false prophet warned about in Deuteronomy 13.‚Äù*
   - Both sides take either **affirmative** or **negative** stance.

### 2. **Use of Sources**
   - Must cite directly: chapter and verse for scripture, or title/date for history.
   - No paraphrasing without clear sourcing.

### 3. **Definition of Terms**
   - Both sides must agree to definitions before debate begins (e.g., ‚Äúfalse prophet,‚Äù ‚ÄúEphraimite,‚Äù ‚Äúatonement‚Äù).

### 4. **On-topic Only**
   - No appeals to emotion, ad hominem, or unrelated doctrines.
   - Stick to the proposition, no introducing wild tangents.

### 5. **One Interruption Rule (Optional)**
   - Each side allowed **1 interjection** per round (used wisely).

## üß± MODERATION

A **moderator's job** is to:

- Keep time
- Enforce rules (e.g., no interruptions, off-topic digressions)
- Prompt clarification if terms are not defined
- Maintain neutrality

# üèõÔ∏è What Is a Debate?

A **debate** is a structured form of argumentation where two or more parties discuss opposing viewpoints on a clearly defined topic, called a **resolution** or **motion**. The goal is not necessarily to "win" by force of personality, but to **present reasoned, evidence-based arguments** while **respecting a system of rules**.

---

# üéØ General Purpose of Debate

1. **Clarification of Ideas**  
   To distinguish and examine competing interpretations or positions on a subject.

2. **Critical Thinking**  
   Forces participants to anticipate counter-arguments, refine logic, and build coherence.

3. **Public Discourse & Education**  
   Allows observers to engage with complex topics through structured exchange.

4. **Rhetorical Skill-Building**  
   Trains speakers in persuasion, clarity, and use of language.

---

# üìú Core Components of a Debate

| Term                 | Definition |
|----------------------|------------|
| **Resolution / Motion** | The proposition being debated (e.g., "X is true") |
| **Affirmative (Pro)** | The side that supports the resolution |
| **Negative (Con)**    | The side that opposes the resolution |
| **Rebuttal**          | A direct response to the opposing side‚Äôs argument |
| **Cross-examination**| Direct questioning of the opponent‚Äôs claims |
| **Judge / Moderator**| Enforces rules, evaluates arguments, and maintains order |

---

# üß± General Structure (Format-Agnostic)

1. **Opening Statements** (each side presents their case)
2. **Rebuttals** (direct response to opponent‚Äôs arguments)
3. **Cross-Examination / Questions**
4. **Closing Statements** (summary and final persuasive appeal)

# üï∞ Common Formats and Time Allotments

| Format               | Notable For | Structure |

# ‚öñÔ∏è Universal Rules of Debate

These rules apply across virtually all formal and informal debates:

### 1. **Clash**
   - Each side must directly **engage with the opposing arguments**. Ignoring counterpoints weakens credibility.

### 2. **Burden of Proof**
   - The **affirmative** side must prove the resolution.
   - The **negative** must show flaws in that proof or offer a better alternative.

### 3. **No Fallacies**
   - Avoid:
     - **Ad hominem** (attacking the person)
     - **Straw man** (misrepresenting the argument)
     - **Appeal to emotion** without evidence
     - **Non sequitur** (logic gaps)

### 4. **Respect Speaking Order**
   - Only speak during your allotted time or with permission (e.g., "point of information").

### 5. **No New Arguments in Closing**
   - Final speeches may summarize but **cannot introduce new claims**.

### 6. **Cite Evidence Clearly**
   - Use verifiable sources, scripture, or logic. Clearly indicate when quoting.

### 7. **Stay On Topic**
   - Arguments must stay relevant to the resolution and not drift.

| Category             | Description |
|----------------------|-------------|
| **Logic & Structure** | Clear flow of argument; no contradictions |
| **Evidence & Support**| Use of sources, texts, data, citations |
| **Responsiveness**    | Addressing opponent‚Äôs points directly |
| **Delivery**          | Clarity, tone, pacing‚Äînot volume or aggression |
| **Persuasiveness**    | Did the speaker actually persuade? |

# üéì Best Practices

- **Define key terms early**
- **Anticipate counterarguments**
- **Keep emotions in check**
- **Speak to persuade, not just to inform**
- **Practice ‚Äústeel-manning‚Äù**: represent the opponent‚Äôs view as strongly as possible before responding

---

# üõë Disqualifying Violations (Typically)

- Personal insults or attacks
- Intentionally misrepresenting opponent's position
- Disruption of opponent‚Äôs time
- Falsification of sources or data
