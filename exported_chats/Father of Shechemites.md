# Asenath’s Origins: Was She Dinah’s Daughter Adopted into Egyptian Nobility?

**Introduction:**  
The biblical figure **Asenath**, wife of Joseph, has long intrigued commentators because she is introduced as an Egyptian, the daughter of Potiphera (priest of On)【13†L90-L98】. This raises the question of how Joseph – a Hebrew patriarch – married into Egyptian nobility, and who Asenath really was. A popular **theory in Jewish tradition** claims that Asenath was actually the daughter of Dinah (Jacob’s daughter) by Shechem, **adopted** by an Egyptian family. This theory attempts to reconcile Joseph’s marriage with family lineage and religious continuity. *Below, we investigate this idea through biblical genealogy, rabbinic exegesis, cultural context, and historical evidence.*【42†embed_image】 *A 15th-century depiction of Joseph meeting Asenath, reflecting later interest in her origins.* 

## Biblical Genealogies and Clues in Genesis  
The Bible itself never explicitly says Asenath was Dinah’s child. She is only mentioned three times, always as “*Asenath daughter of Potiphera, priest of On*” (Genesis 41:45, 41:50, 46:20)【13†L90-L99】. However, **Genesis 46:15** has a curious detail in Jacob’s genealogy that later interpreters scrutinized. It lists the children and grandchildren of Leah who went to Egypt, including *“Dinah his (Jacob’s) daughter,”* and concludes: *“All the souls of his sons and his daughters were thirty-three”* (Genesis 46:15). The number 33 is puzzling, since Leah’s offspring listed seem to total 32 unless an additional female is counted. Some readers propose that the text implies **Dinah had a child** who is counted among the family【7†L296-L302】. One theory is that this extra person could be **Dinah’s daughter** – potentially Asenath – included in the tally of Jacob’s household【7†L296-L302】. While this is not explicit in the Masoretic text (which may simply be using a formulaic plural or counting other unnamed descendants), it opened the door for later exegetes to imagine Dinah’s offspring playing a role in the story.

Another biblical hint connecting Joseph’s family to Dinah’s story is the prominence of **Shechem** in Joseph’s narrative. Shechem was the name of the prince who assaulted Dinah (Genesis 34:2) and of the city he ruled. Interestingly, Jacob later gives Joseph a tract of land at Shechem: “I give you one portion (*shechem* in Hebrew)… which I took from the Amorite” (Genesis 48:22). Many understand this to mean the city/area of **Shechem** was assigned to Joseph’s heirs【31†L2-L7】. Fittingly, when the Israelites returned to Canaan, Joseph’s bones were buried at Shechem on the land Jacob had purchased there【19†L23-L29】. Moreover, in the tribal genealogies, **Manasseh** (Joseph’s firstborn) had a descendant clan called the **Shechemites** – literally named after “Shechem” (Numbers 26:31)【18†L31-L38】. This suggests that the memory of Shechem remained attached to Joseph’s lineage. While these links can be explained as geographical (Shechem became part of Joseph’s territory), proponents of the Dinah-Asenath theory see them as subtle clues: Joseph’s sons (Manasseh and Ephraim) might carry blood ties to the city of Shechem through their mother, if Asenath were the daughter of Shechem and Dinah.

## Rabbinic Traditions Identifying Asenath as Dinah’s Daughter  
Later **rabbinic and midrashic sources** explicitly develop the idea that Asenath was Dinah’s child, born from the incident with Shechem and secretly adopted into Egypt. This legend appears to have arisen to resolve the moral and religious concern of Joseph – a righteous man – marrying an Egyptian pagan woman. As one scholar notes, the biblical author himself does not comment on Asenath’s background, but “later Jewish writers, unlike the biblical author, were troubled” by Joseph marrying a foreign idolater, given that she would be ancestress of two Israelite tribes【3†L263-L267】【21†L1-L4】. In response, the midrash recasts Asenath’s origins in a more Israelite light.

One early source for this tradition is **Targum Pseudo-Jonathan** (an Aramaic paraphrase to the Torah, ca. 7th–8th century CE). In its rendition of Genesis 41:45, the Targum inserts a striking explanation: *“And [Pharaoh] gave him Asenath, **whom Dinah bore to Shechem**, and [who was] brought up by Potiphera’s wife, to be his wife.”*【12†L179-L187】. In other words, Asenath is identified as the daughter born to Dinah after her rape by Shechem, and she was raised in Egypt by the wife of Potiphera (the same Potiphera named in Genesis as her adoptive father). The **Targum** even repeats this note in later verses (Gen. 46:20, 48:9), underlining that Joseph’s sons were born of Asenath “daughter of Dinah” rather than a random Egyptian【12†L179-L187】. 

Medieval midrashic compilations fill in the narrative details. **Pirkei de-Rabbi Eliezer (Chapter 38)** and other midrashim relate that after the brothers Simeon and Levi slew the men of Shechem, Dinah was found to be pregnant with Shechem’s child. She later gave birth to a baby girl, **Asenath**. Not wishing to keep this child (perhaps due to the stigma of the rape), *“Jacob made her a medal (amulet) upon which he wrote the events [of Shechem] and put it around the baby’s neck”* before sending her away【10†L140-L148】. According to this legend, the angel **Michael** transported the infant Asenath to Egypt, where he placed her at the doorstep of Potiphar (Potiphera). Potiphar’s wife, being childless, discovered the abandoned baby. Noticing the inscribed gold amulet, they realized *“this child is the daughter of great ones”* (of noble lineage), and **adopted** her as their own【3†L264-L271】【10†L140-L148】. Asenath thus grew up as an Egyptian aristocrat, the supposed daughter of Potiphera, but in reality she was Jacob’s granddaughter. Years later, when Pharaoh arranged her marriage to Joseph, Joseph was said to have recognized her true heritage – some versions say he recognized the Hebrew writing on her amulet – and happily accepted her as a suitable wife【10†L142-L149】.

A variant of the story is preserved in **Midrash Aggadah** (12th century), which elaborates that Jacob himself, upon returning from the tragedy at Shechem, wrote down the story on a gold plate and hung it around baby Asenath’s neck. *“When Dinah gave birth to Asenath, he put the plate on her neck and set her down by the wall of Egypt.”* Potiphar (identified here with Potiphera) then found the infant by the city wall: *“He heard the sound of a crying newborn… He saw the plate [with] the incidents [written on it]. Potiphar said… ‘This child is the daughter of prominent people!’… and because he raised her, she was called his daughter.”*【29†L288-L297】【29†L297-L302】. In this version, no angel is mentioned – it is a more naturalistic tale of abandonment and rescue – but the core idea is the same: Asenath is Dinah’s hidden child, adopted into an Egyptian household.

These midrashic accounts also connect to other rabbinic interpretations. The **Talmud (Sotah 13b)** notes that Potiphar (Joseph’s former master) is likely the same person as Potiphera, and that he had been emasculated (literally “castrated by the angel” as a punishment in one legend)【12†L185-L192】. This detail, echoed by Rashi and others, explains why Potiphera might have had no offspring of his own – making it plausible that he would adopt a foundling as his daughter. Thus, all the pieces fit: Potiphera’s **barrenness**, an abandoned Hebrew baby of good lineage, and Joseph’s need for a wife who would be acceptable both to Pharaoh and to the patriarchal family traditions.

It should be noted that not *all* rabbinic commentary follows the Asenath-as-Dinah’s-daughter story. **Genesis Rabbah 80:11** records a different outcome for Dinah’s child: in that midrash, Dinah’s rape resulted in the birth of a son, *not* a daughter. That son is identified with **Shaul**, who is mentioned in Genesis 46:10 as the “son of a Canaanite woman” in Simeon’s genealogy【26†L13-L16】. The Rabbis explain that “Canaanite woman” refers to Dinah (since she had been impregnated by the Canaanite prince Shechem), and that Jacob’s son Simeon later married Dinah and raised Shaul as his own【26†L13-L16】. In this tradition, Dinah’s child stayed within Jacob’s clan (as Simeon’s stepson), rather than being sent to Egypt. **Medieval commentators** were aware of both versions – one giving Dinah a son (Shaul) and one giving her a daughter (Asenath)【30†L173-L179】. The “Asenath as Dinah’s daughter” motif, however, gained popularity because it elegantly solved the problem of Joseph marrying out of the family. It allowed later Jewish readers to view **Ephraim and Manasseh**, Joseph’s sons, as fully children of Israel (even maternally), and not the offspring of an idolatrous union.

## Marriage Between Hebrews and Egyptians in Joseph’s Time  
Why was it important to later interpreters that Joseph’s wife be of Hebrew origin? Part of the answer lies in the cultural and religious **restrictions on intermarriage** in biblical thought. In Joseph’s era (second millennium BCE), there was no written Torah law yet forbidding marriage to Egyptians, but there were strong cultural boundaries. The patriarchs, for example, preferred endogamy: Abraham didn’t want Isaac to marry a Canaanite, and Rebekah later lamented Esau’s Hittite wives. Hebrews saw themselves as a distinct covenant family, and marrying into the idolatrous nations was viewed negatively (later codified in the Torah laws of Moses – e.g. Deuteronomy 7:3-4, which, post-Sinai, prohibited intermarriage with Canaanites, and Deut. 23:8-9 which restricted marrying Egyptians for a few generations). Conversely, **Egyptians** had their own prejudice against Hebrews and other Asiatics. The book of Genesis notes that Egyptians segregated themselves from Hebrews at meals, considering them unclean (“Egyptians could not eat with the Hebrews, for that is an abomination to the Egyptians” – Genesis 43:32). Egyptian high society was very class-conscious and ethnocentric. As one commentary observes, *“Proud Egyptians did not care for Hebrews. In order that Joseph’s ethnic past be erased as quickly as possible, Pharaoh gave him an Egyptian name and married him into a prominent Egyptian family.”*【16†L111-L115】. In other words, Pharaoh arranged Joseph’s marriage to Asenath as a political and cultural strategy: by allying Joseph with an Egyptian aristocrat (the daughter of a high priest), Joseph would become fully naturalized in Egypt, easing any ethnic tensions【16†L111-L115】.

From this perspective, if Asenath had actually been a Hebrew by birth, it would have been **kept secret**. Joseph’s rise to power happened under Egyptian authority, so publicly his wife had to be seen as Egyptian nobility. But within the narrative logic, later readers found it fitting that God might have arranged events so that Joseph still married within Abraham’s lineage (unknowingly to the Egyptians). The **adoption theory** satisfies both sides: outwardly, Joseph weds an Egyptian princess, but inwardly (in the eyes of providence) he marries the daughter of Jacob’s own daughter. This would uphold family purity and the covenant line. Rabbinic legend even says that when Joseph discovered Asenath’s true identity (through the amulet or through prophetic insight), he recognized her as kin and thus acceptable【10†L142-L149】. 

It’s worth noting that other solutions existed in Jewish tradition to justify Joseph’s marriage. Some suggested that Asenath and her family converted to Joseph’s faith (essentially portraying her as a sincere **convert** to monotheism)【30†L167-L175】. For instance, the ancient **Hellenistic novel** *Joseph and Aseneth* (written ~1st century BCE/CE) took this approach: it tells how Asenath, initially an idol-worshipping Egyptian maiden, encounters Joseph, repents of idolatry, and fully converts to worship the God of Israel before marrying him【8†L43-L49】【13†L117-L125】. This story stresses Asenath’s piety and has her dramatically renounce Egyptian gods, thus removing any taint from Israel’s family. Early Jewish commentators like *Philo of Alexandria* also seem to have assumed Asenath converted. The **midrashic adoption theory**, by contrast, goes a step further – asserting she was Israelite by blood from the start – thereby sidestepping the interfaith marriage issue altogether. Both approaches (conversion or clandestine Israelite identity) reflect the **strong aversion to marrying pagans** in the Jewish interpretive tradition.

## Adoption and Integration in Ancient Egypt – A Historical Perspective  
How plausible is the idea of a **foreign child being adopted into Egyptian nobility**? While we cannot confirm the Asenath story historically, there are some anthropological insights from ancient Egypt that make the scenario conceivable. **Adoption** was indeed practiced in Egypt, though not as commonly or formally as in Rome or later societies. It often occurred when a couple was childless or needed an heir. Notably, Egyptian priests sometimes adopted children to carry on temple duties if they had no sons of their own【15†L33-L37】. In such cases, the adopted child would be given the status and inheritance of the adoptive family. Potiphera, being a priest of On (Heliopolis), could plausibly adopt a baby if he had no natural offspring – this aligns with the legend’s detail that Potiphar’s wife was barren【10†L140-L148】. 

**Integration of foreigners** into Egyptian society was limited but not unheard of. The Egyptian worldview drew a sharp line between “Egyptians” and “foreigners,” often portraying foreigners as inferior or dangerous. Yet, historical evidence shows that Egyptians did engage in **political alliances, trade, immigration, and intermarriage** with other peoples【17†L47-L55】. For example, Pharaohs of the New Kingdom married foreign princesses for diplomacy, and in the Middle Kingdom there are records of Asiatic (Canaanite/Semitic) people living in the Delta, some serving in the Egyptian army or government. Scholars note a “dichotomy” between the Egyptian ideal of purity and the reality of a diverse population in some periods【17†L47-L55】. The **Joseph story** itself reflects this: a Hebrew rises to the highest office and is given an Egyptian wife, showing that Pharaoh could integrate a capable foreigner at least at the elite level.

For an adoption like Asenath’s, one can point to the general practice of **exposed infants** being rescued and raised in foreign courts – a motif not entirely foreign to the ancient world. (The story of Moses, who was a Hebrew baby adopted by Pharaoh’s daughter, is a later biblical example of a child crossing ethnic lines into an elite Egyptian household.) Although Asenath’s case is the reverse – an Israelite girl adopted by an Egyptian man – it is not implausible in a time when lineage could be obscured and personal merit or divine providence could elevate individuals. If an infant of unknown origin were found by a powerful Egyptian and thought to be of noble birth (as the midrash suggests by the golden plate story), there is little to prevent her from being raised as his own and eventually married into high society. **No Egyptian law forbade adopting a foreign-born child**, especially if the child was assumed or presented to be Egyptian. Thus, from a historical-anthropological standpoint, the adoption theory is not impossible. However, it remains in the realm of legend – we have no Egyptian record of such an adoption in Joseph’s case, and the Bible itself is silent about Asenath’s upbringing aside from naming her parents. The historical value of this theory is in illustrating how cultural integration might have occurred on an individual level, rather than proving it did in this specific instance.

## Shechem’s Legacy in Joseph’s Lineage  
One intriguing facet of this theory is how it ties together the **legacy of Shechem** with Joseph’s descendants. Shechem was a Canaanite city with a complicated role in Jacob’s family history: it was the place of Dinah’s trauma and her brothers’ vengeance, but also a place that Jacob later owned and blessed to Joseph (Genesis 33:19, 48:22). If Asenath was born of Dinah and Shechem, then Joseph’s sons carry the blood of that city’s prince (Shechem son of Hamor) on their mother’s side. In effect, the tribes of Ephraim and Manasseh would descend from Jacob *and* from the native line of Shechem. Some scholars have pondered whether this could symbolically strengthen Joseph’s tribe’s claim to the region of Shechem. Indeed, when the land of Canaan was divided among the tribes, the area of Shechem fell to **Ephraim** and Manasseh – Joseph’s tribes – and became a key city in the northern kingdom of Israel. Joshua 24:32 explicitly notes that the field at Shechem, which Jacob had purchased long ago, **“became the inheritance of Joseph’s descendants.”**【19†L23-L29】 This completes a narrative circle: Shechem, the site of Dinah’s defilement, becomes a heritage for the offspring of Joseph and (potentially) Dinah’s daughter. 

Furthermore, the naming patterns in Manasseh’s genealogy are suggestive. In Numbers 26, the clans of Manasseh include one called **Shechem (the Shechemites)**【18†L31-L38】. According to the text, this Shechem was a grandson of Manasseh (a son of Gilead). It is certainly possible that this name honors the memory of the city of Shechem which the family inherited. But if one accepts the adoption theory, it could also be seen as honoring an ancestor – the original **Shechem son of Hamor**, who was (in that theory) the maternal grandfather of Manasseh. In other words, Joseph’s son Manasseh might have named a son “Shechem,” perpetuating the name within the family line that had descended from him. This is conjectural, but it shows how the pieces of tradition can interlock: the name of Shechem lives on in Joseph’s lineage in a way it does not in any other tribe, perhaps reflecting that Joseph’s marriage brought the **house of Jacob and the city of Shechem** together again.

## Implications for the Tribes of Joseph (Ephraim and Manasseh)  
If Asenath truly were Dinah’s daughter, the **tribal implications** are thought-provoking. Joseph’s two sons, Manasseh and Ephraim, received full status as tribes of Israel (Jacob blessed them and said “Ephraim and Manasseh shall be mine, like Reuben and Simeon” – Genesis 48:5-6). They effectively inherited the birthright portion, each becoming a tribe that emerged from Joseph. These tribes would then have an unusual distinction: their paternal grandfather was Jacob (Israel) and **maternal great-grandfather was also Jacob**, since their mother Asenath would be Jacob’s granddaughter by Dinah. This means Ephraim and Manasseh would have had three out of four grandparents from Jacob’s family (Joseph’s parents were Jacob and Rachel; Asenath’s parents would be Dinah and Shechem, with Dinah being Jacob’s daughter). In terms of Israelite identity, this would make Ephraim and Manasseh *even more closely tied* to the core family of Israel than a reader of Genesis might initially think. It blends the lines of Jacob’s two principal wives’ families as well: Joseph was the son of Rachel, and Asenath (as Dinah’s child) would bring in the line of Leah. Thus, the tribes of Ephraim and Manasseh symbolically unite Rachel’s and Leah’s offspring. Some later Jewish writings even remark that “Joseph’s mate was born in his own household,” seeing divine providence in the idea that Asenath was destined for Joseph from the start【1†L51-L54】. This concept reinforces the unity and purity of the twelve tribes’ origins.

Territorially, as discussed, the theory underscores the idea that Joseph’s tribes had a rightful claim to Shechem and perhaps a mission to transform that former center of idolatry and violence into part of Israel’s holy inheritance. Ephraim and Manasseh went on to be influential tribes – Ephraim in particular became the leading tribe of the Northern Kingdom (often the whole kingdom of Israel is poetically called “Ephraim”). Knowing that their ancestress might have been born from Dinah’s tragedy adds a layer of redemptive narrative: the disgrace at Shechem is redeemed by folding that story into the lineage of Israel’s chosen tribes. It also might explain why Jacob felt moved to give Joseph the extra “portion” at Shechem – not only because Joseph was his beloved son, but perhaps, in the logic of the legend, because Joseph had married the offspring of that city. Of course, these implications are homiletical in nature. Historically or textually, the **tribes of Joseph** were accepted as Israelite without question regardless of Asenath’s origin, since tribal identity followed the father (and Jacob himself claimed Ephraim and Manasseh as his). But for interpreters who love to find symmetrical patterns in Genesis, the Dinah-Asenath connection provides a satisfying completion to the tribal tableau.

## Conclusion  
The idea that Asenath was Dinah’s daughter adopted into Egypt is a fascinating interplay of **exegesis, legend, and cultural context**. On one hand, it is well-supported in **rabbinic tradition**: Targum Pseudo-Jonathan, Pirkei de-Rabbi Eliezer, Midrash Aggadah, and other sources explicitly endorse it, motivated by a desire to keep Israel’s family line spiritually “untainted” and to solve narrative curiosities (like the count of Jacob’s family or Joseph’s unusual marriage)【12†L179-L187】【29†L288-L297】. These traditions demonstrate the creative ways ancient Jewish scholars filled in biblical gaps – effectively saying that God had orchestrated events so that Joseph’s Egyptian bride was in fact of his own kin. The theory finds support in subtle **biblical clues** (the number of Leah’s descendants being 33, the prominence of Shechem in Joseph’s inheritance) and aligns with known **Egyptian practices** (adoption by a childless couple of high status, assimilation of a foreign-born individual who is raised as Egyptian)【15†L33-L37】【17†L47-L55】. It enriches the story of Joseph by weaving it more tightly with the earlier story of Dinah, turning what was a tale of sorrow and shame into one of eventual reconciliation and honor within the family. 

On the other hand, this theory can be **challenged from a plain reading** perspective. The Bible gives no hint that Asenath is anything but Egyptian by birth; Genesis calls her the daughter of an Egyptian priest without elaboration【13†L90-L98】. The midrashic account is a later embellishment, reflecting theological concerns of post-biblical readers rather than historical memory. Modern scholarly approaches tend to view the Asenath-as-Dinah’s-daughter story as an *exegetical legend* – a way for later Judaism to make sense of a puzzling marriage, rather than a literal fact. They point out that another ancient midrash (Genesis Rabbah) contradicts it by assigning Dinah a son instead【30†L175-L179】. In terms of history, while adoption and intermarriage in Egypt are possible, we have no concrete evidence that Joseph’s wife was anything other than an **assimilated Egyptian** who likely adopted Joseph’s faith (as implied by her raising of devout sons). 

Ultimately, the theory’s value is in what it illustrates about Israel’s self-understanding: it emphasizes the **continuity of the covenant family**, the idea that even in exile in Egypt, the lineage of the patriarchs remained intact and guided by God. It also highlights the **broader theme of integration** – Joseph integrated into Egypt, and in the midrash, his wife integrated into Israel. Whether one takes the story as literal truth or as allegory, it adds depth to the Joseph narrative. Asenath, viewed through this lens, becomes a figure of unity between peoples: the bridge between Jacob’s house and the Gentile world. Her story (as Dinah’s lost daughter or as a converting Egyptian) underscores the biblical motif that **Israel’s destiny can be advanced in mysterious ways**, turning even painful incidents into blessings.
