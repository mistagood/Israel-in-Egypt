If a non-profit organization **loses its tax-exempt status**, what happens to its **assets** depends on several key factors ‚Äî most importantly, the **reason for the loss** and how the organization chooses to proceed afterward.

Here‚Äôs a breakdown:

---

## üßæ 1. **Loss of Tax-Exempt Status ‚â† Dissolution**

If a nonprofit **loses its 501(c)(3)** or similar federal tax-exempt status **but continues operations**, the assets typically remain **with the organization**. However:

- It **must now pay taxes** on income (federal and potentially state).
- It can no longer **accept tax-deductible donations**.
- It **must not use assets** in ways that violate its original exempt purposes unless it changes its corporate structure.

### ‚úÖ The nonprofit can:
- Continue operating as a **for-profit** corporation (if allowed by state law),
- Or **regain tax-exempt status** by applying again and correcting issues.

---

## ‚ö†Ô∏è 2. **Dissolution of the Nonprofit**

If the nonprofit **chooses to dissolve** ‚Äî whether because of the loss of status or other reasons ‚Äî then **IRS and state laws** regulate where the assets go:

### ‚û§ IRS Rule (for 501(c)(3)):
> **Assets must be distributed to another 501(c)(3)** or a **governmental entity** for a public purpose.  
> Assets **cannot** go to a private individual or be divided among founders or board members.

### ‚û§ Typical Dissolution Steps:
- Pay off debts and liabilities.
- Distribute remaining assets to qualified tax-exempt organizations.
- File appropriate dissolution paperwork with state and IRS (including final Form 990).

---

## üö´ 3. **What They *Can‚Äôt* Do with the Assets**

- **Cannot** convert assets into personal gain or distribute them to insiders (self-dealing).
- **Cannot** distribute assets to shareholders (because nonprofits don‚Äôt have owners).
- Doing so would violate both **state nonprofit laws** and **federal rules**, and could result in **penalties, lawsuits**, or **IRS enforcement**.

---

## üîÅ Example Scenarios

| Scenario | What Happens to Assets |
|---------|------------------------|
| Nonprofit continues as taxable entity | Keeps assets, but now subject to income tax |
| Nonprofit regains exempt status | Keeps assets and returns to tax-exempt operations |
| Nonprofit dissolves | Must give assets to other 501(c)(3)s or public institutions |
| Nonprofit misappropriates assets | Legal and financial penalties; IRS/state enforcement |

  - **Real estate** may now be subject to **property tax** (varies by state and locality).
  - **Income from operations** is subject to **federal and state income tax**.
  - If it **sells property**, it may owe **capital gains taxes** on the appreciated value.
  - Donations received **are not tax-deductible** to the donor anymore.

This means the nonprofit **still owns its property**, but is now treated like a **for-profit business** in the eyes of the IRS and local tax authorities **unless it legally reorganizes**.

---

## üîÑ 2. **How to Transition from Nonprofit to For-Profit**

You **can‚Äôt simply ‚Äúbecome‚Äù a for-profit** ‚Äî the process requires **legal conversion or asset transfer**. Here's how:

### ‚úÖ **Option A: Create a New For-Profit Entity & Transfer Assets**
1. **Form a new for-profit company** (e.g., LLC, S-Corp, C-Corp).
2. **Apply for EIN** and register with state authorities.
3. **Request approval** from the nonprofit‚Äôs board and (if required) attorney general/state charity bureau.
4. **Transfer assets** *at fair market value* to the for-profit:
   - This may trigger **capital gains tax** or require payment to the nonprofit.
   - Proceeds must go to **charitable purposes** if the nonprofit dissolves.
5. **Dissolve the nonprofit** (if no longer needed) and **file final Form 990**.

This is the most **legally clean** option.

---

### ‚úÖ **Option B: State-Law Conversion (if allowed)**
Some states allow direct **conversion** of a nonprofit into a for-profit entity:

- You must amend the **articles of incorporation**, changing the **purpose clause** and tax classification.
- Must **pay taxes** on **net assets** (treated as a distribution).
- Must still **dispose of charitable assets** for public benefit ‚Äî can't just keep them.

‚ö†Ô∏è **Note:** This is heavily regulated. Many states and the IRS will require **valuation of all assets**, and the assets may need to be **sold off** or **transferred to another nonprofit** before converting.

---

## üìå Key Legal Constraints
- **Charitable assets** are considered a **public trust**. They can‚Äôt be taken by private owners even after status is lost.
- You **cannot privatize** the nonprofit‚Äôs assets without approval from the **IRS** and possibly the **state attorney general**.
- Violations can result in **penalties**, **lawsuits**, or **revocation of licenses**.

---

## üßæ Taxes After Conversion
Once it‚Äôs a for-profit:

- Must file **corporate income tax returns** (Form 1120, or 1120-S for S-Corps).
- Must pay **employment taxes**, **property tax**, and **sales tax** (if applicable).
- **Real estate** that was previously tax-exempt may now be assessed by the county.

In **California**, transitioning a nonprofit to a for-profit is **highly regulated** and must comply with both **state law (via the California Attorney General‚Äôs Office)** and **federal IRS rules**.

Below is a **step-by-step guide** tailored for California, explaining what happens to the assets, how to transition legally, and what tax and compliance consequences to expect.

---

## ‚öñÔ∏è 1. What Happens to Nonprofit Assets in California

Even if the nonprofit **loses its tax-exempt status**, **California law still treats its assets as held in charitable trust**. That means:

- The assets **cannot be used for private gain**.
- Even if the nonprofit continues to operate, its **charitable assets** must remain dedicated to a charitable purpose **unless the Attorney General approves otherwise**.
- If converting or dissolving, **all assets must be transferred** to another **California-registered 501(c)(3)** or used in a way that complies with the organization‚Äôs original purpose.

---

## üîÑ 2. How to Transition to a For-Profit in California

### ‚úÖ Option A: **Dissolve the Nonprofit and Start a New For-Profit**

**This is the cleanest and most legally secure route.**

#### Steps:
1. **Form a new for-profit entity** with the California Secretary of State (LLC or Corporation).
2. **Obtain an EIN** for the new entity.
3. **Get nonprofit board approval** to dissolve and transfer any assets.
4. **File a Dissolution Packet** with:
   - **Form DISS NP** (Certificate of Dissolution)
   - **Form FTB 3555** (California Franchise Tax Board notice)
   - Final **Form 990** with the IRS
   - **Form RRF-1 (charitable registry form)** with the CA Attorney General, marking it as final
5. **Notify the California Attorney General** (Registry of Charitable Trusts) at least **20 days in advance** of any asset transfer.
6. **Transfer assets to another 501(c)(3)** (not to the new for-profit).

> ‚ö†Ô∏è You **cannot** transfer assets from the nonprofit to the new for-profit directly, unless **you purchase them at fair market value** and the proceeds go to another charitable purpose.

---

### ‚úÖ Option B: Convert the Nonprofit to a For-Profit Corporation

**California does allow conversions,** but they are extremely rare and require **Attorney General approval.**

#### Key Requirements:
- **File Articles of Incorporation ‚Äì Conversion** (Form CONV-1) with the California Secretary of State.
- Provide a **valuation of assets**, a plan for how charitable assets will be used or transferred.
- Get **Attorney General approval** via a **notice of proposed transaction**.
- All **charitable assets must be transferred** to another 501(c)(3), or sold to the new entity **at fair market value** (and the money still goes to charity).

> **You still cannot privately benefit from those assets** without the state‚Äôs express permission. Even if status is revoked, assets are still bound to public trust.

---

## üí∞ 3. Taxes & Financial Impact

### After Conversion or Loss of Status:
- Income becomes **fully taxable** under California Corporate Tax Law (Form 100).
- Must pay **property taxes** unless property qualifies for another exemption (e.g. religious, educational, etc.).
- Subject to **sales/use taxes** and **employment taxes** like any business.
- Cannot receive **tax-deductible donations**.

Yes ‚Äî **a nonprofit can donate to a church you start**, **but only under strict conditions**, and the key question is **not ownership**, but whether the **donation serves the original nonprofit‚Äôs exempt (charitable) purpose** and **avoids private benefit or self-dealing**.

Here‚Äôs a precise breakdown:

---

## ‚úÖ 1. **It‚Äôs Legal *If* It Serves a Charitable Purpose**

Your nonprofit **may donate to another 501(c)(3)** organization ‚Äî including a **church you start** ‚Äî **as long as**:

- The donation supports the original nonprofit‚Äôs **exempt mission**.
- The church is **properly organized** and operating **exclusively for religious or charitable purposes**.
- There is **no private benefit** to you as an individual (even if you‚Äôre the founder or pastor).
- The transaction is **properly disclosed and documented** in your nonprofit‚Äôs filings and board minutes.

---

## üö´ 2. **But Beware: Conflict of Interest and Self-Dealing Rules**

Even though you may control both entities (the nonprofit and the church), **the IRS and state regulators are highly sensitive** to any appearance of:

- **Self-dealing** ‚Äî e.g., transferring funds to a church that pays *you* a salary or benefits your family.
- **Private inurement** ‚Äî using tax-exempt funds to enrich insiders.
- **Failure to exercise independent oversight** ‚Äî if the nonprofit board doesn't vote independently on the donation.

If the IRS finds **conflict of interest** or improper benefit:
- You could face **excise taxes**, **revocation of tax-exempt status**, or **penalties for the board**.
- The **Attorney General** in your state (especially California) could investigate for misuse of charitable assets.

---

## üìú 3. **Best Practices to Do This Legally**

| Requirement | What You Must Do |
|-------------|------------------|
| **Establish the church as a separate legal entity** | With its own EIN and bylaws, even if it‚Äôs not yet 501(c)(3). |
| **Ensure shared control is disclosed** | On IRS Form 990 under ‚Äúrelated organizations‚Äù and ‚Äútransactions with interested persons.‚Äù |
| **Get board approval** | The nonprofit board (excluding you if you have a conflict) must vote on the donation. |
| **Document the purpose** | Show how the donation supports the nonprofit‚Äôs mission (e.g., community outreach, education, food programs). |
| **Avoid personal gain** | You can‚Äôt be paid or reimbursed in ways that violate fair market value. |

---

## üßæ 4. If the Church Is Not Yet a 501(c)(3)

- Churches **are automatically tax-exempt** under IRS Code ¬ß 508(c)(1)(A), even without filing.
- However, your nonprofit should still:
  - Confirm the church is **organized and operated exclusively for religious/charitable purposes**.
  - Document how the funds will be used.
  - Avoid transferring large sums without clear oversight, especially in early stages.

---

## üß† Final Rule of Thumb:

> **You can give, but you cannot benefit**.

If the donation:
- advances your nonprofit‚Äôs charitable mission,
- is properly approved and documented,
- doesn‚Äôt enrich you personally,

‚Ä¶then **yes**, your nonprofit can support a church you found. But **do everything by the book**, because **this is one of the most common audit triggers** for the IRS and state AG offices.
