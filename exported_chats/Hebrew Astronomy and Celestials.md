# Hebrew Astronomy and Celestials



---

What did the Hebrews call the planets that we know of, or that they knew of? What were their names, or did they have names for them? And what about the constellations?

---



---

The Hebrews had names for the celestial bodies they observed, including the planets and constellations, though their understanding was rooted in an ancient worldview that did not categorize them as "planets" in the modern sense. Instead, they saw them as wandering stars, distinct from the fixed stars of the constellations.

### **Planets in Hebrew Thought**
The planets visible to the naked eye—Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn—were likely known to the Hebrews, though specific names are difficult to trace with certainty. The Babylonian and later Greco-Roman influences may have introduced more structured planetary identification. However, some references in biblical and extrabiblical texts suggest names or associations:

1. **Mercury** – Possibly associated with a messenger or swift god, but no direct Hebrew name is recorded.
2. **Venus** – Likely connected to the morning and evening star, sometimes called **Nogah (נֹגַהּ)** meaning "brightness" or "shining" (Isaiah 14:12, though this verse is often linked to Lucifer).
3. **Mars** – Possibly identified as **Ma’adim (מַאְדִּים)** meaning "the red one," which is still used in modern Hebrew.
4. **Jupiter** – Might have been known as **Tzedek (צֶדֶק)** meaning "righteousness," still the name for Jupiter in modern Hebrew.
5. **Saturn** – Could be **Shabbatai (שַׁבְּתַאי)**, meaning "related to the Sabbath" or "rest," which is also its name in modern Hebrew.

These names largely survive from later Jewish writings and linguistic connections, but it is unclear if they were known during the early biblical period.

### **Constellations in Hebrew Thought**
The Hebrews had knowledge of constellations, often referred to in poetic or symbolic terms in the Bible. Some key references include:

- **Kimah (כִּימָה)** – Often identified with the Pleiades (Job 9:9, 38:31; Amos 5:8).
- **Kesil (כְּסִיל)** – Usually identified as Orion (Job 9:9, 38:31).
- **Ash or Ayish (עָשׁ / עַיִשׁ)** – Possibly Ursa Major, the Great Bear or Arcturus (Job 9:9, 38:32).
- **Mazzaroth (מַזָּרוֹת)** – A term that may refer to the zodiac or the twelve constellations that mark the yearly cycle (Job 38:32).
- **Nachash Bariach (נָחָשׁ בָּרִיחַ)** – The "fleeing serpent," possibly referring to the constellation Draco (Job 26:13).
- **Mezarim (מְזָרִים)** – Possibly the signs of the zodiac or specific constellations (Job 37:9).

The Hebrews likely inherited many of these names from older Semitic traditions, including Babylonian and Akkadian astronomy, which deeply influenced Near Eastern cosmology.

### **The Mazzaroth and the Zodiac**
The term **Mazzaroth** appears in Job 38:32 and is often interpreted as referring to the zodiac constellations. While not explicitly listed in the Bible, the twelve constellations associated with the zodiac were recognized in the broader ancient Near East, and some Jewish traditions later linked them to the twelve tribes of Israel.

### **Final Thoughts**
While the Hebrews did not systematize the planets and constellations in the way later Greek or Babylonian astronomers did, they certainly observed them and integrated them into their theological and poetic traditions. The biblical references suggest that celestial bodies were seen as part of God’s creation, with symbolic significance rather than objects of direct worship or detailed scientific study.
