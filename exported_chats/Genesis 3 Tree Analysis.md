### Literal Construction

> "And the LORD God said, 'Behold, the man has become like one from us to know good and evil; and now, lest he stretch out his hand and also take from the tree of life, and eat, and live forever.'"

#### What Actually Happens:
- God does **not** command or forbid the eating here.
- God is reflecting: "If Adam now eats from the Tree of Life, he will live forever."
- This is **not** a commandment but a narrative reflection on potential action and consequence.
- The action is **prevented** in Genesis 3:24, where God places cherubim to guard the tree.

---

### Subtle Points in the Hebrew

- **Pen (פֶּן)**: This is a preventive, “lest” or “otherwise.” It expresses concern over a possibility.  
- **Gam (גַּם)**: "Also" indicates that Adam has already done something (eaten from one tree) and might do another.
- **Ve-achal (וְאָכַל)**: "And eat" is in the vav-consecutive, which usually indicates a sequential action in Hebrew narrative.
- **Va-chai le-olam (וָחַי לְעֹלָם)**: "And live forever." The Hebrew le-olam means "to the age," or "into perpetuity," not always "infinity" but an unending or indeterminate length.

---

### Change in Understanding?

- **No explicit prohibition** of the Tree of Life. The command not to eat was about the Tree of Knowledge (Genesis 2:17).
- **God’s concern** is that access to the Tree of Life after gaining knowledge would allow humanity to live eternally in a fallen state.
- **“Lest he stretch out his hand and also take…”** is a preventive action, not a punitive curse.
- **The narrative never says Adam or Eve actually ate from the Tree of Life.** The path is blocked after the fall.

---

### Short Analysis

**In Hebrew, God is not issuing a command about the Tree of Life, but expressing a concern about Adam's potential to become eternal in his fallen state.** The divine action is to block access, not to curse. There is a marked difference in tone between the warning about the Tree of Knowledge (“you shall not eat… you shall surely die”) and the concern about the Tree of Life (“lest he take… and live forever”).

Excellent question. The word translated as "eat" in these verses comes from the Hebrew root **אָכַל** (*’akal*), which is the basic verb for "to eat" in Biblical Hebrew.

### Let’s break it down:

#### The Forms Used

- **אָכֹל** (*akol*) — This is the infinitive absolute. In Hebrew, doubling the infinitive with a conjugated verb (“אָכֹל תֹּאכֵל”) is an emphatic construction, like “eating you will eat” or “surely eat.”
- **תֹּאכֵל** (*to’khel*) — This is the second person masculine singular imperfect, literally: “you eat.”
- **תֹאכַל** (*to’khal*) — Also second person masculine singular, just a different vowel pointing, contextually “you eat.”
- **אֲכָלְךָ** (*akhal’kha*) — “your eating,” with the pronoun “your” attached.

#### The Root Meaning

- **Root: אָכַל (’akal)**
    - Core meaning: "to eat, consume, devour"
    - Used for both literal and figurative eating in the Hebrew Bible.

#### Literal Range

- In the **broadest sense**: "consume," "ingest," "devour"
- Not “chew,” not “taste,” not “partake”—it’s straightforward: "eat" as in “take food in and swallow it.”

#### How is it used elsewhere?

- Genesis 3:6 — וַתֹּאכַל (*vato’khal*) “and she ate” (Eve and the fruit)
- Genesis 18:8 — וַיֹּאכֵלוּ (*vayo’khelu*) “and they ate” (Abraham’s guests)
- Deuteronomy 8:3 — וַיַּאֲכִלְךָ (*vaya’akhilkha*) “and He fed you”

#### Is there any alternate meaning?

- In rare contexts, it can mean “to consume” metaphorically, as in “the sword shall consume,” but in **this context, with trees and fruit, it means eat**—to physically eat.

---

### Summing Up:

- **The word “eat” in these verses is the standard Hebrew word אָכַל, and all its forms there mean “to eat” in the sense of taking food into the mouth and swallowing it.**
- The emphatic construction “אָכֹל תֹּאכֵל” intensifies it: “eating, you eat,” or “you may surely eat.”

Excellent insight. **Yes, it absolutely can.** The exact Hebrew phrase at the end of Genesis 2:17 is:

> **מוֹת תָּמוּת**  
> *mot tamut*

This is what’s called an **infinitive absolute + imperfect** construction. It’s a Hebrew idiom for strong emphasis, often rendered as “surely die.” But—**word for word**—it literally means:

- **מוֹת** (*mot*) = death, dying, to die
- **תָּמוּת** (*tamut*) = you die, you will die

So, as a raw reading:

> “death you die”  
> “dying you die”  
> “in dying, you die”  
> “in death, you die”

Or if you want to retain the *emphatic* sense but keep the double-word construction:

- **“dying, you die”**
- **“death, you die”**
- **“to die, you die”**
- **“with death, you die”**

---

### Does the grammar allow for “in eating death, you will die”?

Here’s how the phrase appears in full:
> כִּי בְּיוֹם אֲכָלְךָ מִמֶּנּוּ מוֹת תָּמוּת  
> *ki b’yom akhal’kha mimenu mot tamut*

**Breaking it down:**
- כִּי (ki) = for, because, when
- בְּיוֹם (b’yom) = in the day, at the time
- אֲכָלְךָ (akhal’kha) = your eating, you eat
- מִמֶּנּוּ (mimenu) = from it
- מוֹת תָּמוּת (mot tamut) = death you die, dying you die

**Literal chain:**
> “for in day your-eating from-it death you-die”

So, grammatically, it can be rendered:
- “For in the day of your eating from it, death you die.”
- “For in the day you consume from it, dying you die.”
- Or, for a more fluid but still raw reading:  
  “In your eating, death you will die.”

---

### Could “eating death” be implied?

If you’re exploring a metaphoric/parabolic layer:  
- The Hebrew **does not explicitly say** “eating death,”  
- **But the structure links “eating” and “death” in a cause-effect way:**  
  - “in the day you eat… death/dying you die.”
- **You could interpret**:  
  - “In eating [this], death [enters] you will die.”  
  - “In consuming, you become consumed by death.”  
  - Or even, “In taking in [this knowledge], you absorb death.”

---

### Scholarly Note

Some mystical and allegorical Jewish and Christian commentaries *do* interpret this as not only a pronouncement of mortality, but as an infusion of death/loss of immortality, spiritual death, or a change of state.
