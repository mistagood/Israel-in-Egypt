The number of PDFs (or, more precisely, the amount of text data) required to fine-tune a model can vary widely based on several factors:

1. **Model Complexity**: More complex models with a greater number of parameters may require more data to fine-tune effectively.

2. **Task Specificity**: Highly specialized tasks may require fewer data if the model is already pre-trained on a similar task. Conversely, a more general task may require more data.

3. **Quality of Data**: High-quality, well-curated data can sometimes make up for a smaller dataset.

4. **Computational Resources**: The more data you have, the more computational power you will generally need. This is not only because the model itself might be large but also because larger datasets require more memory and storage.

5. **Pre-training**: If you're fine-tuning a model that has already been pre-trained on a similar task or domain, you might need less data.

6. **Research Objectives**: Your specific research goals can also determine the amount of data needed. Some studies might focus on the limitations and capabilities of small, fine-tuned models, while others might aim to achieve the highest performance possible, requiring larger datasets.

As a rough guide, you might find the following tiers useful:

- **Small Dataset**: Hundreds to thousands of documents. Suitable for very specialized tasks or as a proof-of-concept.
- **Medium Dataset**: Tens of thousands of documents. Useful for more general tasks or common academic research topics.
- **Large Dataset**: Hundreds of thousands to millions of documents. Needed for very general tasks or high levels of performance.

For academic purposes, the balance often lies in having a dataset that's large enough to be representative of the population you're studying, yet manageable enough to be processed and analyzed given your resources (Schwabish, 2019).
