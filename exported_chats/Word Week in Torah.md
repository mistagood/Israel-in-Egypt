1. **Conceptual Expression** – Instead of using *shavua*, Moses describes time periods in sevens by saying "seven days" (*shivat yamim*, שבעת ימים). 2. **Language Development** – The word *shavua* does exist in Hebrew but is rarely used in early biblical texts. It is more common in later books, such as Daniel, indicating a possible linguistic evolution. Instead, Moses relied on ordinal counting (e.g., first day, second day, seventh day).

3. **The Sabbath Cycle** – While the seven-day cycle was foundational (Genesis 1–2), the Torah primarily refers to the *Sabbath* (*Shabbat*, שבת) rather than a "week." The focus was on six days of work followed by a day of rest rather than defining a named "week" structure.

### Was There No Word for *Week* in Moses’ Time?
The root of *shavua* (שבוע) comes from *sheva* (שבע), meaning "seven." The word was likely known, but its specific use as a unit of time may not have been common in early Hebrew. Instead, people marked time through sabbatical cycles, festivals, and numbered days rather than using an abstract term like "week."

### The Implications:
- **Structured Time Without a Name** – The Israelites clearly understood time in sevens, but Moses never explicitly labels it.
- **Sabbath as the Marker** – Instead of referring to a "week," people oriented their time around Sabbaths.
- **Later Usage of *Shavua*** – The word for "week" became more common in later Hebrew, possibly influenced by external cultures or linguistic shifts.

This absence raises an important historical and linguistic question: If *shavua* wasn't necessary in Moses' time, when and why did it become a common term? It suggests a shift in how time was measured and named, possibly influenced by exile, foreign cultures, or later religious formalization.
