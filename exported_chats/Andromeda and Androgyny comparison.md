Here’s the clean, straight-line etymology—no fluff, no mythology drift.

**They are *not* related.**  
The apparent similarity between **Andromeda** and **androgyny** is purely phonetic. They come from completely different Greek roots with different meanings and histories.

Let’s break them apart with surgical precision.

---

## **1. ANDROMEDA — Greek: Ἀνδρομέδα**
Root components:

- **ἀνήρ / ἀνδρός (anēr / andrós)**  
  meaning **“man, male, husband.”**

- **μέδω / medō**  
  meaning **“to protect, to rule over.”**

So **Andromeda** literally means  
**“She who rules/protects men”** or **“mindful of men.”**

It is a *feminine* name formed from a masculine root (**andr-**) combined with a different verb root (**med-**).

No connection to gender fluidity, duality, or blending. It’s simply a classical compound name.

---

## **2. ANDROGYNY — Greek: ἀνδρόγυνος / ἀνδρογυνία**
Root components:

- **ἀνδρός (andro-)**  
  **“man, male.”**

- **γυνή (gynē / gyne-)**  
  **“woman, female.”**

So **androgyny** literally means  
**“man-woman,”** or **“containing male and female characteristics.”**

The second root in *androgyny* is **–gyn**, “woman,” not **–med**, “to rule.”

---

## **3. WHY THEY SOUND RELATED**
They *begin* with the same Greek root:

- **andr- = man/male**

That’s the only overlap.

- **Andro-meda** = man + ruling/protecting  
- **Andro-gyn-y** = man + woman

Different second halves, different meanings, different functions.

---

## **4. Semiotic / documentary relevance**
From a symbolic-analysis lens:

- **Andromeda** uses *andr-* but is still a **female figure defined in relation to a man** (“she who protects men”).  
- **Androgyny** uses *andr-* + *gyn-* and describes **the merging of male and female categories**, often viewed as boundary-breaking or transgressive in ancient systems.

So semiotically:

**Andromeda = reinforces gender hierarchy (woman serving/protecting men).  
Androgyny = collapses gender hierarchy (man + woman fused).**

These concepts are opposites in the ancient world’s symbolic grammar, even though they share the same initial morpheme.
