### **King Arthur and the Myths of the Early Middle Ages**

**Origins of the Arthurian Legends:**

- **King Arthur** is a legendary figure said to have led the defense of Britain against Saxon invaders in the late **5th and early 6th centuries**.
- The earliest references to Arthur come from **Welsh poetry** and the writings of **Nennius**, a 9th-century monk who compiled the *Historia Brittonum*.
- The most influential early work is **Geoffrey of Monmouth's** *Historia Regum Britanniae* (History of the Kings of Britain), written around **1136**. Geoffrey's work blends history and legend, introducing many elements that became staples of Arthurian lore.

**Nature of the Myths:**

- **Historical Debate:** Scholars debate whether Arthur was a real historical figure, a composite of multiple leaders, or purely mythical.
- **Lack of Contemporary Records:** The period after the Roman withdrawal from Britain (~AD 410) is poorly documented, contributing to the mystery surrounding Arthur.
- **Cultural Significance:** Arthurian legends evolved over centuries, incorporating chivalric ideals and becoming central to medieval literature and folklore.

**Impact on Historical Understanding:**

- **Myth vs. History:** The blending of mythological elements with historical events makes it challenging to separate fact from fiction.
- **Transmission of Tales:** Oral traditions and later medieval romances expanded the Arthurian legends, often reflecting the values and concerns of the times in which they were written.

### **William the Conqueror and the Norman Conquest**

**The Norman Conquest (1066):**

- **Background:** In **1066**, **William, Duke of Normandy**, invaded England, defeating King Harold II at the **Battle of Hastings**.
- **Significance:** This event marked the beginning of Norman rule in England, leading to profound political, social, and cultural changes.

**Sweeping Changes Post-Conquest:**

- **Feudal System Implementation:**
  - **Land Redistribution:** William granted lands to his Norman followers, dispossessing many Anglo-Saxon nobles.
  - **Feudal Hierarchy:** Established a strict hierarchical system, strengthening royal authority.

- **Administrative Reforms:**
  - **Domesday Book (1086):**
    - **Purpose:** A comprehensive survey of landholdings and resources in England, commissioned by William to assess taxes and control over the land.
    - **Name Origin:** Called "Domesday" (Middle English spelling of "Doomsday") because its judgments were seen as final, like those on the Day of Judgment.
    - **Content:** Detailed records of estates, land values, resources, and population, providing invaluable insights into 11th-century England.

- **Cultural and Linguistic Impact:**
  - **Language Shift:** Norman French became the language of the ruling class and administration, influencing the development of the English language.
  - **Architectural Changes:** Introduction of Romanesque architecture, evident in castles, cathedrals, and churches (e.g., the Tower of London).
