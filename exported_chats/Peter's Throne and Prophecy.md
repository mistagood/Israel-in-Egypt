Below is a brief, verse-centered comparison between Jeremiah 16 and the New Testament reference in Mark 1:16–17. I’ve included direct quotations (primarily from the King James Version, though other translations read similarly). The goal is to show how the concept of “fishers” in Jeremiah’s prophecy is revisited by Jesus when he tells Peter and Andrew they will become “fishers of men”—and how the grim tone in Jeremiah shapes our understanding of what follows in the Gospels.

1. **JEREMIAH 16:1–4** – The Grim Setting

   > “The word of the LORD came also unto me, saying,  
   > Thou shalt not take thee a wife, neither shalt thou have sons or daughters in this place.  
   > For thus saith the LORD concerning the sons and concerning the daughters that are born in this place… They shall die of grievous deaths; they shall not be lamented; neither shall they be buried; but they shall be as dung upon the face of the earth…”

   **Analysis**: Jeremiah is forbidden to marry or have children as a sign of the catastrophe looming over Judah. This is not a peaceful prophecy; it is a dire judgment.

2. **JEREMIAH 16:5–6** – No Mourning for the People

   > “For thus saith the LORD, Enter not into the house of mourning… for I have taken away my peace from this people… both the great and the small shall die in this land: they shall not be buried, neither shall men lament for them…”

   **Analysis**: God has removed “peace” (Hebrew: shalom) from Judah. This underscores that any prophet proclaiming peace is lying—judgment is on its way.

3. **JEREMIAH 16:10–13** – Explanation of the Punishment

   > “And it shall come to pass, when thou shalt shew this people all these words… they shall say unto thee, Wherefore hath the LORD pronounced all this great evil against us?... Then shalt thou say unto them, Because your fathers have forsaken me, saith the LORD, and have walked after other gods… therefore will I cast you out of this land into a land that ye know not…”

   **Analysis**: Idolatry and disobedience form the basis of this “evil” or calamity. Jeremiah insists it’s too late for a comforting resolution.

4. **JEREMIAH 16:14–15** – A Flicker of Future Return?

   > “Therefore, behold, the days come, saith the LORD, that it shall no more be said, The LORD liveth, that brought up the children of Israel out of the land of Egypt;  
   > But, The LORD liveth, that brought up the children of Israel from the land of the north, and from all the lands whither he had driven them…”

   **Analysis**: These verses can sound hopeful, but the context of immediate judgment is still overshadowing. Many see this as a future promise of restoration *after* the punishment.

5. **JEREMIAH 16:16** – The Fishermen and Hunters

   > “Behold, I will send for many fishers, saith the LORD, and they shall fish them; and after will I send for many hunters, and they shall hunt them from every mountain, and from every hill, and out of the holes of the rocks.”

   **Analysis**: The language of “fishers” (and “hunters”) here is ominous. It depicts agents of God’s judgment who leave no escape for the guilty. This is the crucial linguistic link: “fishers” in Jeremiah’s prophecy = those who gather people for punishment.

6. **FALSE PROPHETS WHO PROMISE PEACE**  
   While Jeremiah 16 itself does not explicitly say “They prophesy peace,” the broader context of Jeremiah addresses this theme, for example:

   - **Jeremiah 14:13–14**:  
     > “Then said I, Ah, Lord GOD! behold, the prophets say unto them, Ye shall not see the sword, neither shall ye have famine; but I will give you assured peace in this place.

     Here, “peace” is exposed as a false promise given by lying prophets. Jeremiah repeatedly rails against prophets who say, “Peace, peace; when there is no peace” (cf. Jeremiah 6:14, 8:11).

- In other words, the nation’s suffering in the north (i.e., from the direction of Babylon, or more broadly speaking, foreign invaders) will prove so catastrophic that when God eventually acts, people will say: “He rescued us from *that* horror,” eclipsing the old refrain, “He rescued us from Egypt.”  
- Far from a simple “glimmer of hope,” it implies a future captivity whose destruction is on par with the worst event in Israelite collective memory. Only after this new devastation can any talk of deliverance be made—and even then, the dire warning takes center stage.

2) **THE WIDER CONTEXT OF JEREMIAH 16 — JUDGMENT, NOT COMFORT**

A quick glance at the chapter reveals why verses 14–15 must be read in a grim light:

- **Jeremiah 16:1–4**:  
  > “The word of the LORD came also unto me, saying, Thou shalt not take thee a wife… for thus saith the LORD concerning the sons… They shall die of grievous deaths...”  

  Jeremiah is commanded not to marry or have children, signifying that the land is on the brink of devastation so total that normal life (marriage, child-rearing) is unthinkable.

- **Jeremiah 16:5–6**:  
  > “Enter not into the house of mourning… for I have taken away my peace (shalom) from this people…”  

  God explicitly states He has removed peace from the nation. This is key—any prophet claiming peace at that time would be contradicting God’s own pronouncement.

- **Jeremiah 16:16**:  
  > “Behold, I will send for many fishers… and they shall fish them; and after will I send for many hunters, and they shall hunt them…”  

  The “fishers” and “hunters” are not benevolent gatherers; they are agents of judgment. No matter where the people flee, they will be pursued.

Taken together, Jeremiah 16 broadcasts a coming punishment so severe it will dwarf earlier catastrophes. Any portrayal of “peace” during this impending doom is essentially a lie in Jeremiah’s eyes.

3) **JEREMIAH AGAINST PEACE-PROPHETS**

While Jeremiah 16 itself emphasizes calamity, the prophet’s broader writings expand on why proclaiming peace is suspect:

- **Jeremiah 6:13–14 (KJV)**  
  > “For from the least of them even unto the greatest of them every one is given to covetousness… they have healed also the hurt of the daughter of my people slightly, saying, Peace, peace; when there is no peace.”  

- **Jeremiah 14:13–14 (KJV)**  
  > “Then said I, Ah, Lord GOD! behold, the prophets say unto them, Ye shall not see the sword, neither shall ye have famine; but I will give you assured peace in this place.  
  > Then the LORD said unto me, The prophets prophesy lies in my name: I sent them not…”  

These passages condemn false prophets who promise shalom (peace) to a rebellious people. According to Jeremiah, such promises are deceptions leading the nation to disaster rather than repentance.

4) **PAUL’S MESSAGE OF PEACE — POSSIBLE FULFILLMENT OF THE “FALSE PROPHET” THEME?**

The New Testament epistles repeatedly highlight “peace” as part of Paul’s gospel. For example:

- **Romans 5:1 (KJV)**  
  > “Therefore being justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ.”  

- **Ephesians 2:14 (KJV)**  
  > “For he is our peace, who hath made both one, and hath broken down the middle wall of partition between us…”  

- **Romans 12:18 (KJV)**  
  > “If it be possible, as much as lieth in you, live peaceably with all men.”  

Over and over, Paul emphasizes peace—both peace with God (through faith in the crucified Messiah) and peace with one another (Jew and Gentile united). From a traditional Mosaic perspective, however, this might seem to bypass core requirements of the Torah. Seen through a Jeremiad lens:

- Proclaiming a grand “peace” that sets aside the strict demands of the Law could be interpreted as the same brand of “Peace, peace; when there is no peace” that Jeremiah inveighed against.  
- If one believes Paul’s teachings introduce or condone practices contrary to Mosaic commandments (e.g., disregarding dietary laws, circumcision debates, etc.), then he might fit the archetype of the “false prophet” who seduces people with promises of peace and acceptance.  

5) **THE CAPTIVITY AND DESTRUCTION THAT FOLLOWED**

Jeremiah 16 foreshadows impending doom in or near Jerusalem, culminating historically in several devastations—most pointedly the Babylonian conquest (6th century BCE) and, centuries later, the Roman destruction of the Second Temple in 70 AD. Even in the New Testament era:

- Israel lost the Temple and was scattered;  
- Jerusalem was sacked by Roman forces.  

This parallels the idea that any “peace” purportedly available to Israel (through assimilation with Gentile customs or through a new gospel that overshadowed the old ways) did not manifest as actual deliverance from national catastrophe. Instead, the city was laid waste—echoing the very punishment Jeremiah described.

In the Hebrew Scriptures, prophets often speak of “peace,” but almost always in one of two contexts. They either condemn false prophets who promise immediate well-being when destruction is at the door, or they cast a long-range vision of end-time harmony—what many call the “messianic” or “restoration” era—far beyond their present turmoil. No prophet proclaims a current, all-encompassing peace in the midst of oppression as Paul does in his letters.

Isaiah pronounces doom on a stubborn nation yet also foresees a glorious future. In one breath, he rebukes Israel for injustice and false worship; in another, he describes a day when swords will become plowshares and the wolf will dwell with the lamb (Isaiah 2:4; 11:6–9). Yes, Isaiah 9:6 calls the coming ruler “Prince of Peace,” but that title belongs to an eventual reign—an era of light when the zeal of the LORD triumphs. It is not a claim that, in his own day, the people possessed any real peace. Elsewhere, Isaiah 52:7 bursts with the cry, “How beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings, that publisheth peace,” but this good news is tied to the awaited deliverance of Zion. It is a promise for a future redemption, not an endorsement of the status quo.

Jeremiah bluntly calls out anyone who says “peace” while the temple is about to fall. He deplores those who plaster over deep moral rot with a whitewash of comforting words (Jeremiah 6:14; 8:11). The same man who prophesies seventy years of captivity and a land left desolate does not champion an immediate gospel of peace. Even Jeremiah 29:7, “Seek the peace of the city” in Babylon, is advice for exiles to get by in captivity—hardly a declaration that the people are safe from further tribulation.

Ezekiel, in a similar fashion, skewers those who say “Peace” when there is no peace (Ezekiel 13:10). He likewise looks ahead to a “covenant of peace” for a restored Israel (Ezekiel 34:25), but it’s a future condition contingent on the nation’s renewed faithfulness. Micah, who condemns the wealthy for devouring the poor, also prophesies swords turned into plowshares (Micah 4:3). Again, that peace remains a beacon on the horizon, not a present reality. Zechariah pictures a humble king riding on a donkey to proclaim peace to the nations (Zechariah 9:9–10), yet this too anchors itself in messianic expectation.

So while these prophets do indeed use the language of “peace,” they do it in two main ways: either to rebuke those who preach peace irresponsibly in times of doom or to describe a far-off era of divine restoration. No prophet in the Old Testament urges people to embrace a full-blown “gospel of peace” for the present, as Paul does. Paul’s letters emphasize peace as an immediate outcome of faith—“we have peace with God” (Romans 5:1), “He is our peace” (Ephesians 2:14), “the peace of God, which passeth all understanding, shall keep your hearts” (Philippians 4:7). For Paul, this peace is available *now*, even under Roman occupation and ongoing oppression. Such a stance seems at odds with the general tenor of prophetic warnings, which usually claim peace is impossible until a radical upheaval or divine intervention resolves injustice and idolatry.

That is why you see Jeremiah and others railing against any proclamation of peace while the hammer of judgment is swinging. Their calls for repentance typically come with the warning that peace cannot be forced into existence by mere words. By contrast, Paul teaches that through faith in Christ, believers can possess genuine peace immediately. If one reads Paul through a strictly prophetic lens, this might look suspiciously like “peace, peace, when there is no peace.” Yet for Paul’s followers, it is God’s supernatural answer in a dark world, a peace that bypasses earthly circumstances.

So yes, many prophets mention peace. They exalt its beauty but push its full realization into a future age—or they denounce it as a hollow slogan when shouted by false prophets. You won’t find another Old Testament voice echoing Paul’s brand of a present, all-encompassing “gospel of peace.” The common thread among the Hebrew prophets is that any true peace comes after judgment, repentance, and a complete restoration under divine reign. Paul alone stands out for declaring that such peace can be embraced *now*—right in the middle of the world’s ongoing turmoil.
