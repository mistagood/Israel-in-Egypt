What is it

---

The **Rock of Rimmon** is a rocky stronghold in the territory of Benjamin where 600 surviving Benjamite men fled and hid for four months after their tribe was nearly wiped out in the war described in Judges 20–21. It’s usually identified with a cliff or crag north of Jerusalem, near modern **Rummon (Rammun)**, about 8 miles northeast of Gibeah. It functioned as a natural fortress and last refuge for Benjamin’s remnant.

You’ve pulled the threads together in a way the text itself hints at but doesn’t spell out. Here’s how it lines up:  

- **House of Rimmon / Rimmon worship** = foreign, compromised cult practice that Naaman himself had to bow in (2 Kings 5). A house where allegiance is split, pardon has to be begged for.  
- **Rock of Rimmon** = the last fortress of the Benjamites, but also a name loaded with that same foreign resonance. It’s not just a hiding place, it’s a symbolic “house” where defiled remnants hold on.  
- **600 men** = deliberate echo. In Judges 18, *600 Danites* steal Micah’s idol and set up their own shrine with a Levite. In Judges 20–21, *600 Benjamites* hole up at Rimmon. The number ties the two corrupt survivals together.  
- **Concubine defiled (Judges 19)** = metaphor of Israel itself corrupted by this syncretism. The Levite’s concubine is violated in Gibeah, and Gibeah becomes synonymous with Benjamin’s sin (cf. Hosea 9:9; 10:9).  
- **Dan’s only son Hushim (Shuham) listed as Benjamin’s “Aher”** = textual clue that Benjamin’s survival was merged with Dan’s line. If Dan’s line was extinguished (no inheritance in Revelation 7), the survival of Benjamin at Rimmon might be functioning as a levirate raising up of seed in Dan’s name.  

So the metaphor works like this:  
- The **house of Rimmon** represents a polluted sanctuary.  
- The **rock of Rimmon** is the fortress of that pollution, the survival of a defiled remnant.  
- The **600 survivors** are the echo of the 600 idol-stealers, tying Benjamin’s fate to Dan’s corruption.  
- The **concubine** becomes the emblem of Israel, profaned when foreign worship is embedded in the covenant people.  

It’s a grim motif: the survival of Benjamin isn’t just survival, it’s survival *under the shadow of Rimmon*, carrying the unresolved corruption of Dan and the Levites.

Are there statues or idols of Rimmon surviving 

---

No physical idol of **Rimmon** (also called *Ramon* or *Hadad-Rimmon*) has survived with his name carved on it. What we know comes from texts and related Syrian iconography:  

- **Rimmon = Hadad**, the storm-god of Aram-Damascus. He’s often shown as a bearded warrior with a thunderbolt or club in one hand and a stylized lightning spear in the other, standing on a bull.  
- Archaeology has turned up stelae and reliefs of Hadad in places like **Aleppo** and **Arslan Tash**, but they’re usually labeled with Hadad’s name, not “Rimmon.” Rimmon seems to be a local epithet (from the Semitic root *rmm*, “thunder” or “exalted”).  
- Biblical mentions: only **2 Kings 5:18** and indirectly in names (e.g., *Tabrimmon*, *Ben-Hadad son of Tabrimmon* in 1 Kings 15:18). That shows the cult was active in Damascus, but not that the idols themselves endured.  

So, to answer directly: there aren’t known statues labeled “Rimmon” sitting in museums. What exists are storm-god images (Hadad/Baal) from Syria that would have been visually identical to how “Rimmon” was worshipped. Think thunder-wielding god on a bull, not a household figurine like Asherah.
