# African vs. Indigenous Origins of Enslaved People in U.S. Records

**Introduction:**  
Historical records of slavery in America – including slave laws, ship logs, sale advertisements, and news notices – offer evidence about the origins of enslaved people. A close reading of these sources can either reinforce or challenge the traditional narrative that the vast majority of enslaved people in the U.S. came directly from Africa. In particular, we can compare the documented number of Africans transported via the transatlantic slave trade with estimates of Indigenous people enslaved in what became the United States. We can also analyze ship logs and other records for naming patterns (African *vs.* Native), examine South Carolina’s unique role as a hub for both the Indigenous and African slave trades, and identify contradictions or gaps in the historical record. Such an investigation sheds light on possible revisions or misrepresentations in how history remembers the scale of African *versus* Indigenous enslavement.

## 1. Numbers: Africans Transported vs. Indigenous Enslaved

**Africans Transported to the U.S.:**  
Modern research based on slave ship logs (such as the **Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database**) shows that a relatively small fraction of the Africans trafficked in the Atlantic slave trade were brought directly to what is now the United States. Between 1525 and 1866, roughly **12.5 million Africans** were shipped to the Americas, of whom about **10.7 million survived** the Middle Passage and disembarked in the New World【39†L83-L92】. **Only about 388,000** of these survivors – about **3.6%** – were transported **directly to North America (the U.S.)**【39†L93-L100】. This figure surprises many, but it is now considered the **“gold standard”** estimate based on comprehensive shipping records【39†L83-L91】. The remainder of enslaved Africans were taken to Latin America and the Caribbean. (Some Africans who first arrived in the Caribbean were later **re-exported** or sold to North American colonies, but even including those, the U.S.-bound total is on the order of a few hundred thousand, not millions【27†L82-L90】.)

By comparison, due to **natural population growth**, the enslaved African-descended population in the U.S. had swelled to nearly **4 million** by 1860. This was largely because enslaved people in the U.S. (unlike in many Caribbean colonies) had higher birth rates and somewhat better survival rates, resulting in a large **American-born** enslaved population by the 19th century. The relatively **low direct import figure** of ~388,000 Africans contrasts with the much larger African origin population present by the Civil War era, highlighting that the U.S. slave population grew from a small imported base.

**Indigenous People Enslaved:**  
Enslavement of **Native Americans** was widespread in the Americas from the 1500s through the 1700s, though it has often been overshadowed by the African slave trade in historical narratives. Scholars estimate that **between 2.5 and 5 million Indigenous people** were enslaved in the Western Hemisphere from the time of Columbus into the 19th century【16†L9-L13】. Within the territories of the future United States, **tens of thousands** of Native Americans were enslaved, especially in the 17th and early 18th centuries. Historian **Alan Gallay** estimates that **24,000 to 51,000 Indigenous captives** were **exported from the Carolina colony alone between 1670 and 1715**【11†L105-L113】. These were primarily Native Americans from the Southeast (various tribes in the Carolinas, Georgia, Florida region) who were captured in inter-tribal wars or slave raids instigated by colonists. They were often **shipped out of Charleston** to other British colonies *such as* **Barbados, Jamaica, or New England**, or traded away in exchange for African slaves【31†L43-L49】.

By the early 1700s, Indian slavery in the English colonies had grown to a scale that rivaled or exceeded the trade in furs and other commodities. In fact, “as the 18th century dawned the **slave trade in American Indians was so serious that it eclipsed the trade for furs and skins** and had become the **primary source of commerce** between the English and the South Carolina colonials”【31†L43-L49】. This means that, around 1700-1715, the business of capturing and selling Indigenous people was a driving force of the Carolina economy – even more than the famed deerskin (fur) trade. Thousands of Indigenous people from the Carolinas and surrounding regions were **enslaved and sold** during this period【11†L105-L113】, at times **“at prices comparable to African slaves”**【11†L105-L113】. Many ended up in the Caribbean or other distant colonies, which is one reason their story is less visible in later U.S. population records.

**Comparing the Scales:**  The **documented transatlantic imports of Africans to the U.S. (~388,000)** is a number derived from actual ship manifests and port records (primary sources like customs documents, slave ship logs, etc.). In contrast, the **enslavement of Indigenous people** is harder to quantify from primary records because it was often **less formally documented** (much of it was “illicit” or outside official regulation【9†L55-L63】). However, the available evidence suggests that **through the late 1600s and early 1700s, the English colonies enslaved Native Americans in substantial numbers – possibly in the tens of thousands**, especially in the South. For example, by one estimate **Carolinians had exported more Indian slaves out of Carolina by 1715 than the Africans they had imported** up to that point【11†L105-L113】. One source notes that **between 1700 and 1720, Carolina planters imported about 9,000 African slaves**, while at the same time on the order of **tens of thousands of Indians (24k–51k)** were sold and exported【11†L105-L113】. This stark comparison shows that **Indigenous slavery was a significant part of early colonial slavery** – arguably larger than African slavery in the region until the African trade fully took off in the 18th century. 

After around 1720, **most colonies phased out large-scale Indian slavery in favor of importing Africans**【31†L30-L38】. This shift occurred for several reasons: high Indigenous death rates from disease and war, the tendency of Native captives to escape (since they knew the land), and the increasing availability of African slaves via the Atlantic trade【31†L30-L38】【9†L67-L75】. By the mid-18th century, the African slave trade had grown dramatically, and the population of enslaved Indians within the colonies had dwindled (partly due to exports and deaths). For instance, by **1740 in South Carolina’s lowcountry, about 40,000 slaves lived there (roughly 90% of the local population) – these were by then overwhelmingly people of African descent**【40†L1-L8】. 

**Bottom line:** Primary shipping records show hundreds of thousands of Africans were transported to the U.S. (and additional thousands via Caribbean), while archival evidence (though fragmentary) indicates that **tens of thousands of Native Americans** were also enslaved in the colonial era. The African trade ultimately delivered a larger overall population (especially after 1720), but in the earlier period the **scope of Indigenous enslavement was also very large** – a fact sometimes downplayed in popular narratives of American slavery.

## 2. Ship Logs and Naming Patterns: African or Indigenous Origins?

**Slave Ship Manifests:**  
Slave ship logs and manifests were primarily concerned with counting cargo (the enslaved people) and recording origin, destination, and condition, rather than listing individual names. In most cases, **enslaved Africans were not named in transatlantic shipping records** – they might be enumerated by gender and age, and sometimes by African “nation” or ethnic group, but rarely by personal name. For example, a ship log might say a vessel carried “300 slaves from Angola” or “90 Gold Coast negroes,” etc. These indicate African origins (Angola, Gold Coast, etc.). British and American ship captains also commonly recorded the **African regions or ports** of embarkation (such as Senegambia, Windward Coast, Gold Coast, Bight of Biafra, Congo, etc.), which by extension reflect the African origins of the captives. The **Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database** compiles such records, allowing us to know from which parts of Africa those 388,000 people came. (For instance, many came from West African coasts – Senegambia, Sierra Leone, Gold Coast, Bight of Biafra, West Central Africa, etc., correlating with regions like modern Gambia, Ghana, Nigeria, Angola【14†L9-L17】.)

**African “Country Marks” and Names:**  
Other primary records, like **newspaper advertisements for runaway slaves** or bills of sale, do sometimes include names or descriptions that hint at an enslaved person’s origin. In 18th-century South Carolina, enslavers often noted whether a person was **“newly imported” from Africa (a “new Negro”)** and sometimes even recorded the person’s **“country name,” scars, or language**. For example, a Charleston newspaper ad might describe a runaway as **“a new Negro fellow, speaks no English, his name as far as can be understood… is Windy”**【33†L309-L317】. In one compilation of South Carolina runaway ads, many African-born slaves are listed with African names or nicknames like *Cuffee, Quash, Cudjo, Quamina, Minda, Juba,* etc., often noted as their “country name” (a name from their African culture)【33†L279-L288】【33†L295-L304】. Some ads explicitly mention **African ethnicities**: e.g., a woman described as “looks much like an Eboe Negro” (Igbo)【33†L283-L290】, or another whose **“country name was Fantame (Fantee)”** indicating Fante (an Akan group)【33†L295-L304】. These naming patterns – day names like Cuffee (for Friday, from Akan languages), ethnic labels like “Angola”, or just non-European sounding names – strongly suggest **African origins**. 

In contrast, when **Indigenous people** appear in records, they are often identified explicitly as *“Indian.”* Early colonial documents and ads do use terms like “Indian slave” or identify individuals by tribe or a Spanish name. For instance, one 1705 Boston News-Letter ad sought **“an Indian man named Isaac Pummatick”** who had fled, distinguishing him from an enslaved African man who fled with him【35†L13-L21】. In South Carolina’s early records (late 1600s), “Indian” slaves were clearly labeled as such when mentioned. Over time, however, Indigenous people who remained in bondage were often **assimilated into the African slave population**, sometimes through intermarriage or simply through legal classification (discussed more below). By the 19th century, it became *uncommon* to see a slave referred to as “Indian” in American records – not because all Native slavery had vanished, but because many had been absorbed and labeled as “Negro” or “Mulatto” in the biracial slave society.

**Names Given in the Americas:**  
It’s important to note that most enslaved people – whether of African or Native origin – were assigned new names by slaveholders (often European names or descriptive nicknames). Enslaved Native Americans in English colonies sometimes had Spanish or anglicized names, especially if they came from areas influenced by Spanish missions. For example, a Native person from Florida or the Southwest could have a Spanish baptismal name like “Juan” or “Francisco,” which to a casual observer might appear African (since African Catholics also had Spanish/Portuguese names) or simply “Negro” if their appearance was dark. This can blur the lines in the records. However, colonial records *usually differentiated* people by labels such as *“Negro”* (generally meaning African or of African descent) and *“Indian.”* Virginia’s laws from 1705, for instance, make separate mention of **“negro, mulatto, and Indian”** slaves, indicating that record-keepers did track “Indian” slaves as a category in the early 18th century【31†L29-L37】. South Carolina’s early censuses also enumerated Indian slaves separately from African slaves up until the mid-1700s【7†L163-L171】.

In **ship manifests or trade records**, if Indigenous people were trafficked, they might be noted as “Indian slaves” from certain areas. For example, Spanish records from the 1500s sometimes list “Indios” (Indians) being shipped from Florida or the Carolinas to the Caribbean. In English records, large group transports of Indians are rarely openly logged as such (since much of that trade was “illicit” in the sense of not officially sanctioned【9†L55-L63】). Instead, evidence of **Indian slave exports** comes from letters, governor’s reports, or later historical accounting rather than neatly kept ship logs. One colonial report noted that the **“most important items in the trade [Carolina’s] were deerskins and Indian slaves, the latter always being sold at public auction in Charles Town soon after they arrived”**【26†L290-L297】. This indicates that shiploads of captured Native Americans were arriving in Charleston and being sold – but the record might simply list them as a lot (e.g. “50 Indian slaves brought in from war in Yamassee”). Individual names of those Natives were seldom recorded in such logs.

**Do Names Suggest Indigenous Origin?**  
Overall, the **naming patterns in surviving records mostly reinforce African origins** for the enslaved population by the time of the 18th and 19th centuries. Many names in ads and inventories are African-derived or common European names given to people of African descent【33†L317-L324】【33†L325-L330】. If a significant portion of enslaved people were secretly Indigenous, we would expect more telltale signs in naming or descriptions. Instead, we find numerous specific references to African regions, African “country marks,” languages (e.g. not speaking English, or speaking Gullah, etc.), and African names. Indigenous slaves in the 18th century were comparatively few and often clearly labeled as Indian when they appear. 

For example, in a database of **hundreds of runaway slave ads**, researchers noted the individuals were largely described as African or African-American, *“but a few were Indian or Native American,”* and those cases tended to be explicitly identified as such【15†L41-L48】. This suggests that the people being described in these ads were typically of African origin, and when they were not, the advertisers made a point to say so (because it was an identifying feature).

One must remember that **enslaved Africans and Indigenous people often lived and labored together**, especially in the late 1600s and early 1700s before African numbers surged【31†L37-L45】【31†L57-L65】. There was cultural exchange and intermarriage. Some enslaved individuals had **mixed ancestry** (for instance, an African father and an Indigenous mother). In such cases, colonial society usually classified the person based on appearance or maternal lineage – often as a “Negro” or “Mulatto” if they were of mixed African-European-Indian blood. This means some Native ancestry was likely **submerged** under African naming patterns. But strictly from the perspective of ship logs and primary transactions, **records overwhelmingly point to Africa as the provenance** of enslaved people brought into the U.S. after 1700.

In summary, **ship logs and trade records** for the transatlantic trade show clear African geographic origins, and **naming patterns** in colonial sources (runaway ads, inventories) frequently include African ethnic references or names. Indigenous enslaved people appear in records mostly under the explicit label “Indian,” especially in the late 1600s; that label becomes rare later, reflecting the decline of openly documented Native slavery. The **absence of obviously Indigenous names in most 18th-century slave lists** aligns with the historical transition in which African slavery became dominant and Indigenous slavery was largely suppressed or concealed.

## 3. South Carolina: Hub of Both African and Indigenous Slave Trades

**South Carolina’s Early Indian Slave Trade:**  
South Carolina (particularly **Charleston**, originally Charles Town) was a **crucial hub** in both the Indigenous and African slave trades during the colonial era. Founded in 1670, **Carolina began as a colony heavily involved in enslaving Native Americans**. The English settlers (many originally from Barbados) quickly learned they could obtain valuable goods (like deerskins and, eventually, African slaves) by capturing or purchasing Indian captives and then **exporting those Indians to other colonies**. Carolina’s traders, often in alliance with certain tribes, conducted **slave raids** deep into the interior – reaching as far as present-day Tennessee, Georgia, and even into what the French and Spanish considered their territory【9†L41-L50】【9†L47-L55】. Over the span of roughly four decades (1670s to 1710s), **thousands of Indigenous people** (Yamasee, Tuscarora, Cherokee, Creek, Apalachee, and others) were **enslaved and sent out of South Carolina**. Some were sold to plantations in **New England or Virginia**, but **many were sent to the Caribbean (Barbados, Jamaica) or other outposts**【9†L49-L57】【31†L43-L49】. This trade was *so lucrative* that, as noted, it fueled the Carolina economy and even helped finance the purchase of African slaves later on【9†L65-L73】【9†L67-L75】. Gallay’s research argues that **Carolina’s planters exported Indian slaves to fund the import of Africans** – essentially using one form of human trafficking to enable another【9†L65-L73】【9†L67-L75】.

**The Fur Trade Connection:**  
South Carolina’s backcountry trade in the late 1600s and early 1700s had two main commodities: **deerskins (fur) and Indian slaves**. Colonial records show that **Indian slaves and deerskins were the most important items in trade**, always in demand by Carolina merchants【26†L290-L297】. The trade network involved exchanging European goods (like guns, cloth, metal tools, and especially **rum**) for enslaved Indians and pelts. In fact, the trade in **Indian captives eventually *surpassed* the fur trade in value by around 1700**【31†L43-L49】. South Carolina effectively became a **slave-trading hub**: it acted as a **broker of enslaved Native Americans**, who were captured from as far north as the Carolinas and as far west as the Mississippi, then **shipped out through Charleston’s port**. Meanwhile, South Carolina planters themselves were beginning to cultivate rice and other cash crops, for which they desired **African labor** – Africans had agricultural skills (like rice cultivation knowledge) and were thought to be more tractable in the long run. So South Carolina’s traders would often **trade Indian slaves to plantations in the West Indies in exchange for African slaves or for money to buy Africans**【9†L65-L73】. This **interplay between the two trades** made South Carolina uniquely involved in *both sides* of slavery’s brutal commerce.

**Charleston as a Major Slave Port:**  
By the 18th century, Charleston had grown into the **largest slave port in North America**. It is estimated that **over 40% of all enslaved Africans brought to mainland North America passed through Charleston Harbor**【32†L21-L25】. Charleston’s Wharf was a primary entry point for direct shipments from Africa, especially after Carolina’s economy shifted fully to plantation agriculture post-1715. South Carolina planters and merchants forged tight links with **transatlantic slave traders**; ships from West Africa frequently unloaded human cargoes there. Charleston was so entwined with the Atlantic world that it was “sometimes referred to as part of the West Indies” due to its close economic relationship with the Caribbean【27†L83-L90】. Not only did Charleston receive Africans directly, it also continued to receive **small cargoes via the West Indies** (though less than 20% of slaves brought to SC in the 1700s came through the Caribbean, since most came straight from Africa)【27†L81-L89】.

South Carolina’s legislature actively encouraged the African trade for a time. Notably, **after the American Revolution**, South Carolina was the **only state to officially reopen the African slave trade before the federal ban in 1808**【27†L125-L134】. In the brief window from 1804 to 1808, Charleston brought in a huge wave of Africans: **nearly 40,000** new captives arrived in those five years【27†L125-L133】. This illustrates Charleston’s enduring role as a slave import hub right up to the legal end of the trade. Many of those final imports were quickly sold and moved into the newly expanding cotton and sugar regions of the Deep South (the Old Southwest).

**Indigenous Slavery in South Carolina:**  
Even as African slavery grew, South Carolina *did* continue to have some **Indigenous slaves** or **“servants”** in the 18th century, but their numbers dwindled. By the **1720s, a census in Carolina counted about 1,500 Indian slaves** out of 17,000 total population【5†L129-L138】. This indicates Indigenous people were still enslaved on plantations and households. However, after the 1715 Yamasee War (an Indigenous uprising fueled partly by anger over slave raiding【5†L135-L138】), South Carolina leaders **pulled back from the Indian slave trade**. They passed laws to **limit the enslavement of local Indians** to restore peace【7†L163-L171】. Planters increasingly “focused on increasing their labor force through the purchase of enslaved Africans, who were arriving in greater numbers through the port of Charles Town”【7†L163-L171】. Over time, African slaves so outnumbered Indigenous slaves that **the colonial census stopped differentiating between them**【7†L165-L172】. From the mid-1700s on, the term “Negro” in South Carolina became essentially synonymous with “slave,” and authorities no longer bothered to note who was African versus Indian – **Africans had become the dominant source of enslaved labor**【7†L165-L172】.

It’s also worth noting South Carolina’s connection to the Indian slave trade **beyond its borders**. Carolina slave traders, allied with various tribes like the Westo and later the Yamasee (before the war), conducted raids that **devastated Spanish Florida’s missions** and captured hundreds of Christianized Native Americans there【9†L43-L51】【9†L91-L99】. They also traded slaves with northern colonies: for example, **Pennsylvania passed a law in 1705 forbidding the import of Carolina Indian slaves** because so many were being brought into Pennsylvania (and some of those Natives were from tribes allied with the powerful Iroquois, causing political trouble)【31†L15-L24】. This shows South Carolina’s reach in distributing Indigenous captives across the Thirteen Colonies.

In summary, **South Carolina was a central theater for both forms of bondage**. Initially, it was a **major exporter of Indigenous slaves**, fueling its economy and allowing planters to invest in African slaves. Later, it became the **leading importer of African slaves** on the North American mainland, with Charleston as the key port. The colony’s involvement in the Indian slave trade had far-reaching effects, sparking wars (like the Yamasee War of 1715【11†L106-L113】) and shaping relationships with Native nations. And its involvement in the African trade filled its rice and indigo plantations with enslaved Africans, making South Carolina by 1740 a black-majority society built on slavery. This dual legacy is why South Carolina is sometimes called the **prototype “slave society”** among the colonies【23†L1-L4】 – slavery was foundational to its very existence from the start, whether the slaves were Native or African.

## 4. Contradictions and Revisions in the Records

When piecing together the scale of African vs. Indigenous enslavement, historians encounter **gaps, inconsistencies, and even deliberate revisions** in the historical record. These can sometimes misrepresent the relative impact of African and Native slavery. Here are a few key issues and contradictions:

**– Merging of Racial Categories:** One blatant “revision” in records was the **colonial practice of reclassifying Indigenous people as “Negro” or “Black”** once they were enslaved alongside Africans. For instance, **Virginia’s 1670 law** stated that non-Christian servants imported (whether African or Indian) were to be enslaved for life, and soon after, **some “Black Indians” (mixed or dark-skinned Natives) were simply classified as negroes and forced into slavery**【31†L33-L40】. In South Carolina, as noted, by the late 18th century officials stopped distinguishing African from Indian slaves entirely【7†L165-L172】. An **Indian slave in a South Carolina household by 1750 might just be listed as “Negro” on an inventory**, erasing their Native identity in the paperwork. This means the **documentary record after 1750 undercounts “Indians” and inflates “Negro” as a catch-all category** for enslaved people. It creates a contradiction: *we know* Indians were enslaved, but *the records* after a point don’t show them – they were folded into African-descent population counts. This can mislead later observers into thinking **all** enslaved people were of purely African origin, when in fact a portion had Indigenous roots.

A concrete example is the aftermath of the Yamasee War (1715-1717) in Carolina. After this conflict, the colony curtailed Indian slavery and **“Negro increasingly became synonymous with ‘slave’ in the Lowcountry”**【7†L165-L172】. Thus, any **Indigenous slaves who remained would be counted as negroes** in censuses. The **contradiction in the record** is that a person who might have been listed as “Indian slave” in 1710 would be listed just as “slave” (implying African) in 1750. Historians recognize this and have to adjust their interpretations – for example, genealogists sometimes discover that an ancestor thought to be African-American had in fact some Native ancestry that was unrecorded due to such classification practices.

**– Patchy Documentation of the Indian Slave Trade:** Another major issue is that the **Indian slave trade was not systematically recorded by colonial authorities**【9†L55-L63】. It was often a decentralized, sometimes illegal trade (private traders capturing Natives in the wilderness, or trading guns for captives with certain tribes). **No port records or detailed manifests exist for most Indian shipments**, especially those going out of Charleston to the West Indies in the late 1600s【9†L55-L63】. We largely know about the **scope of Indian enslavement from disparate sources**: correspondences, diplomatic records (e.g. complaints by Spanish or by Indian leaders), fragmentary merchant ledgers, and later recollections. Gallay’s work was likened to a detective “reconstructing a complicated story from disparate and patchy sources”【9†L55-L63】. This means there is **room for debate** and revision on the numbers. Earlier historians often **underestimated** the number of Natives enslaved simply because the data was hidden or scattered. Only in recent decades have scholars like Gallay and Andres Reséndez (author of *The Other Slavery*) brought to light just how vast the Indigenous slavery system was. 

This lack of documentation contrasts with the African trade, which, being an established international commerce, left behind bills of lading, shipping registers, etc. Thus we have exact figures of Africans transported on many voyages, but **only rough estimates for Indian slaves**. This disparity in the records may have led to a **historical narrative overly focused on African slavery**, with Indian slavery being termed an “untold story”【9†L55-L63】. It can appear as a contradiction that historians in the past barely mentioned Indian slaves, whereas now we assert millions were victims – the difference lies in which records were examined and how terms were defined.

**– Exaggeration vs. Reality in African Import Numbers:** On the flip side, some historical narratives (especially older ones or general public memory) assumed that a much larger portion of the African diaspora landed in the U.S. For example, it’s not uncommon to hear statements like “millions of slaves were brought to America,” which **conflates North America with the Americas as a whole**. Only recently has popular understanding caught up to the fact that **only 0.3-0.4 million came directly to the area that became the U.S.**【39†L93-L100】. Even Thomas Jefferson once conjectured higher numbers for the slave trade, not having the data. It’s a bit of an ironic *statistical contradiction* that the U.S. ended up with one of the largest enslaved populations by 1860, yet **had one of the smaller direct imports** of any slaveholding society. The growth was internal – an aspect sometimes underemphasized in telling the story.

Primary records like port arrival lists **confirm the 388,000 import figure**, but this was only firmly compiled in modern times. Previously, American historians often **focused on the 4 million slaves in 1860** and might have assumed a steady large importation. The revelation via the shipping data that the U.S. imports were *comparatively low* can seem to challenge the traditional narrative – but it aligns with the fact that the U.S. had a self-reproducing slave population. This also implies that any theory suggesting that a much larger number of Africans were covertly imported (beyond what logs show) would be hard to square with records; indeed, smuggling after 1808 did occur but on a modest scale.

**– Media and Memory Bias:** There is also an historiographical point: **for centuries, “slavery” in American media and literature was equated with African slavery**, largely ignoring the earlier era of Indian slavery. This **bias in historical memory** is itself a form of revision. For instance, 19th-century writers and even 20th-century historians like Ulrich B. Phillips scarcely mentioned Indigenous slaves when discussing slavery’s origins – they portrayed the plantation system as if it sprang directly from an African basis. The **fact that South Carolina was founded partly as a slave colony with Indians** was downplayed. Only in recent scholarly works do we see the narrative corrected to include that **chattel slavery in Carolina was “built on a preexisting system of Indian slavery”**【31†L39-L47】. In other words, there’s a corrective underway: acknowledging that **enslaved Indians provided a template and early workforce** before Africans arrived in great numbers【31†L37-L45】.

**– Conflicting Contemporary Accounts:** Some contemporaneous records themselves show confusion or contradictions about who was considered “Negro” or “Indian.” For example, Spanish records from Florida might refer to a fugitive slave from Carolina as “a negro from Carolina” even if that person was ethnically an Indian – because to the Spanish, if he was enslaved by the English, he was a “negro” (since the English were known to enslave Africans)【31†L33-L40】. Or English colonists sometimes used **“Mustee” or “Mulatto”** to describe people of mixed Indian and African ancestry, but those terms were inconsistently applied. In South Carolina, a **“mustee” was often a person with one white and one mixed-black parent (i.e. 1/4 black, 1/4 Indian, 1/2 white, or similar)** – a very muddled category. Such terms in advertisements and laws could be confusing, and later readers might misinterpret them, affecting population estimates of Indians vs Africans. 

**– The Disappearance of Indians from Slave Records:** The most poignant contradiction is the near **invisibility of Indigenous slaves in later U.S. records**, which belies their very real presence in earlier times. By the 19th century, virtually all enslaved people enumerated in censuses are listed as “Black” or “Mulatto.” Yet, we know some percentage had Native forebears. One reason is that many Indigenous people who were enslaved were eventually absorbed into African-American communities or, if freed, into Native or mixed communities that weren’t recognized by white record-keepers. The historical narrative “revised” them out by omission. This has led some modern observers to speculate or question: *Were far more African Americans actually of Native descent than acknowledged?* While DNA and genealogical research do show a lot of mixture, the truth is that both things are true – there was a **significant transatlantic African slave trade**, and there was also a **significant domestic Indigenous slave trade**, and they intertwined. The records were **“cleaned up” to show only one story (African) for much of U.S. history, which is a distortion by simplification**.

**Conclusion – Gaps and Inconsistencies:**  
The primary records, when scrutinized, **both verify and challenge** the simplistic idea that all enslaved people in the U.S. came directly from Africa. They verify African origins in that we have hard data on hundreds of thousands being shipped from Africa, with African names, ethnicities, and skills evident in many accounts. They challenge the narrative in that they reveal a **huge, often overlooked Indigenous slavery component**, especially in the 17th-early 18th century, and they show how record-keeping practices obscured that fact over time. Key inconsistencies include how different colonies defined who was enslaveable by race (e.g. sometimes exempting “friendly” Indians, other times not), how categories like “Negro” expanded to cover anyone enslaved regardless of origin【7†L165-L172】, and how historical accounts minimized the Indian slave trade until recent research “cracked the case wide open”【11†L130-L138】.

In sum, **slave laws, ship logs, ads, and news reports are pieces of a puzzle**. When assembled, they depict a complex picture: From 1619 onward, Africans were imported and enslaved, but alongside them, **indigenous people were also being enslaved in large numbers** – something clearly documented in early laws and sale records. South Carolina stands out as a nexus where these two systems overlapped dramatically, with Indians being a *currency* to acquire Africans【9†L65-L73】. Over time, the success of the African plantation system (and the ravages of disease/war on Native populations) led to an **“Africanization” of slavery** – and the historical record “forgets” the Indigenous aspect, treating African slavery as the whole story. Recognizing the gaps and silences in the records is crucial: there are **whole shipments of human beings (Native Americans) that went unrecorded or lost in archives**, and there are **entire categories of people relabeled to fit a racial caste system** that only acknowledged African ancestry.

By conducting a **deep investigation into these primary records**, we highlight these inconsistencies and bring a fuller truth to light. It becomes evident that the **African origins of enslaved people in the U.S. are well documented in shipping records and name evidence**, but the scale of a parallel **Indigenous slave trade** – while harder to quantify – was significant and is verified by many colonial sources (albeit scattered). The historical narrative is now being revised to integrate this knowledge: slavery in America was not monolithic and exclusively African in origin; it was a multiethnic atrocity, and **the archives, when read against the grain, reveal those multiple layers**.

# Reclassification of Indigenous Slaves as “African” in Colonial America

**Overview:**  
Historical evidence suggests that some Indigenous people enslaved in colonial America were **bureaucratically reclassified as “African”** (or “Negro”) rather than being openly recorded as Native American captives. This section-by-section analysis examines port records, naming conventions, economic motives, legal changes, and demographic data to highlight inconsistencies in the historical record. These inconsistencies support the theory that a portion of the **rise in “African” slave numbers reflected paperwork changes** – rebranding Native slaves as African – **rather than solely mass importation from Africa**.

## 1. Slave Ship Records and Reported Origins  
**Port logs** from key slave-trading centers (Charleston, various Virginia ports, New York, etc.) reveal discrepancies in the reported origins of enslaved people. In multiple cases, **Indigenous captives were logged as “Negro” or “African”** when disembarked in colonial ports: 

- **Virginia (1710–1718):** Official lists of *“Negroes” imported by sea* included Native Americans. One analysis found *“at least sixty-nine ‘Indians,’ mostly from the Carolinas”* counted among “Negroes” brought into Virginia during 1710–1718【44†L188-L194】. In other words, Indians captured in the Carolinas were shipped to Virginia and recorded as if they were African slaves. This indicates a deliberate mislabeling of Native people as Africans in port documentation. 

- **New York (1715–1736):** Similarly, New York importation records from 1715–1736 show slaves arriving from places like **Campeche, Jamaica, Honduras, the Carolinas, and Virginia** – locations in the Americas – yet they were listed as *“Negroes”*【44†L189-L193】. Many of these captives were explicitly noted to be of **American (Indigenous) ancestry**, despite being classified under the African slave category【44†L189-L193】. This suggests New York’s slave trade included a significant *intra-American traffic*: enslaved Natives from other colonies or the Caribbean sold as “Negro” slaves.

- **Charleston, South Carolina:** Charleston was a major colonial slave port that not only **imported Africans** but also **exported Indigenous people**. During the late 1600s and early 1700s, the Carolinas aggressively enslaved local tribes, and Charleston’s records reflect this. For example, a 1708 census in South Carolina counted **approximately 1,400 Native American slaves alongside 3,000 African slaves**【38†L139-L147】. Indian slaves were frequently shipped out of Charleston to other colonies or the West Indies【38†L139-L147】. After 1715 (the Yamasee War, discussed later), the colony largely stopped openly trading Indians, which likely led to **remaining Indigenous slaves being absorbed into African slave rolls**. In 1719, South Carolina even passed a law to clarify terminology: *“all such slaves as are not entirely Indian shall be accounted as negro”*【42†L215-L223】. This legal directive meant that any person of mixed or uncertain ancestry would officially be listed as *Negro*. **All subsequent slave inventories in S.C. combined many Natives or mixed-race individuals under the “negro” category by design【42†L215-L223】**. Thus, Charleston shipping manifests post-1720 show a sharp “rise” in African slave numbers while Indian slaves vanish from ledgers – a strong clue of reclassification on paper rather than a complete demographic replacement.

**Summary:** Across multiple ports, **ships’ manifests and customs lists did not always reflect actual geographic origin**. Enslaved Indigenous people from North America and the Caribbean were often recorded as *“African”* or *“Negro”* when they arrived in English colonies【44†L188-L194】【44†L189-L193】. These discrepancies in port records highlight a pattern of **Native slaves being relabeled as Africans**, masking the true scale of Indigenous enslavement and making African import figures appear higher.

## 2. Naming Patterns of Enslaved People  
Another inconsistency arises in the **names** given for enslaved individuals in colonial records. If slaves were truly newly imported from Africa in large numbers, one would expect many to have African names or unfamiliar names, at least initially. Instead, historians have found instances of purported *“newly arrived Africans”* already bearing **European names and speaking English**, which aligns more with Native or Creole slaves who grew up in colonial society. Key examples include:

- **English/Christian Names at Arrival:** It was noted that some slaves described as “just arrived” from Africa already had typical English names. For instance, one Massachusetts record recounts the sale of *“a negro boy just arrived from Africa, who married the girl Sabina”*【20†L233-L240】. The boy is identified by the name **“Ned”** – an English nickname – at the moment of purchase【20†L233-L239】. It is unlikely an African child fresh off a slave ship would already be called “Ned” except as an assigned name. This hints that either he was quickly renamed by traders prior to sale, **or he was not straight from Africa at all** (possibly born in the Americas). Similarly, a 1748 New York newspaper advertised *“a Negro man servant called **Robbin**, almost of the complexion of an Indian… talks good English, can read and write, and plays on the fiddle.”*【42†L197-L203】. Described as newly for sale, this man not only has an English name (“Robbin/Robin”) but also speaks fluent English and has skills unlikely for someone just off a slave ship. His *“complexion…almost…Indian”* further suggests he may **have been an Indigenous or mixed-ancestry man passed off as “Negro”**【42†L199-L203】.

- **“Robin the Indian” Becomes “Robin the Negro”:** Colonial parish and inventory records show individuals’ racial designations changing over time without change in identity. In Virginia’s King William’s Parish, an enslaved man was listed one year as *“Robin an Indian”* and, two years later, as *“Robin a negro.”*【42†L195-L203】. He had an English name (“Robin”) throughout. The only thing that changed was the label from Indian to Negro. This is a clear **instance of reclassification** in the records: the same person, originally recorded as a Native American slave, was later categorized as an African slave. Likewise, a 1691 York County (VA) slave list explicitly included a woman labeled *“Kate – Indian”* among the slaves, yet by 1728 an inventory of *“Negroes”* on a Virginia plantation included an *“Indian Robin.”*【42†L195-L203】 In other words, Indian slaves like Kate and Robin were being recorded interchangeably or simultaneously under the Negro/slave listings. Their familiar English names (“Kate,” “Robin”) underscore that these were people integrated into colonial life (likely American-born or long in the colonies), not fresh arrivals from Africa.

- **Early 1619 Arrivals with Iberian Names:** Even the very first group of African-descended people in Virginia (1619) shows hints of non-African origins or prior acculturation. Of the “twenty and odd” Africans delivered to Jamestown in 1619, **at least eleven carried Spanish or Portuguese names**【44†L172-L177】. Contemporary accounts list names like *Antonio* (several men) and *John Pedro*【44†L172-L179】. These captives had been taken from a Spanish slave ship, suggesting they were from Angola originally – however, their Spanish names imply they had been baptized or raised in a Spanish colonial context. Historian Jack D. Forbes goes further to suggest some of these individuals *“could well have been of part-American (Indigenous) ancestry”*, since Spain often integrated or enslaved Native Americans in its colonies【44†L172-L179】. While direct evidence of their ancestry is lacking, the presence of European names and language among supposed “Africans” in 1619 already complicates the picture of who was classified as “Negro.” It shows that from the very start, **enslaved peoples’ identities were often blurred – many so-called Africans had already been in the New World and some may have had Indigenous heritage or creole upbringing**.

**Summary:** The naming and cultural markers in slave records expose contradictions. Enslaved people advertised as *“new Africans”* sometimes had **pre-assigned Anglo names, spoke fluent English, or had Christian backgrounds**, which is inconsistent with a direct-from-Africa origin. These patterns make sense if a number of those recorded as “African” were in fact **Indigenous captives (or American-born mixed people)** being folded into the African slave population on paper. The reuse of common English names like *Robin, Tom, Kate,* etc., for “imported” slaves and documented cases of individuals switching from Indian to Negro designation strongly support reclassification practices【42†L195-L203】.

## 3. Economic Incentives for Reclassifying Native Slaves  
Colonial authorities and slaveholders had compelling **economic and pragmatic reasons** to reclassify Indigenous slaves as African. Enslaving local Native Americans was initially common and profitable, but over time it became politically problematic and sometimes legally restricted – yet the *demand for labor* remained. Rather than forgo enslaving Indigenous people altogether, colonists often found it **beneficial to hide or relabel this practice**. Key factors include:

- **Legal Prohibitions and Loopholes:** In some empires, **enslaving Indigenous people was officially outlawed** fairly early, forcing colonists to find loopholes. Notably, the **Spanish Crown banned Indian slavery outright in 1542**, declaring that *“no Indian can be made a slave under any circumstance”*【49†L39-L47】. Despite this clear law, Spanish colonists across the Americas **quietly continued enslaving Natives** by exploiting legal gray areas and redefinitions of status【49†L47-L55】【49†L49-L57】. They used devices like encomienda (forced labor drafts), “just war” enslavement claims, debt peonage, or simply labeled Indians as mestizos or morenos to evade the letter of the law【49†L49-L58】. **This legal obfuscation allowed Spaniards to profit from Indigenous labor while seeming to comply with the ban**【49†L49-L58】. In British colonies, explicit bans on Indian slavery were less uniform, but there were periods and places where authorities discouraged or limited the practice (often to keep peace with tribes or because they preferred African labor). For example, after 1715’s Yamasee War – a brutal conflict largely sparked by Native grievances over slave-raiding – South Carolina began backing away from openly enslaving local Indians【33†L93-L100】. The economic incentive then was to **avoid future costly wars**: reclassifying or quietly trading remaining Indian captives as “Negroes” allowed slavery to continue without constantly inciting Indigenous resistance.

- **Alliances and Diplomacy:** Colonial powers also had **diplomatic incentives** to cease openly enslaving Native peoples. As colonies grew, Europeans needed alliances with certain tribes (for the fur trade, as military allies against rival colonies, etc.). Continually raiding those same groups for slaves was counterproductive. Thus, agreements were often made to stop enslavement of allied tribes. However, colonies still **faced labor shortages**, especially on plantations. The workaround was often to **import African captives or use Indians from other regions**, and then **classify all slaves simply by the catch-all “Negro” category**. This way, a colony could claim it no longer enslaved “our Indians” even as it quietly absorbed other Indigenous captives. Economic logic favored this subterfuge: **enslaved Africans were expensive to purchase and import** across the Atlantic, whereas **capturing Indigenous people locally (or obtaining them via intercolonial trade) was relatively low-cost**. By re-labeling an Indigenous slave as African, an owner could increase their labor force without incurring the high expense of a transatlantic purchase. Essentially, **it was a cost-saving measure** – one could acquire a “forbidden” Native slave and simply pretend they were part of the African slave inventory.

- **Maintaining Slave Supply:** During certain periods (late 17th – early 18th century), the growth of plantation economies outpaced the direct importation of Africans. Colonies like the Carolinas **supplemented their workforce with Native American slaves** to meet demand. Historian Allan Gallay and others have shown that **thousands of Indigenous people were sold into bondage out of the American Southeast**, often in exchange for African slaves or credit. For instance, Carolina merchants in the early 1700s would ship captured local Indians to West Indies markets and **receive African slaves in return** – a triangular trade within the Americas. Reclassification came into play especially when those Indian captives were kept or moved within English colonies: once separated from their home context, they might simply be recorded as “Negro servants.” This *fiction* helped planters justify their labor force. The economic motive is clear: by **classifying Indigenous laborers as African, enslavers could maximize their human property under the cover of legality and social acceptability**.

- **Examples of Economic Statements:** South Carolina planter Samuel Eveleigh, in 1735, underscored the necessity of non-white labor by saying rice cultivation *“can’t be produced by white people… the whites can’t work in the wet… as Negroes do”*【38†L139-L147】. By the 1730s, South Carolina had shifted to a predominantly African slave labor force【38†L139-L147】, but this transition was smoothed by earlier reliance on Natives. When Indigenous slaves became harder to obtain openly (due to disease and Indian wars), colonists eagerly imported more Africans. However, **some of the apparent “increase” in Africans was likely the formal reclassification of surviving Indians**. The economic incentive was to protect the investment: a slave-owner with Native slaves wouldn’t want to lose them if laws or treaties said “no Indian slavery,” so it was financially preferable to **paper them over as African** and thus maintain the status quo on the plantation.

**Summary:** Colonial powers had **strong incentives to reclassify rather than release Indigenous slaves**. Legally, it kept them out of trouble as anti-Indian-slavery sentiments grew, and economically it allowed them to continue exploiting a readily available labor source without paying the premium for imported Africans. The result was a bookkeeping trick: **Native Americans in bondage became “Africans” on manifests and ledgers**, enabling colonists to claim a growing African workforce even when part of that workforce was locally acquired. This benefited colonial economies while sidestepping moral, legal, or diplomatic fallout.

## 4. Legal Shifts and the “Rise” in African Numbers  
Throughout the colonial era, **laws and administrative practices around slavery evolved** – and these changes often coincided with statistical shifts in the recorded slave population. In particular, as colonies *phased out overt Indigenous slavery*, they simultaneously reported *increases in the African slave population*. This timing suggests that **bureaucratic relabeling was a factor**. Key legal and policy shifts include:

- **Gradual Outlawing of Indian Slavery:** In British America, no single law eliminated Native slavery across all colonies, but many regions took steps that effectively ended formal Indian enslavement. For example, New England colonies by the early 18th century had largely stopped keeping Indians as lifetime slaves (replacing it with indenture or transporting captives out of colony). In the South, **the aftermath of the Yamasee War (1715)** marked a turning point. South Carolina, which had aggressively traded Indian slaves before, **“gradually disassociated” from the Indigenous slave trade after 1717**【33†L93-L100】. Colonial officials realized that continuing to enslave local tribes would lead to constant warfare and instability. Thus, by the 1720s, South Carolina and neighboring colonies were officially relying on African slavery. **On paper, Indian slavery vanished** – but many Native people were still held in bondage unofficially or in other colonies.

- **Redefinition by Racial Laws:** Some colonies explicitly redefined racial categories in their laws to absorb Indians into the “Negro” population. A striking example is **South Carolina’s 1719 law** mentioned earlier: it declared that *“all such slaves as are not entirely Indian shall be accounted as Negro”*【42†L215-L223】. This legal language meant that **any person with mixed ancestry (part Indian, part African or even part European) would be categorized as Negro** for tax and record-keeping purposes. The *immediate effect* was that **official slave censuses in South Carolina after 1720 showed almost all slaves as “Negro,” even if many had Native heritage【42†L215-L223】**. It artificially “boosted” the count of Africans while reducing the count of Indians to near zero – a purely bureaucratic adjustment. In New Jersey, a similar pattern occurred: colonial ads and documents from the mid-1700s reveal that people of mixed Indian and African blood were simply termed *“negroes”* in practice【42†L223-L231】【42†L236-L243】. Virginia’s laws, as early as 1705, classified **anyone with an Indian parent and white parent as “Mulatto,”** effectively removing such individuals from the “Indian” category legally【24†L27-L35】. Over time, even “Mulatto” itself came to be seen as a type of “colored” or black person in the caste system. **These laws systematically reclassified persons of Indigenous descent into the African/Black category**, which directly influenced the statistics of how many “Negro slaves” a colony reported. 

- **Coinciding Numerical Shifts:** The legal/terminological shifts correlate with sudden changes in recorded demographics. In **South Carolina**, the colony that had an enormous Indian slave trade, the official numbers tell a story: in 1708 there were roughly *1,400 Indian slaves* recorded (and 3,000 Africans)【38†L139-L147】. But a few decades later, South Carolina’s records show virtually *no Indians* – instead, a steep rise in the enslaved African population (Africans became the vast majority of slaves by the 1730s)【38†L139-L147】. While part of this is due to new importation of Africans, it’s also true that many Indian slaves **died from disease or were sold out of colony** by that time【38†L139-L147】. Crucially, any **remaining Native enslaved people would no longer be counted as “Indian” after 1719** due to the law – they would appear in the African count. So the *official* African slave population climbed not only from imports but also because **Indigenous slaves were being folded into that category**. We see a similar phenomenon in places like Virginia: by the late 18th century, Virginia leaders claimed that tribes like the Gingaskin were “nearly extinct” and that their reservation had become an *“asylum for free negroes.”*【46†L257-L266】 This was during the push to terminate the reservation (circa 1800). In reality, many Gingaskin people were still there, but officials **chose to classify them as black** rather than Indian to justify taking their land【46†L257-L265】【46†L268-L272】. This shows how **legal classification followed political expediency** – erasing Native identity on paper to serve colonial interests.

- **Paper vs. Reality:** In summary, the **“rise” in African slave numbers in colonial records often coincided with legal measures that suppressed the acknowledgement of Native slaves.** Instead of a purely demographic increase from imports, part of the increase was a *statistical artifact* of re-labeling. Laws that barred Indian slavery, or defined anyone with any African ancestry as Negro, meant that enslaved Indigenous people (especially of mixed background or those held illicitly) would henceforth be **counted as African in registers**【42†L215-L223】. This creates a *paper trail* suggesting a growing African slave population exactly as the Indian slave population “disappeared” – a strong sign of reclassification at work.

## 5. Survival of Indigenous People and Absorption into “African” Classification  
One of the central questions is whether Indigenous populations were truly **wiped out or “decimated”** in areas where they stopped appearing in records – or whether they **survived and persisted, but under new labels**. By examining census data, tax records, and community histories, we find evidence that many Native Americans were **never truly gone; they were living within colonial society, identified as Black or mixed-race**. This suggests continuity hidden by nomenclature:

- **Census and Tax Records:** Early U.S. and late colonial censuses often did *not count Native Americans* (especially those not on reservations) as a separate category. For instance, prior to the mid-19th century, census enumerators east of the Mississippi had basically three racial categories: white, black, or mulatto (and Indians “not taxed” were usually *not* listed at all)【24†L39-L47】【24†L43-L49】. Any person with a lifestyle integrated into colonial society (thus taxable) could not be labeled “Indian” without contradicting the premise that “Indians not taxed” are invisible. Thus, an **Indigenous person living among colonists would likely be recorded as free colored (mulatto/Negro)** in census or tax lists【24†L39-L47】【24†L43-L49】. This means that even as Native American populations *appear* to plummet to zero in official counts, many were simply being **absorbed into the “colored” population counts**. In Virginia, for example, by the 1800s, remnants of tribes were almost entirely classified as colored. A 1785 Virginia statute held that anyone with at least one-quarter African blood was “Negro,” but conversely many mixed Native people fell under the “mulatto” or “Negro” definition as well【23†L1-L9】【24†L27-L35】. The result is that **Indigenous ancestry was erased in records**, though the people themselves and their bloodlines endured.

- **Community Reclassification:** There are documented cases of whole Native communities being reclassified over time. The **Accawmacke (Eastern Shore of Virginia) Indians**, for instance, largely lost official recognition and were labeled “Negro” or “Mulatto” by local authorities by the 19th century【3†L200-L209】. Genealogical research has shown many of them were *“nearly or completely full-blooded Indians,”* yet Virginia law had them recorded as colored【3†L200-L209】. The Gingaskin Reservation example (Nottingham Indians of Virginia’s Eastern Shore) illustrates this: in the 1780s whites claimed only “3 or 4 genuine Indians” remained and that the rest of the reservation’s people were “idle… free negroes”【46†L257-L265】. On that basis, the state dissolved the reservation. However, those people did not vanish – they and their descendants **continued to live in the area, simply categorized as Black** landowners going forward【46†L268-L276】【46†L269-L272】. In Delaware, the so-called **“Moors” and Nanticoke** Indian descendants were enumerated as “Negro” or “mulatto” for generations, obscuring their Native identity in records (a pattern not corrected until the later 20th century in some cases). Such cases confirm that **Indigenous people were often *administratively merged* into the African American populace**, even when they maintained a distinct heritage in private. 

- **Marriage and Mixed Families:** Another mechanism of absorption was the formation of mixed Native-African families, whose children were usually classified as Black. Enslaved Africans and Indigenous people frequently lived and labored together, leading to intermarriage. Under colonial slave codes (following the principle of partus sequitur ventrem), the children of these unions inherited the mother’s status – and if the mother was a slave (African or Indian), the child became a slave. But what *label* that child received was a choice made by owners or record-keepers. Many mixed-ancestry slaves were simply called *“mulatto”* or *“negro.”* An example from New Jersey: an ad for two runaways in 1734 describes one man as *“half Indian and half Negro… speaks good English and his country [is] Indian.”*【42†L223-L231】 He is not explicitly called Negro, but a later 1747 ad in the same region refers to a 53-year-old *“Negro fellow named Sampson…had some Indian blood in him”* and a 12-year-old boy named Sam *“born of an Indian woman, looks like an Indian…they both talk Indian very well.”*【42†L229-L237】 Despite their obvious Native heritage (they even speak an Indigenous language), the advertisement still primarily labels them as Negro slaves. By the Revolutionary era, it was routine that **people of mixed African-Native ancestry were simply grouped as blacks**【42†L244-L250】. For instance, the son of an African-descent father and a Delaware Indian mother – Cyrus Bustill’s son, who became a prominent Quaker – was socially regarded as a “Negro”【46†L244-L252】. A 1778 wanted notice from New Jersey explicitly lumps an entire family together as *“the said negroes and [an] Indian”* – the wife and child are called Negroes, while the husband is noted as an Indian man (Lewis)【46†L236-L243】. This shows how a family that was actually part-Indian ended up mostly classified as Negro. **In slavery and freedom, the one-drop rule (even before it was formally codified) pushed Indigenous lineage into the background**, favoring an African/black identification.

- **Population Not Actually “Extinct”:** Where records imply Indians “died out,” a closer look often reveals **survival through absorption**. The drastic decline of Native counts in colonial records was not solely due to mortality. Certainly, disease and war took a heavy toll on Indigenous nations, and many areas did see steep population drops. But the notion that *all* Eastern Woodlands Indians vanished is disproven by the continued presence (often undocumented) of Native-descended people. Many were hiding in plain sight within the “colored” communities of the colonies and later states. For example, South Carolina’s 1,400 Indian slaves in 1708 did suffer from epidemics like smallpox soon after【38†L139-L147】【38†L141-L148】, yet not every Native person died. A number became servants or slaves in other colonies (some Carolina Indians were sold to Virginia, New York, or Caribbean plantations). Those individuals and their offspring were recorded as part of the African slave population thereafter【44†L188-L194】【44†L190-L193】. In New England, the remnant Nipmuc, Wampanoag, and other tribal members often lived among free blacks by the 1750s, usually listed as mulatto or black in church and town records. The **persistence of Indigenous DNA and oral traditions in African American communities** today attests to this absorption. What the paper trail shows as a *decline to zero* was in part a **statistical illusion caused by reclassification**.

**Summary:** Indigenous people in colonial America were not universally exterminated or completely absent; many **survived by blending into (or being forced into) the African-American population**. Colonial record-keeping practices and laws meant that these Native survivors were **counted as Black** – whether enslaved or free. Thus, when we see African slave numbers skyrocketing and Indian numbers dwindling in records, we must consider that **part of that “African” population was actually of Native American origin, hiding in plain sight under a different label**. The continuity of Indigenous bloodlines under the “African” designation is a powerful indicator that reclassification occurred on a broad scale.

## **Conclusion**  
The historical evidence – from **port logs and slave inventories to naming anomalies, legal statutes, and demographic patterns** – consistently points to a significant *paper reclassification* of Indigenous enslaved people as “African” during colonial times. This does not deny that hundreds of thousands of Africans were brutally transported to America, but it **challenges the assumption that every enslaved person labeled “African” in colonial records in fact came directly from Africa**. In many cases, colonists found it convenient or necessary to categorize Native Americans as Negroes, thereby bolstering African slave numbers in the archives without actually increasing the transatlantic importation.

**Contradictions and inconsistencies** in the record highlight this phenomenon:

- Ships’ manifests and community records that **counted Native Americans within totals of “Negro” slaves**【44†L188-L194】【42†L215-L223】.
- Enslaved individuals advertised as African who clearly had **local birth, Christian names, and spoke English**【42†L197-L203】.
- Sudden legal changes that **erased the Indian category and inflated the Negro category**【42†L215-L223】.
- Communities of proven Native descent that **ended up classified as colored** and thereby “disappeared” as Indians【46†L257-L265】【46†L268-L276】.
- Statistical leaps in African slave numbers that **outpace documented importation**, explained by the inclusion of Indigenous people【44†L183-L191】【44†L189-L193】.

All of these suggest that a **bureaucratic sleight-of-hand** was at work alongside genuine importation. Colonial society underwent a reframing where “slave” came to be synonymous with “African,” and any enslaved person – regardless of origin – was pushed into that category. In effect, the **Indigenous slave trade went underground**: it was not so much abolished as obscured within the larger African slave trade.

By critically examining the records, modern researchers like Jack D. Forbes have concluded that **a considerable number of “Africans” in early America were actually Native Americans in whole or part**【44†L183-L191】【42†L244-L250】. This hidden history alters our understanding of both African American and Native American ancestry, and it uncovers a deliberate strategy by colonial powers to maximize control over all enslaved peoples through classification. The mass **“importation” of Africans was real, but it was augmented and at times overstated by counting indigenous captives as Africans**. In sum, the rise of African slavery in the Americas was not a simple replacement of a “vanished” Native population, but rather a complex process in which many Native Americans were **enslaved under a new identity** – effectively disappearing on paper even as they lived on in body and lineage within African American communities【46†L257-L265】【46†L268-L276】.

When setting up a book for electronic or print publication, it’s helpful to follow industry conventions so that your work looks polished and professional. Below is a general guide covering layout, font choices, and sizes, which you can adjust based on your specific needs and the final trim size.

---

## 1. Trim Size and Page Setup

### Print Publications
- **Common Trim Sizes (in inches)**
  - 6″ x 9″ (trade paperback)
  - 5.5″ x 8.5″
  - 8.5″ x 11″ (more common for textbooks or workbooks)
- **Margins**: For a 6″ x 9″ book, a typical starting point might be:
  - **Outer margins**: 0.75″ to 1″
  - **Gutter (inner) margin**: 0.75″ to 1″, or slightly larger if the book is thick (to accommodate binding)
  - **Top and bottom**: 0.75″ to 1″  
  Ensure your chosen print-on-demand service or printer’s guidelines are met (they may require slightly different minimum margins).

### Electronic Publications (eBooks)
- eBooks are **reflowable**, meaning the reader’s device can alter font size, line spacing, and sometimes font family. As a result, you don’t have to fix a strict “page” layout. Instead, focus on **clean, semantic formatting** (e.g., consistent use of heading tags, paragraph styles, etc.).
- Include **a responsive cover image** that looks good at both thumbnail and full-screen sizes.
- For eBooks, it’s best to stick to a single standard font or let the user’s device default to their preferred font.  

---

## 2. Font Choices

### Print

1. **Body Text**  
   - **Serif fonts** are standard in print for readability and a classic look. Examples include:
     - **Garamond** (elegant and compact, popular in trade publishing)
     - **Times New Roman** (common, though some find it overused)
     - **Palatino** or **Book Antiqua** (slightly wider, good readability)
     - **Georgia** (modern but still quite readable in print)
   - Font sizes typically range from **10 pt to 12 pt** for body text. The sweet spot is often **11 pt** in a standard 6″ x 9″ layout.

2. **Headings and Chapter Titles**
   - You can stay with the same serif font, using **bold** or a larger size for emphasis (e.g., 14–16 pt).
   - Alternatively, use a **contrasting sans serif** for headings, such as **Helvetica**, **Arial**, or **Futura**. This helps headings stand out.
   - Keep headings consistent across chapters (same font, size, style).

3. **Line Spacing (Leading)**
   - In print, **1.15–1.2** line spacing often works well for a clean, readable page.
   - If using 11 pt text, your line spacing might be around 13–14 pt.

4. **Special Sections (Quotes, Sidebars)**
   - Pull quotes or block quotes can be set **1 point smaller** than body text or in italics, with slightly wider margins.
   - For any specialized text (footnotes, endnotes, etc.), ensure it’s at least 9 pt for legibility.

### Electronic (eBooks)

1. **Body Text**  
   - eReaders allow the user to adjust font size. As an author, you typically **don’t fix a font size** in the ePub/Kindle file. Instead, you might define a base font size (like 1em in CSS), which the device interprets.
   - A popular choice is to specify a general **serif** (e.g., Georgia, Times) or **sans serif** (e.g., Arial, Helvetica) in your stylesheet, but keep in mind many devices override it.

2. **Headings**  
   - Use HTML heading tags (`<h1>`, `<h2>`, etc.) to structure your text. Let the eReader handle the scaling.
   - Keep bold or italic styling minimal so that it remains clear on smaller screens.

3. **Line Spacing**  
   - Similar approach: define a default line-height in the CSS (like 1.2–1.4). Readers can still adjust as they wish.

---

## 3. General Design Considerations

1. **Paragraphs**  
   - Indented paragraphs are standard in print. Typically, an indent of about **0.25″–0.3″** works well at a 6″ x 9″ trim.
   - In eBooks, paragraph formatting often defaults to either first-line indent or block paragraphs with spacing. Avoid forcing large custom indents in the ePub’s CSS.

2. **Widows and Orphans**  
   - **Widows** (a single line at the bottom of a page) and **orphans** (a single line at the top of a page) can be distracting. Most professional layout software (e.g., InDesign) has controls to prevent these.
   - Check your final PDF or proof copy to ensure paragraphs aren’t broken awkwardly.

3. **Chapter Openings**  
   - In print, it’s common to start each chapter on a **right-hand (odd) page**, possibly with a drop cap or small caps for the first line.
   - For eBooks, keep it simple: a heading with an `<h1>` tag, maybe a small vertical space, then the text. Don’t rely on page-based design elements that won’t translate well to reflowable text.

4. **Images and Graphics**  
   - For print, ensure images are **300 dpi** at actual print size for clarity.
   - For eBooks, images should be optimized for file size while remaining legible on various screen sizes. Usually 72–150 dpi is sufficient, but scaling is dynamic.

5. **Front Matter and Back Matter**  
   - Standard sections in a print book include a title page, copyright page, dedication, table of contents, acknowledgments, etc.
   - In eBooks, a **linked Table of Contents** is crucial for navigation. Tools like Kindle Previewer or ePubCheck can help ensure everything is linked properly.

6. **Software**  
   - For print layout, **Adobe InDesign** is industry standard. **Affinity Publisher** or **Microsoft Word** (with caution) can be used if you manage your styles carefully.
   - For eBooks, you can export from InDesign to ePub or use specialized tools like **Vellum (Mac)**, **Calibre**, or even direct Word-to-Kindle conversion. Clean, consistent styling is key.

---

## 4. Practical Recommendations

- **For Print (6″ x 9″)**  
  - **Font**: Garamond 11 pt (body), 14–16 pt (chapter titles), 12–14 pt (subheadings).  
  - **Line Spacing**: 1.2 (about 13–14 pt leading).  
  - **Margins**: 0.75″ outer, 0.9″ gutter (adjust if high page count).  
  - **Paragraph Indent**: 0.25″.  
  - **Avoid**: overly fancy display fonts in the main text; keep the interior clean and professional.

- **For eBooks**  
  - **Base Font**: Let the device handle user preference; specify a fallback like `font-family: serif;`.  
  - **CSS**: Keep it minimal. For instance:  
    ```css
    body { font-size: 1em; line-height: 1.4; margin: 0; }
    h1 { font-size: 1.4em; margin-top: 1em; }
    ```
  - **Structure**: Use heading tags for chapters, proper metadata, and a logical table of contents.  
  - **Test**: Check your file on multiple devices (Kindle, iPad, phone) to ensure reflow and readability.

---

### Final Thoughts

- **Consistency is key.** Pick a font combination and layout approach, then use it throughout.  
- **Proof extensively**. Print a proof copy or preview your eBook on various devices to catch issues with spacing, font rendering, or broken headings.  
- **Follow publisher guidelines**. If you use a print-on-demand service (KDP Print, IngramSpark, etc.), always follow their margin, bleed, and file format requirements. If you’re publishing an eBook, adhere to the platform’s best practices (Amazon KDP, Apple Books, etc.).

By selecting appropriate fonts (serif for print, device defaults for eBooks), a comfortable size (10–12 pt for print body text), and consistent formatting, you’ll produce a professional-looking publication that’s easy to read and meets industry standards.
