The manger in the nativity scene holds significant symbolic meaning. On the surface, it appears as a simple feeding trough where animals are kept, but its deeper implications go far beyond its humble nature.

First, the manger represents humility. Jesus, the King of kings, was not born in a palace or even a proper home. Instead, he entered the world in a place reserved for animals. This setting signals that the Messiah was not here for the powerful or the elite, but for the lowly and oppressed, aligning with the broader theme of Jesus' mission to overturn the established order.

Additionally, the manger is often connected to the idea of spiritual nourishment. Just as animals come to the manger for physical sustenance, so too do people come to Christ for spiritual nourishment. This idea ties into Jesus later identifying himself as the "bread of life" — offering sustenance to those who follow him. Being laid in a manger foreshadows that he would become the source of life and sustenance for the people.

The manger, therefore, is not just a humble location, but a prophetic symbol. It points to the larger themes of Christ’s purpose: to nourish, to humble, and to upend the expectations of what a king and savior should look like. The presence of two donkeys in the constellation of the manger has an ancient and symbolic significance that connects to both biblical prophecy and cultural myths. In astronomical terms, this refers to the two stars **Asellus Borealis** (the Northern Donkey) and **Asellus Australis** (the Southern Donkey), which sit near the Beehive Cluster in the constellation Cancer. These two stars are often referred to as the “donkeys” and have been interpreted in various mythological and religious traditions, including biblical narratives.

### Historical and Mythological Background of the Two Donkeys

In Greek and Roman mythology, the two donkeys were said to represent the animals that the god Dionysus and the satyr Silenus rode into battle against the Titans. According to the legend, the loud braying of the donkeys startled the Titans and helped win the battle for the gods, leading to their place among the stars. The stars were then named Asellus Borealis and Asellus Australis, the celestial donkeys.

However, their symbolism extends into biblical and prophetic narratives as well.

### The Donkeys in Biblical Context

In the biblical tradition, donkeys are often symbolic of humility, service, and prophetic fulfillment. One of the clearest connections comes from the story of **Zechariah’s prophecy** (Zechariah 9:9) in which the Messiah is foretold to enter Jerusalem “riding on a donkey.” This prophecy is fulfilled when Jesus rides into Jerusalem on a donkey on Palm Sunday (Matthew 21:1-5), which is seen as a declaration of his kingship—but a kingship rooted in humility, unlike worldly power.

The idea of **two donkeys** can also symbolically point to the dual nature of Christ’s mission. One donkey can represent Jesus' first coming, characterized by peace and humility, while the second can point to his return as a ruler in judgment, often connected with apocalyptic themes. This notion of two donkeys, paralleling two phases of divine intervention, subtly echoes the broader idea of Jesus as both the suffering servant and the returning king.

### The Donkeys and the Manger

The placement of these two donkey stars near the Beehive Cluster (known as **Praesepe**, meaning “manger” in Latin) ties directly into the nativity scene’s symbolism. In this celestial context, the **manger** is not just a humble feeding trough but also a symbolic gathering point for important cosmic forces. In Christian tradition, the donkey itself is a humble and service-oriented creature, symbolizing the kind of kingship Jesus would embody.

Thus, the two donkeys in the constellation can be seen as **heavenly symbols** of the earthly donkeys that played a role in Christ’s narrative—whether as his mode of transportation or as witnesses to his humble birth. The celestial donkeys connect the idea of **servitude, prophecy, and divine kingship** back to the manger, grounding this grand cosmic story in humble earthly realities.

### Further Symbolism of Donkeys in Prophecy

In rabbinical literature, the **donkey** is also a messianic symbol. The **Midrash** explains that the donkey on which the Messiah rides symbolizes the paradox of the Messiah’s arrival: lowly and humble, yet destined to bring great change. This contrast of the donkey—an animal of burden—points to the idea that the Messiah will carry the weight of the world’s suffering. The two donkeys in the stars could represent the dual burden of Jesus’ mission—first, to bear the sins of humanity and, second, to return in judgment.

Moreover, donkeys were central to agricultural societies and often represented **the people themselves**—carriers of the burden of oppression. Their appearance near the “manger” in the stars mirrors the scene on earth where humble animals, symbolic of common people, were present to witness the birth of the one who would bear the burdens of humanity.

### Conclusion: Cosmic and Earthly Donkeys

The two donkeys in the constellation near the celestial manger weave together multiple threads of symbolic meaning. Historically linked to Greek myths and battles, they are reinterpreted in biblical prophecy and point to the humble, servitude-driven nature of Jesus’ mission. As symbols of humility, service, and prophecy, these donkeys remind us of the two-fold nature of Christ’s role in history: first, the suffering servant, and second, the returning king.

The connection between these stars and the nativity’s manger scene deepens the significance of the animals that witnessed Jesus' birth. They are not mere bystanders; instead, they represent the humble and downtrodden, the very people Jesus came to uplift. The presence of these celestial donkeys further illuminates the cosmic and prophetic layers embedded in the birth of Christ.
