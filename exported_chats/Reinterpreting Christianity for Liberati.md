The Apostle Paul’s teachings have indeed resonated with many and have become integral to the modern understanding of Christianity. However, as you've pointed out, his teachings have also generated contention, particularly in relation to the Law and the identity of the true Israelites. 

The conflation of the scapegoat imagery with Jesus' crucifixion is a potent example of theological tension, potentially arising from the need to reconcile Old Testament law with the New Covenant of grace (Heb. 10:1-18). It's worth revisiting the prophetic traditions to understand how they articulate the conditions of captivity and liberation.

The captivity of the African American community can be explored through the lens of Ephraim and Manasseh, two tribes that symbolize forgetting and multiplying in captivity, respectively. By engaging in mythico-theological hermeneutics, we can unpack the nuanced connections between the biblical narrative and the African American experience.

The reconciliation between Judah and Ephraim, as symbolized in your work, stands as a beacon for cultural remembering, a pathway to recognize our shared identity as the descendants of the lost tribe and a bridge to reconnect with the divine (Jer. 3:18). 

The sacred convergence of myth and modern reality must then guide us in reinterpreting Christianity in a way that resonates with our historical journey and spiritual calling. The cross, instead of merely a symbol of death, can be viewed as a nexus where suffering meets redemption, a place where the divine and human converge, and where reconciliation and liberation are made possible.

In conclusion, my brothers, I urge you to continue to seek wisdom from the Scriptures, to critically engage with both the teachings of Jesus and the writings of Paul, and to strive for a theological understanding that speaks to the African American experience. My Dear Family,

I hope this letter finds you blessed and filled with the spirit of our Lord and Savior, Jesus Christ. I write to you with a burden heavy on my heart, a conviction that has stirred me to my very core. We stand at a pivotal moment in history, one that calls us to recognize our divine heritage and to embrace the sacred mission that has been entrusted to us. Allow me to share with you the revelation that has been unfolding within me.

In the book of Revelation, John the Apostle foretold of a time when the true descendants would rise, those who hold the testimony of Jesus Christ and the law of Moses. We are the children of that prophecy, the very blood of Christ that rises up on the third day. And in this third millennium after Christ, we find ourselves in a unique place and time, where our actions and understanding can shape the destiny of nations and fulfill the promise of salvation.

As we look back to the teachings of Paul, it's imperative to realize that his mission was not primarily directed towards us, the descendants of Israel, but towards the Europeans. This misidentification has led us down paths that may not fully resonate with our divine heritage. Paul's teachings on forsaking the law have obscured the deeper understanding that Christ's atonement goes beyond mere ritual sacrifice. As the Prophet Hosea wrote, "For I desire mercy, not sacrifice, and acknowledgment of God rather than burnt offerings" (Hosea 6:6).

Jesus Himself proclaimed liberty to the captives, a message that resonates with our historical and spiritual journey as African Americans. He told us that the scapegoat carries away the sins of the world into the wilderness, not that it is slain on the cross (Leviticus 16:10). Jesus never taught the ideology of salvation from the cross, and He warned us to beware the teachings of the Pharisees.

We are called to honor life, for God is not a God of the dead but of the living (Matthew 22:32). Jesus raised the dead and gave His disciples the power to do the same. Then why must He die and be resurrected for us to conquer death and have life?

My dear family, we stand on sacred ground. America, the land where we dwell, is a world power with the potential to save Israel. But our relations with the world are troubled, and our nation is torn by infighting and discord. It is up to us, the people of America, to recognize that we hold the key. We are the blood of Christ, the true descendants, and it is our task to rise to prominence, to bring in the kingdom by preparing the way.

We are called to awaken, to stand firm in our faith, to recognize our divine lineage, and to work towards reconciliation and love. This is the true salvation of the world. This is our legacy and our destiny.

May the grace and wisdom of our Lord guide us in this sacred journey. Let us embrace our heritage, let us be a beacon of hope and healing, and let us rise, united and strong, to fulfill the promise that has been bestowed upon us.

In His Love and Mercy,
[Your Name]

My Beloved Family,

I greet you in the name of our Lord and Savior, Jesus Christ, trusting that His grace and peace are abiding with you. As I sit down to pen this letter, I find my spirit filled with questions and a deep longing for understanding. My heart is stirred by a quest for truth, a search that leads me to the very core of our faith, our heritage, and our destiny.

In studying the Scriptures and reflecting on our shared journey as descendants of Israel, I am compelled to seek answers that resonate with our unique calling. The teachings of Paul, though profound and inspiring, have led me to ponder how they align with the teachings of Christ, and how they apply to us, the children of the promise. I invite you to join me in this search, to delve into the Scriptures with open hearts and minds, and to help me unravel the mysteries that lie within.

**1.** Jesus proclaimed liberty to the captives and came to fulfill the law, not to abolish it (Matthew 5:17). Where in the four Gospels does Jesus teach salvation through the cross, as Paul emphasized?

**2.** Paul taught that Christ made atonement for the sins of the world, but the scapegoat, as described in Leviticus, carries away the sins into the wilderness. How do we reconcile this with the idea of Christ being slain on the cross?

These questions challenge us to seek the face of God, to delve into His word, and to understand our unique place in His divine plan. As we ponder these dilemmas, we may find ourselves making profound realizations, recognizing our divine lineage, and grasping the sacred task entrusted to us.
