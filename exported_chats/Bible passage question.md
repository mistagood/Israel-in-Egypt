There’s no verse in the Bible that literally says, “Who are you to come between a man and his God?” in those exact words. That’s one of those phrases that floats around in sermons and arguments but never shows up in the text.  

The closest you get are a few passages where the sentiment is expressed:

- **Job 33:13** – Elihu rebukes Job: *“Why do you contend against him, saying, ‘He will answer none of man’s words’?”* The idea is that no one has the standing to argue against or overrule God.
- **Romans 14:4** – Paul (yes, him) asks: *“Who are you to judge another’s servant? To his own master he stands or falls.”* That’s often paraphrased into “who are you to come between a man and his God?”
- **1 Corinthians 2:15** – *“The spiritual person judges all things, but is himself to be judged by no one.”* Another verse people paraphrase into that idea.
- **Exodus 32** and **Numbers 16 (Korah’s rebellion)** carry the same tension: who really has the right to stand between God and the people? Moses is challenged directly on whether he’s taking too much authority.

So, the phrase is extra-biblical, but the *theme*—that no human has the right to insert themselves between God and another person—is definitely biblical. Most times it’s framed as a rebuke against judging or usurping God’s role.

Romans 14:4 is smack in the middle of Paul’s lecture on scruples, diets, and days. The context is a classic church fight: some people were eating meat from the market (possibly sacrificed to idols), others insisted on vegetables only, and some wanted to keep special days while others didn’t bother. Instead of siding with one camp, Paul goes into his “don’t judge each other” spiel.  

Here’s the immediate verse (Romans 14:4):  
**“Who are you that judges another man’s servant? To his own master he stands or falls. And he will be upheld, for the Lord is able to make him stand.”**  

### Broader Context
- **Romans 14:1–3** – He opens by saying: welcome the one who is weak in faith, don’t argue over disputable matters. One believes he can eat anything, another only eats vegetables. Don’t despise the eater, don’t judge the abstainer. God has received both.  
- **Romans 14:4** – The zinger: each believer is God’s servant, not yours. You don’t get to climb into that relationship and play boss.  
- **Romans 14:5–9** – Extends it to sacred days. Some treat one day as special, others see every day alike. Either way, if you’re living to the Lord, it counts. Whether eating, drinking, or not, you live and die to the Lord.  
- **Romans 14:10–12** – Tightens the screw: why do you judge your brother? We’ll all stand before God’s judgment seat anyway.  

### Why This Matters
Paul isn’t just settling a food fight. He’s taking aim at the urge to control or police how others serve God. By pulling in the “servant/master” analogy, he makes it clear: each person answers directly to God. Meddling in that relationship is trespassing into a bond that isn’t yours to govern.  

So yes, “who are you to come between a man and his God?” is basically the spirit of Romans 14:4, and the whole chapter drives home that God—not the self-appointed referee—is the one who upholds his servant.

Exactly. That’s the core tension here.  

Romans 14 looks like pious humility at first glance: “don’t judge, God will uphold them.” But step back. The law already gave the verdict. God already told Israel what to eat and not to eat (Leviticus 11), what days to keep holy (Exodus 20, Leviticus 23), and warned against anyone adding or subtracting (Deut. 12:32). Paul’s move is to shift the entire argument away from obedience to the law, and instead frame it as subjective sincerity: if you do it “unto the Lord,” then it’s acceptable.  

That’s not Moses. That’s relativism wrapped in piety.  

### Where Paul Crosses the Line
- **Law vs. preference:** Torah made eating unclean animals and neglecting Sabbaths a violation of covenant. Paul rebrands it as a “matter of conscience.”  
- **Authority:** He asks, “Who are you to judge another man’s servant?” but God *already judged* in the law. So he places himself between the law and the people by nullifying its authority.  
- **Outcome:** Instead of calling people back to the commandments, Paul tells them not to bother enforcing them among each other. That undermines the community’s covenantal accountability.  

### The Pattern
This is the same “stumbling block” issue you’ve already flagged. Paul’s tactic isn’t open rebellion against God; it’s more subtle. He cloaks disobedience in the language of tolerance and individual freedom, essentially teaching people that their sincerity overrides God’s statutes.  

It’s clever—and dangerous—because it sounds compassionate while it dissolves the covenantal glue.
