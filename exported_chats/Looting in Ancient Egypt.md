## **Looting in Ancient Egypt Associated with Civil Unrest (18th–19th Dynasties Focus)**

Civil unrest in Egypt usually followed either (1) power transitions, (2) economically weakened reigns, or (3) unpopular rulers. During these windows, tomb looting and palace plundering surged. The archaeological evidence tends to fall into three categories: administrative papyri records, physical damage to tombs, and changes in burial practices.

---

# **1. End of Akhenaten’s Reign & Amarna Collapse (Late 18th Dynasty)**

This is the *best-documented* case of systematic looting tied directly to governmental breakdown.

**Context:**  
Akhenaten’s religious revolution weakened the state bureaucracy. As central authority broke down near the end of his reign and during Smenkhkare/Neferneferuaten → Tutankhamun transition, Egypt entered a period of instability.

**Evidence of Looting & Unrest:**  
• **Amarna Excavations (Barry Kemp, Amarna Project):**  
   - Workers’ villages and elite houses show signs of rushed abandonment.  
   - Valuable items removed or destroyed.  
   - Storage magazines broken open.  
   - Looting pits from antiquity visible in elite tombs.

• **Royal Tomb (TA26 & other Amarna tombs):**  
   - Desecrated in antiquity.  
   - Cartouches hacked out.  
   - Offering tables smashed.  
   - Burial goods stripped.

• **Tomb of Akhenaten (TA26):**  
   - Robbed extensively; sarcophagus destroyed.  
   - The earliest waves of robbery appear to coincide closely with the collapse of Amarna itself, not later Ramesside robbery.

**What connects this to civil unrest:**  
Amarna’s population fled during a religious/political schism. With police and royal guard disrupted, tomb guardians disappeared. Looting was opportunistic and immediate—directly linked to political chaos.

---

# **2. The Transition from Horemheb to Ramesses I (18th → 19th Dynasty)**

**Context:**  
Horemheb tried to restore order after Amarna but died childless; coronation of Paramessu (Ramesses I) likely involved factional jockeying among Amun priesthood and military.

**Evidence:**  
• Administrative papyri from Deir el-Medina show early Ramesside concerns about widespread tomb robbery.  
• Correspondence about increased patrols and harsher penalties indicates a recent uptick.  
• The *Turin Strike Papyrus* (year 29 of Ramesses III, but referencing earlier practices) notes complaints about lack of pay—usually early warning signs of theft and looting.

This period does **not** show a singular event of civil unrest, but it does show the system newly vulnerable to plunder.

---

# **3. The Most Famous Looting Wave: The Tomb Robbery Trials (Late 19th – 20th Dynasty)**

Though a bit later (Seti II–Ramesses IX), the causes are rooted in instability beginning late in the 19th dynasty.

**Context:**  
Egypt was weakened by:  
• Succession disputes after Merenptah  
• The Seti II vs. Amenmesse civil war  
• Heavy international pressure from Libyans, Sea Peoples, and failing agricultural cycles

Civil unrest → economic decline → large-scale looting.

**Primary Sources:**  
• **Abbott Papyrus** (Ramesses IX) — investigations into robbery of Theban royal tombs  
• **Amherst Papyrus** — detailed confessions  
• **Leopold–Amherst Papyrus** — describes burglary of tombs and storerooms  
• **Mayer B Papyrus** — catalogues stolen items and the criminal networks  
• **Judicial Papyrus of Turin** — legal proceedings against high officials (including the queen Tiye conspiracy wave earlier)

**What this shows:**  
Organized gangs, sometimes including temple staff, policemen, and workmen, were stripping tombs with help from corrupt officials.

While this is technically 20th Dynasty, the conditions producing it begin right after the 18th/19th dynastic transition.

---

# **4. Looting at the End of the 18th Dynasty in Thebes (Tutankhamun–Horemheb)**

Even before the Ramessides, Theban tombs experienced waves of robbery.

**Evidence:**  
• KV55 (Amarna cache) — robbed in antiquity  
• Tombs of nobles dated to Tutankhamun–Ay show penetration shafts from ancient plunderers  
• Kitchener’s 19th-century survey noted ancient break-ins with pottery layers corresponding roughly to late 18th dynasty activity

These robberies seem tied to the weakened economy after the Amarna period.

---

# **5. Looting Not Associated with Unrest but Still Relevant**

Egyptian tomb robbery was not limited to political chaos. Even in stable periods, tomb robbery happened due to:

• High value of gold  
• Knowledge of tomb construction among workers  
• Economic strain on lower classes

But the *surges* correlate strongly with:

• Collapse of central government  
• Religious upheaval  
• Succession disputes  
• Foreign invasions
