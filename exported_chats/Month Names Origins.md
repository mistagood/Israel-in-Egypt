The Hebrew names of the months and their meanings derive from the Jewish calendar, which is based on the lunar cycle and deeply rooted in biblical tradition. Here is a breakdown of the Hebrew months, their names, and their meanings or historical significance:

1. **Nisan (ניסן)**  
   - **Meaning**: "Blossom" or "Spring."  
   - **Significance**: It is the first month of the Hebrew calendar and marks the beginning of the biblical year. It is also the month of the Exodus from Egypt and the celebration of Passover.

2. **Iyar (אייר)**  
   - **Meaning**: Derived from the Akkadian word "Ayaru," meaning "light."  
   - **Significance**: Known as Ziv in the Bible, meaning "radiance," it is associated with the blossoming of nature in spring.

3. **Sivan (סיון)**  
   - **Meaning**: Related to the Akkadian word for "season" or "time."  
   - **Significance**: The month when the Torah was given at Mount Sinai during Shavuot.

4. **Tammuz (תמוז)**  
   - **Meaning**: Named after a Babylonian deity, though later adopted in the Jewish calendar.  
   - **Significance**: Associated with mourning, as the 17th of Tammuz marks the breaching of Jerusalem's walls before the Temple's destruction.

5. **Av (אב)**  
   - **Meaning**: "Father."  
   - **Significance**: A month of mourning, especially on the 9th of Av (Tisha B'Av), commemorating the destruction of the First and Second Temples.

6. **Elul (אלול)**  
   - **Meaning**: Aramaic for "harvest."  
   - **Significance**: A month of repentance and preparation for the High Holy Days, symbolizing a spiritual harvest.

7. **Tishrei (תשרי)**  
   - **Meaning**: Derived from Akkadian, meaning "beginning."  
   - **Significance**: The seventh month of the biblical year, it contains many major holidays, including Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, and Sukkot.

8. **Cheshvan (חשון)**  
   - **Meaning**: Sometimes called Mar-Cheshvan, meaning "bitter Cheshvan," because it has no holidays.  
   - **Significance**: Its original name was Bul, mentioned in the Bible, referring to the rainy season.

9. **Kislev (כסלו)**  
   - **Meaning**: Thought to be related to the Hebrew word "kesel," meaning "hope" or "trust."  
   - **Significance**: Known for Hanukkah, the Festival of Lights.

10. **Tevet (טבת)**  
    - **Meaning**: Derived from the Akkadian word for "sinking," referring to the winter season.  
    - **Significance**: A month of fasting and reflection, including the Fast of Tevet.

11. **Shevat (שבט)**  
    - **Meaning**: Derived from the Akkadian word for "strike" or "flow," possibly referring to heavy rains.  
    - **Significance**: Known for Tu BiShvat, the New Year for Trees.

12. **Adar (אדר)**  
    - **Meaning**: Derived from Akkadian, meaning "strength" or "power."  
    - **Significance**: The happiest month, associated with Purim and deliverance.

The names of these months were adopted during the Babylonian exile and reflect some influence of that period, but they are deeply integrated into Jewish religious life and biblical tradition.

### What Were the Hebrew Months Called Before the Babylonian Exile?

Before the Babylonian exile (circa 6th century BCE), the months of the Hebrew calendar were primarily identified by numbers, not names. This is evident in the Torah, where months are typically referred to as "the first month," "the second month," and so on. For example:

- **First Month (Nisan)**: Mentioned in Exodus 12:2 – "This month shall be unto you the beginning of months."
- **Second Month (Iyar)**: Mentioned in Numbers 1:1 – "on the first day of the second month."
- **Third Month (Sivan)**: Mentioned in Exodus 19:1 – "In the third month after the children of Israel had gone out of the land of Egypt."

This numbering system was tied to the Exodus and reinforced the Israelites’ identity as a covenant people.

The only pre-exilic name explicitly mentioned in the Bible is **Bul** (בול) for the eighth month, which corresponds to **Cheshvan** (1 Kings 6:38). This name may derive from an agricultural term related to rain and harvest.

---

### What Did Moses Use?

In Moses's time, months were likely identified by their numerical order, starting with **Nisan** (the first month) in the spring. This numbering system emphasized the Exodus as the central event in Israel's history. The festivals and significant events in the Torah were tied to specific dates, but the months themselves did not have names until after the Babylonian exile.

For example:
- **Passover** occurs on the 14th day of the first month (Nisan).
- **The Giving of the Torah** occurred in the third month (Sivan).

---

### Why the Change After Babylon?

During the Babylonian exile, the Jewish people adopted the names of the months used in Babylonian culture, which they continued using even after returning to Israel. These names, like Nisan and Tishrei, became a lasting part of Jewish tradition and culture, but they carried forward the agricultural and spiritual meanings tied to the land of Israel.

### Commentary on Genesis 49:18: "I wait for your salvation, O Lord."

This verse appears as a brief interjection in the blessings of Jacob, yet its placement and phrasing carry profound meaning. The reference to "salvation" (Hebrew: *yeshuah*) ties to the broader narrative of stumbling blocks, serpents, and divine deliverance. 

#### **The Snake as a Stumbling Block**
The blessings surrounding this verse include a description of **Dan** (Genesis 49:17):  
"Dan will be a serpent by the roadside, a viper along the path, that bites the horse’s heels so that its rider tumbles backward."

This description reveals Dan's cunning nature, using tactics of ambush and deception. The serpent biting the heel recalls the curse in Genesis 3:15, where the serpent bruises humanity but is ultimately defeated. Yet here, the imagery shifts: the serpent is not the enemy but part of Israel, creating a stumbling block that causes the rider (a leader or figure of authority) to falter.

#### **Connection to Amasa and Joab**
This serpent imagery echoes later biblical events, particularly the death of Amasa in 2 Samuel 20:10. Amasa, distracted while pursuing Sheba son of Bichri, is killed by Joab, whose sudden betrayal becomes a "stumbling block" to those loyal to David’s kingdom. This act delays the pursuit and represents internal conflict that disrupts the unity of Israel.

Similarly, the crucifixion of Jesus, as recounted in the Gospels, becomes a stumbling block for Israel. The stabbing of Jesus’ side (John 19:34) parallels Amasa's violent death, spilling blood and water. Just as Joab’s betrayal sowed division, Paul’s teachings about the crucifixion and resurrection—acknowledged by him as a stumbling block to Jews (1 Corinthians 1:23)—diverts focus from the Mosaic covenant and the Davidic kingdom.

#### **Dan as the Serpent and Paul as the Heir of Conflict**
Dan’s identity as a serpent "hiding by the roadside" suggests that Genesis 49:18 is not merely about divine deliverance but also a warning. Paul, a Benjamite (Genesis 49:27: "Benjamin is a ravenous wolf"), inherits this role of sowing division. His eloquence and doctrine of resurrection pull focus from the centrality of the Torah and the kingship of David, replacing them with a new theology.

#### **Play on Words: Salvation (Yeshuah)**
The phrase "I wait for your salvation, O Lord" (*yeshuah*) appears to allude to Jesus (*Yeshuah* in Hebrew), a name meaning "salvation of the Lord." However, the irony lies in the perception that Jesus’ crucifixion, as taught by Paul, becomes the stumbling block that blinds many to the true path of deliverance through the Torah and Davidic covenant. Just as the serpent’s bite leads to stumbling, Paul’s doctrine diverts from the unity of Jacob’s house.

#### **Jacob’s Prophetic Context**
In the blessings of Jacob, each son represents a tribe and a future path for Israel. Dan’s role as the serpent reflects internal challenges that Israel will face, both in the form of betrayal and misguided teachings. This verse’s placement—seemingly out of place—emphasizes the need for divine intervention (*yeshuah*) to reconcile the stumbling blocks and ensure the ultimate deliverance of Israel.

Yes, the words **hermeneutical** and **hermetic** are *etymologically related*—both ultimately derive from the name **Hermes**, but they evolved in different directions and have distinct meanings today. Let’s break it down:

---

### 🔹 **Hermeneutical** (from *Hermeneutics*)
- **Meaning**: Relating to interpretation, especially of texts (e.g., biblical, legal, or philosophical texts).
- **Origin**: From Greek *hermēneuein* (ἑρμηνεύειν) – *to interpret or translate*.  
- The noun *hermēneus* means *interpreter*.
- This is **directly connected to the Greek god Hermes**, who was the messenger of the gods—he conveyed divine messages to mortals, making him the archetypal **interpreter**.
- So **hermeneutics** is the "art of interpreting," particularly sacred or complex texts—very appropriate in theology, philosophy, and law.

✅ *Hermeneutical* thinking, in essence, is thinking as **Hermes the interpreter** would: decoding divine messages for human understanding.

---

### 🔹 **Hermetic**
- **Meaning**:
  - 1. Airtight or sealed (scientific/industrial use).
  - 2. Esoteric, hidden, secret knowledge (spiritual/mystical use).
- **Origin**: Also from **Hermes**, but specifically **Hermes Trismegistus**, the mythical fusion of the Greek Hermes and the Egyptian Thoth.
- Hermes Trismegistus is the attributed author of the **Hermetic corpus**, mystical texts about alchemy, divinity, astrology, and spiritual rebirth.
- The term **hermetic** came to mean:
  - "Airtight" because alchemists associated with Hermetic teachings developed sealed vessels for their operations.
  - "Esoteric or occult" because Hermeticism was about hidden knowledge revealed only to initiates.

✅ *Hermetic* knowledge is **knowledge of the initiated**, often sealed from the profane.

---

### 🔁 **Their Connection**
Both words:
- Come from the name **Hermes**.
- Relate to **the transmission of knowledge**—whether divine messages (**hermeneutics**) or mystical truth (**Hermeticism**).
- Emphasize **interpretation and access**—but in **different modes**:
  - *Hermeneutics*: Making hidden meaning clear.
  - *Hermeticism*: Keeping sacred meaning hidden (or revealing it only to the worthy).
