The phrase **“the allegory of Jacob”** isn’t a fixed biblical expression, but it can refer to interpreting the story of **Jacob (Yaʿaqov, יַעֲקֹב)** as an *allegory*—that is, as a symbolic or spiritual narrative rather than a purely historical account.  

Let’s unpack both words precisely first, and then their union as a concept:

**1. Jacob — יַעֲקֹב (Yaʿaqov)**  
The name comes from the Hebrew root **ʿqb (עקב)** meaning *heel, follow, supplant, or overreach*. It appears in Genesis 25:26 when Jacob was born grasping his twin Esau’s heel. This act gives rise to the name:  
> “And afterward his brother came out, and his hand took hold on Esau’s heel; and his name was called Jacob.” (Genesis 25:26)

So, linguistically, *Jacob* means “he grasps the heel” or figuratively “he supplants.” The root also carries the sense of *to watch, to track, to outwit*—someone who follows closely behind another.

**2. Allegory**  
An *allegory* (from Greek ἀλληγορία, *allegoria*, “speaking otherwise”) means telling a story whose events or characters stand for deeper moral, spiritual, or historical truths.

**3. “The Allegory of Jacob”** — interpreted  
Taken together, *the allegory of Jacob* is the idea that Jacob’s life—his struggles, name changes, and family—embodies the **spiritual journey of transformation**:
- **From Jacob to Israel** — “He who supplants” becomes “He who prevails with God” (Genesis 32:28). This renaming marks the inner victory of spirit over flesh, deceit turned to divine striving.  
- **The Two Brothers** — Jacob and Esau represent opposing forces: the carnal and the spiritual, the hunter and the tent-dweller, the external inheritance and the internal covenant.  
- **The Two Camps (Genesis 32:7)** — Jacob divides his people into two camps before meeting Esau, symbolizing the division and ultimate reunification within Israel—both familial and prophetic.  
- **The Wrestling at Peniel (Genesis 32:24–30)** — his wrestling “until the breaking of day” is often treated allegorically as humankind’s struggle with God, or the transformation through suffering into divine identity.

In short:  
**The allegory of Jacob** expresses the movement from deception and division toward enlightenment and unity. It’s the metamorphosis of the human soul (Jacob) into divine consciousness (Israel).

You’re recalling a passage that sounds very much like something in **Paul’s letters**, but it’s actually **not** in the *Book of Hebrews* — it’s in **Galatians 4:22–24**, not Hebrews.  

Here’s the exact passage:

> **Galatians 4:22–24 (KJV)**  
> *For it is written, that Abraham had two sons, the one by a bondmaid, the other by a freewoman.  
> But he who was of the bondwoman was born after the flesh; but he of the freewoman was by promise.  
> Which things are an **allegory**: for these are the two covenants; the one from the mount Sinai, which gendereth to bondage, which is Agar.*

So Paul (not the author of Hebrews) calls the story of **Abraham’s two sons—Ishmael (by Hagar) and Isaac (by Sarah)**—an *allegory*.  

In Greek, the word is **ἀλληγορούμενα (allegoroumena)**, meaning “spoken in another sense” or “symbolically interpreted.”  
Paul uses it to explain that:
- **Hagar** = the covenant from Mount Sinai (the Law, slavery, the earthly Jerusalem).  
- **Sarah** = the covenant of promise (freedom, the heavenly Jerusalem).

This is the only place in the New Testament where the word *allegory* itself appears.

If you were thinking of **Jacob**, that’s understandable — Hebrews *does* reference **Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph** often (especially in Hebrews 11, the “faith” chapter), but it never uses the term *allegory* there. Hebrews treats them as **examples of faith**, not allegories.

So, to summarize:

- **Book:** *Galatians 4:22–24*  
- **Figure:** Abraham (through Sarah and Hagar)  
- **Theme:** Two covenants — one of bondage, one of freedom  
- **Word used:** *Allegory* — Greek *allegoroumena* (ἀλληγορούμενα)

That’s a sharp reading, and it goes right to the structural rhythm of Genesis: repetition with transformation.  

You’re right that Paul’s *allegory of Hagar and Sarah* in *Galatians 4* effectively detaches the Abrahamic story from its own internal logic. Genesis doesn’t describe two covenants made with Abraham—only one, reaffirmed repeatedly (Genesis 12, 15, 17). The covenantal line is explicit: Abraham → Isaac → Jacob → Israel. Ishmael is blessed as a nation but **excluded from the covenant**. Paul’s “two covenants” scheme is a theological overlay, not a feature of the Genesis text.

Your insight that **Jacob’s two wives mirror the same allegorical scaffolding** is powerful—and it’s much closer to the narrative’s own rhythm. Leah and Rachel embody the pattern of reversal:  
- **Leah**, unloved but fertile, represents the outward, visible success of the flesh—many sons, immediate fruit.  
- **Rachel**, beloved but barren, represents the hidden covenant line—the promise delayed, faith tested, divine remembrance.  

When *“God remembered Rachel”* (Genesis 30:22), it’s the same phrasing used for Noah (Genesis 8:1) and Abraham (Genesis 19:29). It marks divine intervention and renewal. From the woman “looked down on,” the true heir (Joseph) emerges—the ruler and restorer.  

So yes, the story of Jacob is an allegory of **two houses, two covenants, and two births**—but the allegory is *organic*, not imposed. It grows from within the family narrative rather than being read backward through a later doctrinal lens. The barren-then-remembered wife, the younger-then-chosen son—these motifs are the Torah’s heartbeat. They tell a story of how divine favor repeatedly subverts human order: Cain–Abel, Ishmael–Isaac, Esau–Jacob, Leah–Rachel, Manasseh–Ephraim.

The pattern you describe—*forgotten, then remembered; despised, then exalted*—is the covenant’s internal allegory of redemption. It’s the seed theme that keeps sprouting throughout the text, until it blooms into the idea that the ruler, the redeemer, always comes from the unlikely womb.
