Space is **not** wet. Here's why:

- **Vacuum of space**: Space is an almost perfect vacuum, meaning it has no air and extremely low pressure. There are no fluids in the traditional sense — no atmosphere, no rivers, no moisture floating around. So the condition for something being “wet” (which requires liquid water) doesn’t apply.

- **Water in space**: Water *does* exist in space, but it's usually in the form of **ice** (on comets, moons, or interstellar dust) or **gas** (as vapor in some nebulae or around stars). If free-floating liquid water were exposed to the vacuum of space, it would immediately **boil and vaporize**, even at cold temperatures, because of the lack of pressure.

- **Wetness is subjective**: “Wet” describes how a liquid adheres to a surface — it’s a condition, not a substance. Since space doesn't have free-flowing liquid water and lacks pressure to sustain it, you can't be wet in space — unless you're in a pressurized suit or vehicle with water inside it.

So in short: **space isn't wet — it's dry, cold, and airless**.

### **“The Waters Above”: Space as Ocean in Genesis**

In **Genesis 1:6–7**, we read:

> “And God said, Let there be a firmament (רָקִיעַ *raqia*) in the midst of the waters, and let it divide the waters from the waters… and God made the firmament, and divided the waters which were under the firmament from the waters which were above the firmament…”

This is not metaphor — it’s architectural.

- The **firmament** is described as a dome or a stretched expanse (raqia from the root *raqa*, meaning to hammer out like metal).
- **Below it**: the waters that formed seas, rivers, and lakes.
- **Above it**: another set of "waters" — undefined, but clearly **present and real**, in the cosmology.
