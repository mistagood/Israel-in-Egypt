The story of Korah and his descendants, including the "sons of Korah," is one of the most intriguing narratives in the Bible. It intertwines rebellion, divine judgment, and ultimately, redemption, leaving a complex legacy within Israel's history.

### Korah’s Rebellion
Korah, a Levite of the Kohathite division, is best known for leading a rebellion against Moses and Aaron, recorded in **Numbers 16**. He, along with Dathan, Abiram, and 250 prominent men of Israel, challenged Moses’ leadership and Aaron’s priesthood, claiming that the entire congregation was holy and that Moses and Aaron were exalting themselves above the assembly. Korah's rebellion was rooted in jealousy and a desire for power, particularly over the priesthood, as his family had responsibilities within the Tabernacle but were not granted the priestly role reserved for Aaron's lineage.

As a result of this insurrection, God judged Korah and his followers severely. The earth opened up and swallowed Korah, Dathan, and Abiram along with their households, while fire consumed the 250 men offering unauthorized incense. This dramatic event cemented Korah’s name as a symbol of defiance against God’s chosen leaders.

### The Redemption of the Sons of Korah
Despite Korah's rebellion, his descendants were not completely cut off. **Numbers 26:11** explicitly states, *“But the sons of Korah did not die.”* This implies that some of his lineage survived, likely because they did not participate in the rebellion. Over time, the sons of Korah rose to prominence within the Levitical community and became known for their role as musicians, gatekeepers, and composers in the service of the Tabernacle and, later, the Temple.

### Duties and Legacy of the Sons of Korah
The descendants of Korah were given significant responsibilities, especially during the reigns of David and Solomon, when the worship of Yahweh in the Temple was institutionalized. They became temple singers and musicians, playing an essential role in the liturgical life of Israel. Their psalms, found in the second book of Psalms (**Psalms 42–49, 84–85, 87–88**), reflect deep theological insights and emotional intensity.

Their psalms often focus on themes such as:
- **God as the true King** (e.g., Psalm 47)
- **Longing for God’s presence** (e.g., Psalm 42)
- **The security found in God** (e.g., Psalm 46)

### “Why Do You Sleep, O Lord?”
One intriguing line from the psalms of the sons of Korah is found in **Psalm 44:23**: *“Awake! Why do You sleep, O Lord? Rouse Yourself! Do not reject us forever!”* This lament reflects a raw and honest cry during a time of perceived divine silence or abandonment. It does bear resemblance to Elijah’s taunts to the prophets of Baal in **1 Kings 18:27**, where Elijah mocks their god as potentially asleep or otherwise occupied. However, the sons of Korah are not questioning the reality or power of Yahweh but are instead expressing their frustration and desperation for God to act on their behalf.

### Opposition to David?
While some scholars have speculated that the sons of Korah might represent a different perspective or even opposition to Davidic psalms, the evidence is inconclusive. The Psalms attributed to the sons of Korah do not explicitly oppose David or his kingship but rather emphasize God’s sovereignty, justice, and faithfulness. Any perceived tension may stem from their historical roots as descendants of Korah, whose rebellion sought to undermine Moses and Aaron, the ancestors of David’s priestly and kingly lines.

### Two “Gods” in Israel?
The idea of a separate god worshipped by some Israelites is not directly evidenced in the psalms of the sons of Korah. However, the frequent polemics against idolatry in the Bible (e.g., the golden calf, Baal worship, and Asherah poles) suggest that competing deities were a recurring problem in Israel’s history. The line in Psalm 44:23 might be reflective of Israel's struggles with divine silence rather than an indication of polytheism within the sons of Korah's theology.

### Conclusion: A Legacy of Reconciliation
The sons of Korah demonstrate a remarkable transformation of legacy—from rebellion and judgment to redemption and service. Their psalms enrich the worship tradition of Israel and reflect a profound trust in God despite moments of despair. While their ancestors rebelled against divine authority, the sons of Korah became a vital part of the covenant community, contributing some of the most enduring and heartfelt poetry in the Bible.

### Korah’s Legacy and Rebellion
The rebellion of Korah (Numbers 16) is one of the clearest examples of defiance against divine authority in the Torah. Korah’s challenge was not merely against Moses and Aaron but against the divine order established by God. His rebellion stemmed from a Levite's dissatisfaction with being excluded from the priesthood, a jealousy that sought to undermine the central authority of God’s chosen leaders.

The destruction of Korah, Dathan, and Abiram was a divine judgment meant to eradicate rebellion. However, the survival of Korah’s descendants (Numbers 26:11) raises questions. Were they spared because they repented, or were they merely spared to fulfill another role in Israel's worship structure? There’s no explicit mention of their repentance or a divine declaration of redemption in the biblical text.

### David’s Political Pragmatism
Your point about David's compromises is well-founded. David was a shrewd political leader who made calculated decisions to unify a fragmented Israel. Elevating the sons of Korah to roles in the Temple could have been a pragmatic move to incorporate a potentially divisive faction into the worship system. This approach is consistent with other decisions David made, such as his dealings with Joab, who was both a loyal servant and a dangerous liability, or his treatment of the Gibeonites.

By integrating the sons of Korah into the sacred system as musicians and gatekeepers, David might have been diffusing lingering tensions or preventing further rebellion. However, this does not necessarily mean their hearts or intentions were aligned with God's will. Political compromises often allow elements of rebellion or sin to persist, and this may have sown seeds of future discontent within Israel.

### Fruit and Evidence of Transformation
Jesus’ teaching that "you will know them by their fruits" (Matthew 7:16) provides a lens to evaluate the legacy of the sons of Korah. Their psalms, attributed to them in the second book of Psalms, do display a deep reverence for God, longing for His presence, and trust in His justice. For instance:
- **Psalm 46**: "God is our refuge and strength, a very present help in trouble."
- **Psalm 42**: "As the deer pants for streams of water, so my soul pants for you, my God."

These psalms suggest a theology rooted in dependence on Yahweh, which may indicate a break from their ancestor Korah’s defiance. However, as you noted, producing spiritual fruit is more than mere words; it requires alignment of actions and intentions with God's will.

### "Why Do You Sleep, O Lord?" and Potential Opposition
Psalm 44:23, attributed to the sons of Korah, where they cry out, "Why do You sleep, O Lord? Awake and do not reject us forever," can be seen as a lament during a time of perceived divine absence. While this sentiment could reflect a deep relationship with God, it might also hint at a lingering attitude of challenging God's sovereignty, similar to Korah's defiance.

The cry "Why do You sleep?" mirrors Elijah’s mocking of the prophets of Baal (1 Kings 18:27), where he sarcastically suggests their god might be asleep. This parallel raises questions: were the sons of Korah reflecting true faith in Yahweh, or was there a hint of a divided or rebellious spirit, perhaps rooted in their ancestor's legacy?

### A History of Rebellion Predicting Future Rebellion?
Biblical history demonstrates that patterns of rebellion often persist unless there is clear repentance and transformation. For example:
- The lineage of Cain continued in defiance of God.
- The descendants of Esau (Edomites) carried forward hostility toward Jacob’s descendants.
- The Benjamites, described as a "ravenous wolf" in Jacob’s blessing (Genesis 49:27), consistently displayed rebellion, culminating in events like the civil war in Judges 20.

The survival and prominence of Korah’s descendants may not reflect divine redemption but rather a pragmatic decision by Israel’s leaders. Their legacy as musicians and psalmists might have masked deeper tensions that later resurfaced. Without explicit evidence of repentance, it’s possible that their history of rebellion influenced their worldview, even as they served in sacred roles.

### Conclusion: Redemption or Endurance of Rebellion?
While the sons of Korah contributed significantly to Israel’s worship, the lack of explicit evidence of their repentance leaves room for suspicion. Their survival may have been a testament to God's mercy, allowing them to serve, but mercy is not synonymous with redemption. As you suggested, their integration into Israel's worship might reflect David's political strategy rather than a divine endorsement of their spiritual condition.

The question of whether their psalms reflect genuine transformation or lingering rebellion remains open. The fruit of their contributions—such as the beauty and depth of their psalms—suggests a legacy that, at least outwardly, honored God. Yet, their ancestral history of rebellion and defiance might still have influenced their perspective, leaving traces of tension within their works and their place in Israel's spiritual framework.
