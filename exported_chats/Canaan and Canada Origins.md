## 1. **Canaan** – Original Name and Meaning

### **Language Origin**
The term **"Canaan"** (Hebrew: **כְּנַעַן – Kənaʿan**) is the name used by the **Hebrews** and appears extensively in the Bible and in other ancient Near Eastern texts. However, it likely **was not what the people called themselves.** The native language was a form of **Northwest Semitic**, closely related to early Hebrew, Phoenician, and Ugaritic.

### **Etymology**
- The most common interpretation of “Canaan” is **“lowland” or “land of purple dye”**, from a Semitic root:
  - Hebrew root: **כָּנַע (kanaʿ)** = to bow down, be low, be humble
  - Akkadian equivalent: **kinahhu** = reddish-purple dye (used in textiles)

  Thus, Canaan might mean:
  > “The lowlands” or “the land of purple dye-makers” (Phoenicians were famous for this dye, derived from murex snails).

### **What Did Canaanites Call Themselves?**
While “Canaan” is what outsiders (Egyptians, Hebrews) often called the territory, **the people who lived there**—particularly **in cities like Ugarit, Sidon, Tyre, and Jerusalem**—likely identified **by city-state or tribal names**, such as:
- *Amorites*, *Jebusites*, *Hivites*, *Hittites*, etc.
- **Phoenicians** (later period) called themselves **Kenaʿaniyyo or Kinānu** in inscriptions, showing some acceptance of the exonym.

## 2. **Canada** – Original Name and Tribal Language Roots

### **Colonial Naming History**
The name **Canada** comes from the **St. Lawrence Iroquoian** word **"kanata"**, which meant **“village” or “settlement.”**
- First recorded by **Jacques Cartier** in 1535 when indigenous people pointed him toward **Kanata**, meaning the nearby village of Stadacona (now Quebec City).
- Cartier misunderstood it as the name of the larger land.

### **Original Word:**
- **kanata** (Iroquoian, especially Wendat/Huron or Laurentian)
  - Meaning: **“village,” “settlement,” or “dwelling-place”**

### **Tribal Speakers:**
- The **St. Lawrence Iroquoians**, a now-extinct group of Iroquoian-speaking people who lived along the St. Lawrence River (present-day Quebec and Ontario).
- Related languages: Mohawk, Huron-Wendat, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, Tuscarora.

### **Breakdown of the Word:**
In Iroquoian structure, **“ka-”** is a prefix (often relating to a noun), and **“-nata”** resembles structures meaning **place** or **dwelling**.

Thus:
> **Kanata** ≈ "the place" or "that settlement"
