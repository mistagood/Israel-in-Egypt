In the grand tapestry of biblical narratives, the story of Saul's transformation into King Saul in 1 Samuel chapter 10 serves as a profound allegory. This transformation, marked by prophetic signs and a divine selection, unfolds against a backdrop of Israel's desire for a human king, an act described by Samuel as a reflection of their wickedness. The transformation of Saul into a different person through the fulfillment of prophetic signs symbolizes a pivotal moment in Israel's history, one that we can liken to the transformation of Saul of Tarsus in the New Testament.

Saul of Tarsus, a Pharisee and Benjamite by lineage, undergoes a dramatic transformation on the road to Damascus, changing his name to Paul. This metamorphosis can be seen as a foreshadowing of the earlier Saul's anointment as king. Just as the Old Testament Saul was transformed through divine signs, the New Testament Saul (Paul) experienced a profound spiritual transformation. However, unlike the former, whose kingship marked the beginning of Israel's monarchical era, Paul's transformation introduced a new doctrinal path that would lead many astray.

Paul's role as a "stumbling block" is critical to understanding this parallel. Deuteronomy 13 warns against prophets who perform signs and wonders yet lead the people away from God's commandments. Paul's introduction of the doctrine of resurrection atonement, a departure from Mosaic Levitical atonement, can be viewed through this lens. His teachings, while appearing miraculous and transformative, diverge from the strict adherence to God's law, creating a new path that distracts from the original commandments.

This narrative is further enriched by the account in 2 Samuel. The incident involving the two bodies of the innocent dead man, possibly Azahel, lying in the road and causing a diversion until removed, parallels the distraction caused by Paul's teachings. Just as the Israelites fixated on the bodies until they were moved to continue their mission, Paul's resurrection atonement caused a significant distraction from the true path prescribed by God's law. The focus on this new doctrine diverted attention from the mission to uphold God's commandments, echoing the diversion and eventual redirection in 2 Samuel.

Moreover, the pursuit of Sheba, the Benjamite who led a rebellion against King David, further symbolizes the consequences of this diversion. Just as Sheba's rebellion needed to be quelled to restore order, Paul's departure from Mosaic law represented a rebellion that needed addressing to return to the foundational principles of faith. This alignment underscores the enduring struggle between maintaining the purity of God's law and the allure of new, seemingly transformative teachings.

In essence, the parallel between the Old Testament Saul and the New Testament Paul serves as a profound commentary on the cyclical nature of divine testing and human leadership. Both Sauls, despite their transformations and divine interactions, ultimately present challenges to the faithfulness of God's people. Their stories remind us of the importance of discerning true adherence to God's commandments from the distractions of seemingly miraculous signs and new doctrines.
