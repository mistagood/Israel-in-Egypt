### **2. Reinterpreting Freud’s Stages as Power Dynamics**
   Each stage can be dissected as follows:
   
   - **Oral Stage (0-1 year)**: 
     - **Power Dynamics**: Frame as a fundamental period where infants first experience control (or lack thereof) over their immediate needs. The focus on feeding serves as an introduction to autonomy.
     - **Interpretation**: Instead of “oral fixation,” the need to gain control over comfort (hunger, attention) introduces the power struggle over dependency and independence.
   
   - **Anal Stage (1-3 years)**: 
     - **Power Dynamics**: Reposition toilet training as a battleground for autonomy, where children gain agency over their bodies and learn how to assert control within caregiver-imposed limits.
     - **Interpretation**: Rather than fixation on cleanliness or order, this stage represents a foundational experience in negotiating personal boundaries and internal vs. external control.
   
   - **Phallic Stage (3-6 years)**: 
     - **Power Dynamics**: Identify this stage as when children begin to internalize societal hierarchies by observing the power dynamics between male and female figures, typically mirroring the dominant figure (often the father).
     - **Interpretation**: The Oedipal complex as a rivalry for power acknowledgment rather than romantic interest, with children seeking validation and social standing within the family hierarchy.

   - **Latency Stage (6-puberty)**: 
     - **Power Dynamics**: Define this period as one of social and power refinement, where the child navigates group dynamics, authority, and peer influence.
     - **Interpretation**: Rather than sexual repression, it’s a period of social learning focused on understanding hierarchy, competition, and cooperation in wider social structures.

   - **Genital Stage (puberty onward)**: 
     - **Power Dynamics**: Present this stage as the realization of one’s social identity and the conscious choice of power roles within societal structures.
