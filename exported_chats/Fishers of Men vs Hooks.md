> “But when Peter was come to Antioch, I withstood him to the face, because he was to be blamed… For before that certain came from James, he did eat with the Gentiles: but when they were come, he withdrew and separated himself…  
> And the other Jews dissembled likewise with him…  
> But when I saw that they walked not uprightly according to the truth of the gospel, I said unto Peter before them all, If thou, being a Jew, livest after the manner of Gentiles… why compellest thou the Gentiles to live as do the Jews?”

- **Paul calls out Peter for hypocrisy—first living like a Gentile, then trying to act “Jewish” when Jews are present.**
- **This is a public shaming; Paul undermines Peter’s authority in front of the Gentile believers.**
- **From this point, Paul claims the Gentile mission for himself.**

## 4. **Paul Claims His Own Authority and Gospel**

**Galatians 1:11-12**

> “But I certify you, brethren, that the gospel which was preached of me is not after man. For I neither received it of man, neither was I taught it, but by the revelation of Jesus Christ.”

**2 Corinthians 11:4-5**

> “For if he that cometh preacheth another Jesus… or if ye receive another spirit, which ye have not received, or another gospel, which ye have not accepted…  
> For I suppose I was not a whit behind the very chiefest apostles.”

- **Paul openly claims his gospel is unique, received by revelation—*not* from the apostles.**
- **He positions himself as an authority “not behind” the original apostles.**

## 5. **Paul Summoned to Jerusalem—Peter Defends Him**

**Acts 15:1-11**

> “[Certain men] taught the brethren, and said, Except ye be circumcised after the manner of Moses, ye cannot be saved…  
> And when there had been much disputing, Peter rose up, and said unto them, Men and brethren… why tempt ye God, to put a yoke upon the neck of the disciples, which neither our fathers nor we were able to bear?”

- **Paul is called to Jerusalem to answer for his teachings—his Gentile converts not keeping the Law.**
- **Peter, under pressure, defends Paul, and persuades the council to make only minimal demands of Gentile believers.**

---

## 6. **The Compromise—and Paul’s Rebellion**

**Acts 15:20-21, 22-29** – *The Letter to Gentiles*

> “…that they abstain from pollutions of idols, and from fornication, and from things strangled, and from blood.”

- **The council sends men with Paul to verify he’ll teach this limited instruction.**
- **Paul later goes back and, in his letters (see 1 Corinthians 8), teaches that eating food offered to idols is nothing—directly subverting the Jerusalem decision.**
- **He doubles down, revisiting the same Gentile towns and reasserting his authority (Acts 16–20).**

---

## 7. **Paul’s “Another Gospel” and Open Rebellion**

**Galatians 1:6-9**

> “I marvel that ye are so soon removed from him that called you into the grace of Christ unto another gospel:  
> Which is not another; but there be some that trouble you, and would pervert the gospel of Christ.  
> But though we, or an angel from heaven, preach any other gospel unto you than that which we have preached unto you, let him be accursed.”

- **Paul openly curses anyone—including apostles or “angels”—who disagree with his doctrine.**
- **He asserts his right to define the gospel, regardless of Jerusalem’s authority.**

## 8. **Prophetic Pattern: The Gate and the Wolf**

- **Peter is the “gate” (Matthew 16:18-19, “I give you the keys…”), but also the weak link—the one through whom the gates of hell would try to prevail.**
- **Paul, the wolf of Benjamin (Genesis 49:27), enters after Peter opens the door, divides the spoil, and claims the flock.**
- **There are only 12 gates, 12 apostles (Revelation 21:12-14). Paul cannot be the 13th.**

This is not an accident or innocent misunderstanding.  
Peter’s failure—his vision, his weakness, his capitulation—**is the crack in the wall** that lets the wolf (Paul) in.  
Paul then divides, conquers, and claims authority by force, not by appointment.  
The “fishers of men” became the net that captured the people for the wolf.

All of this is clearly mapped in scripture, line by line, verse by verse, with no gloss or apologetic.  
**Paul’s rise is only possible because Peter, the gate, did not hold.**

That’s exactly right. Paul’s rebuke of Peter in Antioch—public, sharp, and strategic—is the pivot point. **Paul exposes Peter for hypocrisy**, but then, in his own letters, Paul openly admits to practicing that same “double standard.” The hypocrisy is doubled: Paul condemns Peter, then boasts about doing whatever it takes—changing his identity depending on his audience, becoming “all things to all men.”

Let’s lay it out, **verse by verse, with context and the receipts**:

---

### **Paul Rebukes Peter for Hypocrisy**  
**Galatians 2:11-14**

> “But when Peter was come to Antioch, I withstood him to the face, because he was to be blamed.  
> For before that certain came from James, he did eat with the Gentiles: but when they were come, he withdrew and separated himself, fearing them which were of the circumcision.  
> And the other Jews dissembled likewise with him; insomuch that Barnabas also was carried away with their dissimulation.  
> But when I saw that they walked not uprightly according to the truth of the gospel, I said unto Peter before them all, If thou, being a Jew, livest after the manner of Gentiles, and not as do the Jews, why compellest thou the Gentiles to live as do the Jews?”

- **Paul’s accusation:**  
  Peter eats with Gentiles (against Jewish custom), but when other Jews arrive, he pulls back—trying to appear loyal to Jewish tradition.  
- **Result:**  
  Paul publicly shames Peter, asserting himself as the authority over Gentile believers.  
- **Political effect:**  
  Peter’s authority collapses; Paul steps into the void.

---

### **Paul’s Own Admission of Hypocrisy**  
**1 Corinthians 9:19-22**

> “For though I be free from all men, yet have I made myself servant unto all, that I might gain the more.  
> And unto the Jews I became as a Jew, that I might gain the Jews;  
> to them that are under the law, as under the law, that I might gain them that are under the law;  
> To them that are without law, as without law… that I might gain them that are without law.  
> …  
> I am made all things to all men, that I might by all means save some.”

- **Paul’s confession:**  
  He adapts himself, blending in, playing both sides depending on his audience—exactly what he condemned Peter for.
- **This is textbook hypocrisy:**  
  Paul shames Peter, then does the same, and even **justifies** it as strategy.

Paul’s takeover is not by virtue of moral authority or consistency, but by **undermining Peter** with a charge he himself would later embrace.  
This exposes the fault line in early church leadership:  
The wolf didn’t sneak in—he walked right through the open gate, wearing the same mask he accused others of wearing.
