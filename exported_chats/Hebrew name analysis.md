Let’s take a close, linguistic look at Genesis 35:18 in Hebrew, focusing on the two name forms—*Ben-’Oni* (בֶּן־אוֹנִי) and *Binyamin* (בִּנְיָמִין)—to see if the text implies nickname versus formal name.

---

### 1. Hebrew Text and Syntax
> וַיְהִ֣י בְצֵֽאת־נַפְשָׁ֗הּ כִּ֣י מֵתָה֮ וַתִּקְרָ֣א שְׁמ֣וֹ בֶּן־אוֹנִ֔י וְאָבִ֖יו קָרָ֥א לֽוֹ בִּנְיָמִֽין׃  
> *“And it came to pass, as her soul was departing (for she died), that she called his name Ben-Oni; but his father called him Benjamin.”*

Hebrew verb **וַתִּקְרָא (vatikraʾ)** “she called,” and **קָרָא (karaʾ)** “he called,” both use the same verb *qārāʾ*, meaning “to name, to proclaim, to summon.” There is no lexical distinction between “nickname” and “official name” here—each simply “called (his) name.”

However, the narrative structure does distinguish:  
- **The mother names him first** (in the moment of death, emotionally charged).  
- **The father immediately renames him** (possibly to override or reinterpret the meaning).

---

### 2. Meaning of *Ben-’Oni* (בֶּן־אוֹנִי)
Root: **אָוֶן / אוֹן (ʾōn)**  
Two semantic ranges exist:

1. *ʾōn* = “strength, vigor” (positive: Genesis 49:3 “Reuben, my firstborn, my might and the beginning of my strength” — רֵאשִׁית אוֹנִי).  
2. *ʾōn / ʾāven* = “sorrow, trouble, affliction” (negative, as in Hosea 9:10 “Beth-Aven,” “house of iniquity/sorrow”).

So *Ben-’Oni* could mean either “son of my sorrow” or “son of my strength.” The Masoretic vocalization favors *Oni* (ending with *-i* = “my”), making it personal: “my sorrow” or “my vigor.” Context—Rachel dying—suggests sorrow.

---

### 3. Meaning of *Binyamin* (בִּנְיָמִין)
Root: **יָמִין (yāmīn)** = “right hand,” often idiomatic for “south” or “favored side.”  
So *Binyamin* means “son of the right hand” (or “son of the south”). Both meanings connote strength, good fortune, or positional favor.

---

### 4. Narrative Implication
The text implies Jacob deliberately *reframed* the child’s name. Rachel’s naming is emotional and mortal; Jacob’s renaming is theological and enduring. In ancient Semitic practice, the father’s naming had legal and genealogical authority. Therefore:

- **Ben-Oni** functions as a **momentary epithet**, a dying mother’s utterance.  
- **Benjamin** becomes the **official covenant name** used in tribal lineage lists.

The contrast is not “nickname” in the modern casual sense (like “Benji”), but rather **recontextualization**: sorrow transformed into strength or favor.

---

### 5. Subtextual Wordplay
Hebrew storytelling often uses double-meanings. The juxtaposition of *’Oni* (sorrow/strength) and *Yamin* (right hand/fortune) suggests a deliberate inversion: sorrow becomes strength, left becomes right. This poetic reversal fits Jacob’s pattern—he blesses the younger, crosses hands later when blessing Ephraim and Manasseh (Genesis 48). So the naming scene anticipates that gesture.

---

### Conclusion
There’s no grammatical sign that *Ben-Oni* was merely a “nickname.” Both parents *“called his name.”* Yet by cultural and contextual logic, **Rachel’s name is ephemeral and emotional; Jacob’s name is permanent and legal.** The narrative preserves both to show transformation: *“son of my sorrow”* becomes *“son of my right hand.”* The Hebrew itself encodes that pivot from grief to restoration.

### 2. The Hebrew preposition
The phrase translated *“in sorrow”* is **בְּיָגוֹן** (*beyāgōn*), literally “in grief” or “with sorrow.”

- The prefix **בְּ- (be-)** means *in*, *with*, or *by means of*.  
- It is not **עִם (ʿim)**, which would explicitly mean *together with* or *along with* (as in “with her” = *ʿimmāh*).

So Jacob **does not say “with Sorrow”** in the sense of a companion (like “with Ben-Oni”); he says *“in sorrow,”* describing the condition or state of grief that will accompany his descent to Sheol.

---

### 3. Why it still connects to *Ben-Oni*
Although the preposition is *b-* (“in”), not *ʿim* (“with”), the **root idea overlaps**. The Hebrew *yāgōn* (sorrow) resonates semantically with *ʾōnî* (sorrow/affliction) from *Ben-Oni*. The consonantal structure is different (י-ג-ו-ן vs. א-ו-ן), but both words are used for inner grief, affliction, or pain.

This overlap means Jacob’s repeated laments—“you’ll bring down my gray hairs *in sorrow* to Sheol”—sound like deliberate wordplay on Benjamin’s name, even though grammatically he says *“in”*, not *“with.”* The effect in Hebrew is poetic: his life will end *beyāgōn*, echoing the *ʾōnî* of his son’s name.

---

### In sum
Jacob does **not** say *“with Ben-Oni”* in a literal grammatical sense; the Hebrew preposition is *b-* (“in sorrow”), not *ʿim* (“with”).  
But the linguistic echo between *beyāgōn* and *Ben-ʾōnî* still ties the scenes together—Rachel’s dying name for her son becomes the very word that defines Jacob’s mourning for him.
