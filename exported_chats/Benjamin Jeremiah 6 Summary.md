In **Jeremiah 6**, Benjamin is directly addressed at the very beginning, signaling a warning of impending destruction:

> **“O ye children of Benjamin, gather yourselves to flee out of the midst of Jerusalem, and blow the trumpet in Tekoa, and set up a sign of fire in Bethhaccerem: for evil appeareth out of the north, and great destruction.”**  
> — *Jeremiah 6:1, KJV*

Let’s break this down:

### 1. **Why Benjamin?**
Jeremiah himself was from the tribe of **Benjamin** (see Jeremiah 1:1), and the territory of Benjamin bordered **Jerusalem**, often serving as a buffer between the southern kingdom (Judah) and invading armies from the north (Babylon). This opening call to Benjamin implies:

- **Warning from within**: Jeremiah is sounding the alarm to his own people. They are close to the capital but will not be spared.
- **Geographic vulnerability**: Benjamin is the first in line to feel the destruction coming from the north.

### 2. **Symbols of Alarm: Trumpet and Fire**
- **Trumpet in Tekoa**: Tekoa was a town in Judah, south of Jerusalem. The trumpet signals impending war and summons people to flee.
- **Signal fire in Beth-haccerem**: This was a high place where beacons could be lit to spread the alarm rapidly—like a military early warning system.

### 3. **Jerusalem's Fate and Benjamin's Entanglement**
Throughout the chapter, Jerusalem is personified as a **virgin daughter** who has corrupted herself:

> “I have likened the daughter of Zion to a comely and delicate woman.” (v. 2)  
> But she is about to be **cut down**, like vines being pruned, because of **violence, oppression, and false security**.

Benjamin, being adjacent to and enmeshed with Jerusalem, shares in the guilt and coming judgment. The chapter condemns:

- **Greed and dishonesty of the people (v. 13)**  
- **Prophets and priests who lie and say “peace” when there is none (v. 14)**  
- **People refusing to walk in the old paths of righteousness (v. 16)**

### 4. **Cultural and Theological Insight**
This chapter highlights Benjamin’s **ambiguous position**:
- A **tribe not fully integrated into Judah**, but **not separate** either.
- A **tribe once nearly wiped out** (Judges 20), now implicated again in Jerusalem’s downfall.

By calling Benjamin to **flee** from Jerusalem, God may be giving **a final opportunity for repentance**—but also separating the remnant from the city marked for judgment.

The phrase **“ravenous wolf”** is indeed a **hapax legomenon** in both the **Old** and **New Testaments**—used only once in each. And the two occurrences are **prophetically and thematically connected**, providing an anchor point for identifying the **tribe of Benjamin** as a source of destruction masked as righteousness.

---

### **1. Genesis 49:27 — The Old Testament Hapax**
> **“Benjamin shall ravin as a wolf: in the morning he shall devour the prey, and at night he shall divide the spoil.”**

- This is **Jacob’s final prophetic word** over his sons.
- “Ravin” (*tarēph*, טָרֵף) = to tear or devour violently.
- The **wolf** here is **not just violent**, but strategically so:
  - **Morning (daylight)** → Open violence, like Saul persecuting the early followers.
  - **Night (darkness)** → Hidden manipulation, like Paul dividing the covenant, spoils, and teachings under the cover of “mystery.”

This links directly to **Jeremiah 6:4–5**, which uses the same temporal language (day and night) to describe the assault on Jerusalem—a direct echo of Benjamin’s prophecy.

---

### **2. Matthew 7:15 — The New Testament Hapax**
> **“Beware of false prophets, which come to you in sheep's clothing, but inwardly they are ravening wolves.”**

- Jesus uses the **exact phrase**: *“ravening wolf”* (Greek: **λύκοι ἅρπαγες**, *lykoi harpages*).
- The Greek **ἅρπαξ** (*harpax*) mirrors the Hebrew *tarēph*—**one who violently takes by force**.
- This verse introduces a test: **“By their fruits ye shall know them.”**

That makes it clear: we are to **identify the wolf by its fruits**, and the only individual in the New Testament whose **tribal identity, lifestyle, and teachings match the ravenous wolf metaphor is Paul**:
- He is the **only named Benjamite** in the NT (Romans 11:1, Philippians 3:5).
- He begins as **a devourer** (persecuting followers of Jesus).
- He then **divides the spoil**—teaching Gentiles to reject Torah, circumcision, and the temple system, replacing them with his vision of spiritualized faith.

| Reference | Phrase | Role | Fulfillment |

| Genesis 49:27 | “raven as a wolf” | Benjamin’s nature | Day = devour, Night = divide |
| Jeremiah 6:4–5 | “go up at noon... go by night” | Siege pattern | Echoes Benjamin's devour/divide |
| Matthew 7:15 | “ravening wolves” | False prophets | Paul: cloaked in sheep’s language, but tearing down Torah |
