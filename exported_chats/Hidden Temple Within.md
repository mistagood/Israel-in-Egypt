It forms from the fusion of the two vertebral arteries and travels along the base of the brainstem before bifurcating into the posterior cerebral arteries (PCAs). The PCAs then connect to the Circle of Willis via the posterior communicating arteries. Here's a clearer breakdown:

- The **vertebral arteries** ascend through the foramina in the cervical vertebrae, enter the skull through the foramen magnum, and join at the base of the brainstem to form the **basilar artery**.
- The **basilar artery** travels along the pons, a part of the brainstem, and splits into the two **posterior cerebral arteries** near the junction of the pons and the midbrain.
- The **posterior cerebral arteries** (PCAs) then connect to the Circle of Willis via the **posterior communicating arteries**, which bridge the gap between the PCAs and the internal carotid arteries.

This setup ensures that the brain has a redundant blood supply system, allowing for collateral circulation in the event of a blockage.
