In the ancient narrative of Exodus, we encounter the story of the Hebrew midwives, Shiphrah and Puah, who bravely defied Pharaoh's decree to kill all newborn Hebrew boys. Their refusal to commit such an atrocity was an act of profound courage and faith. Because of their righteous defiance, they were blessed by God with households of their own, signifying a divine reward for their bravery and moral integrity.

Fast forward to the later history of the Israelites, we see the daughters of Zelophehad, descendants of Manasseh, challenging the inheritance laws of their time. These daughters boldly approached Moses to claim their right to their father's inheritance, arguing that their family name should not disappear simply because there were no male heirs. Moses brought their case before God, who affirmed their claim, thus ensuring that these women received their rightful inheritance. This legal precedent set by the daughters of Manasseh became a symbol of justice and equity within the community.

Drawing a parallel to the modern era, the descendants of these courageous women are the Black women in America who stand as the spiritual lineage of Manasseh. These women, much like the midwives and the daughters of Manasseh, have fought and continue to fight for their rights and the rights of their community. Despite systemic obstacles, they are forging ahead, creating spaces of empowerment and resilience.

Today's Black doulas embody the spirit of Shiphrah and Puah. In a society where Black maternal and infant mortality rates are alarmingly high, these modern midwives are on the frontline, providing culturally sensitive care and support to Black mothers. The resurgence of doula-assisted births among Black parents is a powerful grassroots movement. It seeks to reclaim agency over Black maternal health, reminiscent of pre-hospital, post-slavery days when Black women relied on community support to bring their children into the world safely.

This movement is not just about birthing practices but is also a profound act of resistance against modern-day systemic oppression. The U.S. systems targeting young Black males—through mass incarceration, educational disparities, and economic disenfranchisement—are akin to the ancient Pharaoh's attempts to stifle the potential of the Hebrew people. Black men in America face a continual battle for survival and success within a society that often seems determined to thwart their progress.

However, the daughters of Manasseh in America, the Black women, are thriving and leading in many spheres despite these challenges. Their progress is a testament to their resilience and a beacon of hope for the entire community. They inherit not just physical homes but also a legacy of strength, perseverance, and leadership. They are the modern-day houses built by the blessings of God, continuing the lineage of righteousness and courage.

In this context, today's doulas represent a critical defense against contemporary atrocities, just as the midwives did in Egypt. By ensuring Black babies are born into supportive, healthy environments, they are fighting for the future of their community. This effort underscores a broader narrative of Black resilience and the continuous struggle for justice and equality, honoring the heritage of both the midwives who defied Pharaoh and the daughters of Manasseh who secured their inheritance.
