The concept of the **week** and its emergence in biblical history or broader ancient culture is a fascinating subject. As you suggested, it is important to explore whether the seven-day week, as we now observe it, originated from divine command or was influenced by ancient cultures, such as the Sumerians or Babylonians, and how it became formalized over time.

### Early Biblical Texts and the Seven-Day Cycle

In the earliest biblical texts, particularly in the Torah, we see the concept of a **seven-day period** in the Creation story (Genesis 1) and the commandment to observe the **Sabbath** on the seventh day (Exodus 20:8-11). This seven-day rhythm seems to emphasize the holiness of the Sabbath, the day of rest, rather than instituting a recurring weekly structure. The Torah repeatedly ties the **seventh day** to creation, yet as you pointed out, the emphasis is not on a continual cycle of "weeks" but rather on **specific events** and **numbered days**, often connected to the lunar cycle.

### Post-Exilic Influence: Babylonian Connection?

The seven-day week appears to have taken root as a more formalized structure after the Babylonian exile (around the 6th century BCE). There is compelling evidence that the Babylonians, who influenced the Jewish people during their exile, had their own version of a **seven-day week** connected to their lunar calendar. The Babylonians used a calendar where months were divided into four parts of roughly seven days each, based on the phases of the moon—new moon, first quarter, full moon, and last quarter. Each of these phases was marked by religious festivals or activities, with the **seventh day** (or certain significant seventh days) being treated as holy or auspicious.

While the Babylonians did not observe the Sabbath in the same way as the Israelites, they did honor the **seventh day** in religious contexts, often connected to their gods. Their system of worship and cosmic order, particularly the reverence for celestial bodies, influenced the way they divided time.

### The Sumerian Precedent

Going back further, there are traces of Sumerian influence on timekeeping, where they also divided their months into segments based on the lunar phases. The Sumerians are among the first recorded civilizations to use a **base-60 system**, which later influenced the way hours, days, and weeks were calculated. They did not explicitly refer to a seven-day week, but their system of dividing time was inherited and adapted by the Babylonians.

### The Formalization of the Seven-Day Week in Jewish Tradition

The influence of Babylonian culture on Jewish practices cannot be overlooked. During the Babylonian Exile, many Jewish practices evolved, and after returning to Judah, the exiles brought back new elements, including influences on the calendar and timekeeping. By this point, the **seven-day cycle** may have become more ingrained in Jewish culture, not merely as a reflection of the creation story, but also as a structure absorbed from Babylonian timekeeping methods.

In the **post-exilic period**, Jewish texts increasingly refer to the Sabbath within the context of a recurring **seven-day week**. By the time of the Second Temple period and later rabbinic tradition, the **week** as a continual cycle of seven days, culminating in the Sabbath, became more formalized.

### The Influence of Roman Culture

The seven-day week gained further traction through its adoption by the **Romans** in the 1st century CE. Initially, the Romans used an eight-day week, but with the increasing influence of Jewish and later Christian practices, the seven-day week became the standard in the Roman world. The **Roman Empire** played a key role in spreading the seven-day cycle throughout Europe and beyond, contributing to the system we know today.

### Conclusion: A Doctrine of Men?

Based on these historical connections, it seems plausible that the **week**, as we understand it today, may indeed have roots in **Babylonian** or **Sumerian** practices rather than being a direct command from God as found in the Torah. The emphasis in Moses' teachings is on **specific days** (like the Sabbath and festival days) rather than a formal seven-day week. The transition from a purely lunar or event-driven calendar to a fixed seven-day cycle likely reflects the **blending of cultural influences**—particularly Babylonian—during the post-exilic period.

Thus, your hypothesis that this concept of the week could be a "doctrine of men" rather than a direct divine command holds weight. The establishment of a seven-day week as a continuous cycle seems more aligned with ancient **Mesopotamian** religious practices and was later absorbed into Jewish, Roman, and ultimately Christian and global traditions.
