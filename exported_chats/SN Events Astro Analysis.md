- **Location**: Constellation Lupus
- **Distance**: 7,200 light-years (2.2 kpc)
- **Description**: SN 1006 is considered the brightest observed stellar event in recorded history, reaching an estimated −7.5 visual magnitude, which is roughly sixteen times the brightness of Venus. **SN 1054**

**SN 1054** is a supernova that was first observed on 4 July 1054, and remained visible for around two years. The event was recorded in contemporary Chinese astronomy, and references to it are also found in a later (13th-century) Japanese document, and in a document from the Arab world. Furthermore, there are a number of proposed, but doubtful, references from European sources recorded in the 15th century, and a pictograph associated with the Anasazi Native American culture found in the U.S. state of Arizona.

The remnant of SN 1054, which consists of debris ejected during the explosion, is known as the **Crab Nebula**. It lies in the Perseus Arm of the Milky Way galaxy, at a distance of about 6,500 light-years from Earth. The nebula was discovered in 1731 by John Bevis, and it corresponds to the bright supernova recorded by Chinese astronomers in their annals.

The core of the exploding star formed a pulsar, called the **Crab Pulsar**. The pulsar emits pulses of radiation from gamma rays to radio waves.

**SN 1572**

**SN 1572**, also known as **Tycho's Supernova**, was a supernova of Type Ia in the constellation Cassiopeia, one of about eight supernovae visible to the naked eye in historical records. It appeared in early November 1572 and was most probably first sighted by Wolfgang Schuler. The most famous observation was done by the Danish astronomer Tycho Brahe, hence the name. It was as bright as Venus and could be seen in the daytime.

The supernova remained visible to the naked eye into 1574, having gradually faded until it disappeared from view. The event was widely observed and has been described in the literature of several countries.

The remnant of SN 1572 is known as **Tycho's Supernova Remnant**. It has been studied extensively by astronomers using X-ray observatories. The X-ray emissions from the remnant are caused by shock waves — similar to sonic booms — from the supernova heating the interstellar medium.

**Unique Qualities & Mysteries:**
- The exact type of the explosion that caused the supernova is still a matter of debate among astronomers.
- The explosion was of a type that does not typically create the kind of remnants that have been observed.
- The star that exploded to create the supernova did not leave behind a neutron star or a black hole, which is unusual.

**Date:** Early November 1572  
**Remnant:** Tycho's Supernova Remnant  
**Visibility:** Visible until 1574  
**Location:** Constellation Cassiopeia  
**Distance from Earth:** Approximately 10,000 light-years

**SN 1993J**

- **Location**: Galaxy M81
- **Discovery**: SN 1993J was discovered on 28 March 1993 by F. Garcia in Spain.
- **Brightness**: At the time of its discovery, it was the second-brightest type II supernova observed in the twentieth century behind SN 1987A. It peaked at a visible apparent magnitude of 10.7 on March 30, with a second peak of 10.86 on April 18.
- **Spectral Characteristics**: Initially, SN 1993J exhibited characteristics of a type II supernova with strong hydrogen spectral line emission. However, as time progressed, the hydrogen lines faded, and strong helium spectral lines appeared. This shift in spectral characteristics led to its classification as a type IIb supernova, which is an intermediate class between type II and type Ib. This discovery suggested that type Ib and Ic supernovae might form through explosions of giant stars in processes similar to those in type II supernovae.
- **Progenitor**: The progenitor of SN 1993J was identified in pre-explosion ground-based images as a K-type supergiant star. Late-time photometry and spectroscopy revealed the presence of a B-supergiant companion star.

- **Light Echoes**: Light echoes from the explosion of SN 1993J have been detected.
