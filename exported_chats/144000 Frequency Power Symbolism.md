### **1. 144,000 Hz (or 144 kHz) – Frequency**

**144 kHz** is in the **longwave radio band**, which is rarely used for commercial broadcasting today but has been used for:

- **Time signal stations** (like WWVB in the U.S.)
- **Low-frequency communications**, especially for:
  - Maritime navigation systems (LORAN-C)
  - Military communication
  - Submarine communication (due to longwave penetration of water)

At 144 kHz:
- The **bandwidth** is narrow (a few kilohertz), meaning only low-data-rate transmissions are possible.
- You can send:
  - Morse code (CW)
  - Very low-bitrate digital signals (e.g., time codes, weather data)
  - Simple voice transmissions with limited fidelity

---

### **2. 144,000 kHz (or 144 MHz) – Frequency**

**144 MHz** is part of the **VHF (Very High Frequency)** band. Specifically:
- It's in the **2-meter amateur radio band**, which spans **144–148 MHz** in many countries.

At this frequency:
- **Voice, digital data, and even video** can be transmitted.
- Common uses:
  - **Ham (amateur) radio**
  - **Packet radio**
  - **Repeater communications**
  - **Satellite uplinks/downlinks**
  - **Line-of-sight terrestrial communication**

This is a very flexible frequency band with widespread amateur radio use.

---

### **3. 144,000 kW – Power**

**144,000 kilowatts** (or **144 megawatts**) is an **enormous amount of power**. For perspective:
- Standard **AM broadcast stations** in the U.S. may transmit at 50 kW.
- The highest-powered shortwave stations are around 500–1,000 kW.
- 144,000 kW is more akin to the output of a **large power plant**, not a transmitter.

This amount of **radio transmission power** is **unrealistic** and would violate all international broadcasting norms. However, in a metaphorical, symbolic, or speculative context (such as sci-fi or biblical symbolism), it could represent a theoretical or symbolic *world-scale broadcast or energy event*.
