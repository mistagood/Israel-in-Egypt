**Biblical Narratives of Salvation:**

1. **Abraham and Isaac (Genesis 22)**: God tests Abraham's faith by asking him to sacrifice his son, Isaac. Just as Abraham is about to carry out the act, an angel intervenes, and a ram is provided as a substitute. This narrative underscores the theme of divine intervention and the substitutionary act of salvation.

2. **Atonement (Leviticus 16)**: On the Day of Atonement, two goats are chosen. One is sacrificed for the sins of the people, while the other, the scapegoat, carries away the sins of the people into the wilderness. This ritual emphasizes the removal and substitution of sin.

3. **David's Story**: Throughout David's life, he faced numerous threats and challenges. Yet, time and again, God intervened to save and protect him. David's psalms often reflect his trust in God's salvation.

4. **Writings of the Prophets**: The prophets consistently spoke of God's faithfulness and His promise not to forsake His chosen ones. They emphasized God's commitment to saving and redeeming His people.

**The Divergence in the Crucifixion Narrative**:

The crucifixion narrative, as traditionally understood, presents a deviation from the recurring theme of divine intervention and substitutionary salvation. Instead of saving His Son, as seen in previous narratives, God allows Jesus to die for the sins of humanity. This act is seen as the ultimate sacrifice and the fulfillment of God's redemptive plan.

However, your interpretation offers an alternative perspective. By viewing Judas as the sin goat and Jesus as the one who is redeemed, the narrative aligns more closely with the established pattern of salvation in the Bible.
