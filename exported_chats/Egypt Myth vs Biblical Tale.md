The myths of Horus, Set, Osiris, and Isis from Egyptian mythology and the biblical figures of Cain, Abel, Seth, and Jesus are indeed rich in narrative and symbolism, often reflecting the universal themes of rivalry, redemption, and resurrection. Here's a brief overview of the two sets of stories:

**Egyptian Mythology:**
- **Osiris:** He is a god of fertility, agriculture, the afterlife, the dead, resurrection, life, and vegetation. Osiris was murdered by his brother Set, who was jealous of his power. Osiris's body was cut into pieces and scattered across Egypt.
- **Isis:** Osiris's wife and sister, Isis, is a goddess of magic, motherhood, and fertility. She reassembled Osiris's body and brought him back to life, long enough to conceive their son, Horus.
- **Horus:** Horus, the son of Osiris and Isis, is often depicted as a falcon-headed god. He represents sky, war, and protection. In a famous myth, Horus battles Set to avenge his father's death and claim the throne of Egypt.
- **Set:** Set, the god of chaos, deserts, storms, and foreigners, killed and dismembered his brother Osiris out of jealousy. He is Horus's uncle and arch-nemesis, representing the personification of evil and chaos.

**Biblical Figures:**
- **Cain and Abel:** Cain and Abel were the sons of Adam and Eve. Cain, the firstborn, became a farmer, and Abel became a shepherd. When God favored Abel's sacrifice over Cain's, Cain, in a fit of jealousy, killed Abel. This story symbolizes the first murder and the themes of jealousy and brotherly conflict.
- **Seth:** Seth is mentioned as the third son of Adam and Eve, born after Abel's murder. He is seen as a replacement for Abel and is considered by some traditions to be the ancestor of Noah, thus all humanity after the flood.
- **Jesus:** In Christian theology, Jesus is the Son of God, whose life and teachings are recorded in the New Testament. His crucifixion and resurrection are central to Christian beliefs and symbolize the ultimate sacrifice for humanity's sins and the promise of eternal life.

**Comparative Analysis:**
- Both sets of myths deal with themes of jealousy, fraternal conflict, death, and resurrection.
- Horus's battle with Set mirrors the conflict between good and evil, similar to the moral and spiritual battles depicted in various biblical stories.
- The resurrection of Osiris by Isis has parallels with the resurrection of Jesus, symbolizing renewal and the victory of life over death.
- The sibling rivalry and murder in both myths (Cain and Abel, Osiris and Set) reflect on human nature and the consequences of envy and hatred.

These stories, while rooted in different cultures and religious beliefs, reflect universal themes that resonate with human experience across civilizations. The idea that they may originate from a common source or reflect a collective unconscious, as proposed by theorists like Carl Jung, suggests that such myths are fundamental to understanding human nature and societal values.
