## **1. *Beriah* (בְּרִיעָה) — Hebrew Name**

### **Hebrew Spelling**
בְּרִיעָה  
*Berîʿāh*

### **Root**
The name is built on the Hebrew root **ב־ר־ע (b-r-ʿ)**.

### **Meaning**
The plain meaning comes directly from the root idea of **calamity / misfortune / evil occurrence**.

The strongest lexical basis comes from:

**בְּרִיעָה — “in evil,” “in trouble,” “born in misfortune.”**  
Root **בְּרַע / בְּרָע** = “in evil,” “in a bad circumstance.”

This is why Genesis explicitly explains his name:

**Genesis 46:17**  
“…and the sons of Asher; *Jimnah, and Ishuah, and Isui, and Beriah,* and Serah their sister…”

**1 Chronicles 7:23** provides the semantic key:  
“…and he called his name **Beriah**, because it went *evil* with his house.”

Here the text *itself* ties the name to **בְּרָע – “in calamity.”**

### **Literal Definition**
**Beriah = “in calamity,” “born in misfortune,” “in evil circumstances.”**

Nothing symbolic—this is straight lexical Hebrew.

---

## **2. *Berean* (Βεροιῆς) — Greek Title**

### **Greek Spelling**
Βεροιῆς (Bereōs), from the city **Berea** (Βέροια).

### **Meaning**
Greek origin is uncertain. Three scholarly possibilities:

1. From **phero** (φέρω, “to carry/bear”) → *folk etymology only*  
2. From a Macedonian local root → *most probable*  
3. From Semitic influence—unlikely in mainstream linguistics

### **Literal Definition**
**Berean = a person from the city of Berea.**  
(Acts 17:10–11)

There is **no Hebrew root** in the Greek term.

---

## **3. Are *Beriah* and *Berean* Linguistically Connected?**

Strict linguistic answer:

**No.**  
They are not connected etymologically.

### **But phonetically?**
Yes—**they sound similar**:

- Beriah (bər-EE-ah)  
- Berea / Berean (bə-REE-ə / bə-REE-ən)

That similarity is **phonetic**, not etymological.

### **Semantically?**
- **Beriah** = “born in calamity.”  
- **Berean** = “someone from Berea.”

No overlap.

---

## **4. Why People Commonly Conflate Them**
Because they look and sound similar in English transliteration:

- B-R-A pattern appears in both.
- Endings “-iah” and “-ean” appear similar.

But the internal structure is completely different:

| Name | Language | Root | Meaning |
|------|----------|--------|----------|
| **Beriah** | Hebrew | ב־ר־ע | in evil, born in misfortune |
| **Berean** | Greek | local Macedonian root | resident of Berea |

# **Berea — Meaning and Origin**

## **Language**
Greek  
Βέροια (Beroia) → later Latinized as *Berea*

## **Meaning**
**The meaning of Berea is *unknown*.**

That is the academically correct answer.

The name does **not** come from a known Greek root. It is considered:

- Pre-Greek (pre-Hellenic)  
- Macedonian / Thracian in origin  
- A non-translatable place-name

This is why all major lexicons (BDAG, LSJ, Strong's, Thayer’s) list it as:

> **Berea – a Macedonian place name of uncertain meaning.**

---

# **Etymological Theories (None Proven)**

Scholars have proposed possibilities, but none are accepted:

### **1. From Greek *pherō* (φέρω) — “to carry, to bear”**
Pure folk-etymology.  
Not linguistically supported.

### **2. From Greek *barus* (βαρύς) — “heavy, weighty”**
Also not supported.  
No historical phonological transition matches.

### **3. From Semitic root *beraa* (“to create”)**
Appears only in Christian homiletic writing, not real linguistics.

### **4. Thracian / Macedonian hydronym**
Most likely theory.  
Many early Macedonian cities were names of streams, valleys, or springs.

**Berea sits at the foot of Mount Bermion, beside ancient water sources.**  
So the name is likely environmental—but the actual root is lost.

---

# **What We *Can* Say with Certainty**

### **Berea = A Macedonian city name whose etymology is unknown.**

### **Berean = A person from Berea.**  
(Acts 17:10–11)

There is **zero connection** to Hebrew **Beriah** or any Hebrew root.

# **1. Rephah (רְפָא / רְפָה)**

### **Hebrew Spelling**
רְפָא — *Rephā*  
(Variant: רְפָה)

### **Root**
From the Hebrew root **ר–פ–א (r-p-ʾ)** or **ר–פ–ה (r-p-h)** depending on the vocalization.

There are two possible meanings depending on which root the name is tied to, and lexicons list both possibilities.

---

## **Meaning Option 1 (Most Common): “Healer” / “Healing”**
From root **רָפָא** (rapha) = **to heal**.

This is the same root as:

- **רֹפֵא (rofeh)** — physician  
- **יְהוָה רָפְאֶךָ** — YHWH your healer (Exodus 15:26)

So **Rephah** can mean:

- **“Healing”**  
- **“Healed”**  
- **“One who heals”**  

---

## **Meaning Option 2: “Weak,” “Slack,” “Relaxed,” “Let go”**
From root **רָפָה** (raphah) = **to relax, weaken, slacken**.

Used in verses like:

- “hands **grew weak**” (2 Sam 4:1)
- “do not **slacken** your hand” (Joshua 10:6)

This yields meanings like:

- **“Weakening”**  
- **“Relaxation”**  
- **“Slackness”**

---

## **Biblical Context**
Rephah appears in 1 Chronicles 7:25 as a descendant of Ephraim.  
The text does **not** define the name, so we rely purely on root analysis.

# **Sheerah (שֶׁאֱרָה)**

# **1. Hebrew Spelling**
שֶׁאֱרָה  
*She’erah* or *Se’erah*

---

# **2. Root**
Her name is built on the Hebrew root:

### **ש–א–ר (sh–ʾ–r)**  
The core meaning of this root is:

- **to remain, to be left over**  
- **to survive**  
- **to constitute a remnant**  
- **kinship / flesh / relative** (via the noun *she’er*)

It is a root of **continuation, survival, remaining**, and **blood-relations**.

This is the same root in the terms:

- **she’er (שְׁאֵר)** — flesh, close kin (Lev 18:6)  
- **she’ar (שְׁאָר)** — remnant (Isa 10:20–22)  
- **she’erit (שְׁאֵרִית)** — remnant, survivors (Zeph 2:9)

So the meaning domain is very stable.

---

# **3. Literal Meaning of “Sheerah”**
Based on morphology and root semantics:

### **Sheerah = “kinswoman,” “relative,” “flesh,” or “remnant woman.”**  
Other academically supported nuances:

- **“The one who remains”**  
- **“She who survives”**  
- **“Remnant (female)”**  
- **“Family / kin woman”**

Her name *literally* ties her to **remnant** and **continuity**.

---

# **4. Linguistic Notes**
- The prefix **שֶׁ– (she-)** + the root **ʾ-r** yields a feminine form indicating **one who is kin** or **one who remains**.
- The internal aleph (אֱ) marks the noun-formation from the root *she’er* (flesh/kin).

This gives us a very clear lexical identity.
