whats the difference between the modern definition of scapegoat and the levitical scapegoat tradition?

---

The term "scapegoat" has its origins in the religious rituals described in the Old Testament of the Bible, specifically in the book of Leviticus, but its modern usage in English has evolved to take on a different meaning.

In the Levitical tradition, a scapegoat was a literal goat that was ritually burdened with the sins of the people and then sent into the wilderness as part of the Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement) ceremonies. Two goats were selected: one was sacrificed, and the other, the scapegoat, had all the sins of the people symbolically placed upon it by the high priest. This scapegoat was then released into the wilderness, symbolically carrying away the sins of the community.

The modern definition of "scapegoat," however, has lost its literal and religious connotations. Today, a scapegoat refers to a person or group that is unjustly blamed for problems. The scapegoat bears the blame for others, often as a way of deflecting responsibility or accountability. For example, if a project at work fails and one person is singled out and blamed for the failure, even though many factors or people may have contributed to it, that person could be considered a scapegoat.

So, while both the Levitical and modern definitions involve the idea of bearing blame, the context, connotation, and usage have changed significantly. In Levitical tradition, the scapegoat is a part of a religious ritual to symbolically cleanse the people of their sins. In contrast, the modern usage usually refers to an unjust situation where blame is assigned to an innocent party or is disproportionately assigned to one party among many that may share responsibility.
