### 1. **Simon Peter as a Pharisee**
   - **Luke 7:36-50**: Here, a Pharisee named Simon invites Jesus to dinner, during which the woman with the alabaster jar anoints Jesus' feet. Simon internally questions Jesus' prophetic ability, saying, "If this man were a prophet, he would know who is touching him and what kind of woman she is—that she is a sinner."  
   - If Simon the Pharisee and Simon Peter are the same individual, this passage reveals a key aspect of Peter’s personality: his skepticism and tendency to challenge or misunderstand Jesus’ actions and mission.
   - **Matthew 16:22-23**: Peter famously rebukes Jesus for predicting his death, prompting Jesus to respond, “Get behind me, Satan! You are a stumbling block to me." This mirrors the tension in their interactions, where Peter seems to embody a mindset more aligned with worldly or traditional (Pharisaic) expectations than spiritual insight.

### 2. **Healing of the Leper and Peter's Calling**
   - **Mark 1:40-45 / Luke 5:12-16**: A man with leprosy approaches Jesus, saying, “If you are willing, you can make me clean.” Jesus, indignant, replies, “I am willing,” and heals him, commanding him to show himself to the priest as Moses commanded (Leviticus 14).  
   - If this leper is Simon Peter, it would explain Peter’s initial hesitation to fully trust Jesus as more than a healer. His early interactions with Jesus (e.g., calling him “Master” in Luke 5:5) suggest a recognition of authority but not yet the full revelation of Jesus’ identity as Messiah.

### 3. **Peter and the Miraculous Catch**
   - **Luke 5:1-11**: The miraculous catch of fish convinces Simon Peter to fall at Jesus' knees, saying, “Go away from me, Lord; I am a sinful man!” If this is a later interaction, it could signify Peter’s transition from skepticism (as a Pharisee) to belief. His acknowledgment of sinfulness might tie to his previous resistance to Jesus’ mission or failure to keep the commands (e.g., staying quiet about his healing).

### 4. **The Pharisee Among the Disciples**
   - **Mark 7:1-5**: The Pharisees and some teachers of the law question Jesus about why his disciples eat with unclean hands. If Simon Peter is a Pharisee, this passage might reflect the inner conflict among the disciples themselves, with Peter voicing concerns typical of his Pharisaic upbringing.
   - **Luke 7:39 / Matthew 26:6-13**: Simon’s reaction to the woman with the alabaster jar—whether during the Pharisee’s dinner (Luke) or the incident at Bethany (Matthew)—suggests a consistent tension in Peter’s understanding of Jesus’ mission, particularly regarding who is worthy of grace.

### 5. **Peter’s Tendency to Oppose Jesus**
   - **John 6:68-70**: When Jesus asks the Twelve if they will leave after the difficult teaching about eating his flesh and drinking his blood, Peter responds, “Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life.” Despite his loyalty, Peter’s resistance to deeper spiritual truths appears repeatedly, aligning with the Pharisaic focus on external adherence over inner transformation.
   - **Luke 22:61-62**: Peter’s denial of Jesus during his trial reflects not only fear but also lingering doubts or misunderstandings about Jesus’ role as the suffering servant, which many Pharisees struggled to accept (Isaiah 53).

### Conclusion
The evidence in the gospels allows for a plausible interpretation that Simon Peter carried Pharisaic tendencies into his discipleship. His frequent opposition to Jesus, doubts about his mission, and struggles with spiritual understanding align with the characteristics of a Pharisee. This interpretation enriches our understanding of Peter’s transformation, as he moves from a skeptical, rule-bound mindset to a faith-driven leader of the early church. 

This hypothesis would benefit from deeper examination of passages where Peter’s actions reflect Pharisaic attitudes (e.g., debates over purity laws) and further analysis of how his transformation might parallel the broader struggle of Israel to embrace Jesus as Messiah.

This harmonization offers a richer and more nuanced understanding of the events leading to the crucifixion.

3. **Prophetic Fulfillment**:
   - Jesus’ warnings to His contemporaries and future generations align with Old Testament prophecies about rebellion and judgment.
   - This wicked generation extends into the end times, where the doctrine of resurrection becomes a stumbling block, as prophesied in Isaiah and Daniel.

---

### **Conclusion**
The Bible consistently describes a "wicked generation" as one that abandons God’s law, rejects His prophets, and embraces false doctrines. These passages point to an end-times generation characterized by the distortion of Jesus’ message, particularly through the adoption of foreign ideas like resurrection and divine kingship. By harmonizing these prophecies, we see that this generation is both a continuation of Israel’s rebellious past and the culmination of spiritual corruption in the last days.
