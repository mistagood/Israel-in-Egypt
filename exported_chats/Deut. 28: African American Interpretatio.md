# Deuteronomy 28: A Verse-by-Verse Commentary Relating to African Americans

## Introduction

Deuteronomy 28 is a chapter in the Hebrew Bible that outlines blessings for obedience and curses for disobedience to the covenant between God and the Israelites. This chapter has been interpreted in various ways throughout history, and some interpretations have been applied to the experiences of African Americans. This commentary aims to explore the text in a scholarly manner, focusing on its cosmic symbols, mystic implications, and the specific context of African Americans.

---

### Verse 1: "And it shall come to pass, if thou shalt hearken diligently unto the voice of the LORD thy God, to observe and to do all his commandments which I command thee this day, that the LORD thy God will set thee on high above all nations of the earth."

#### Commentary

The Hebrew word for "hearken" is "Shama," which implies not just hearing but understanding and obeying. The promise of elevation "above all nations" is a cosmic symbol of divine favor and spiritual ascension. For African Americans, this verse can be seen as an encouragement to adhere to divine principles for collective upliftment.

---

### Verse 15: "But it shall come to pass, if thou wilt not hearken unto the voice of the LORD thy God, to observe to do all his commandments and his statutes which I command thee this day; that all these curses shall come upon thee, and overtake thee."

#### Commentary

The term "curses" in Hebrew is "Klalah," which signifies a reversal of divine favor. The text suggests that disobedience leads to a cosmic imbalance. This has been interpreted by some as explaining the historical suffering of African Americans, although such a view can be problematic when it implies that suffering is a result of collective disobedience rather than systemic injustice.

---

### Verse 32: "Thy sons and thy daughters shall be given unto another people, and thine eyes shall look, and fail with longing for them all the day long: and there shall be no might in thy hand."

#### Commentary

This verse has been cited to draw parallels with the Transatlantic Slave Trade, where African families were torn apart and "given unto another people." The phrase "no might in thy hand" symbolizes powerlessness against oppressive forces. However, it's crucial to note that using this verse to justify or explain the slave trade would be a flawed interpretation that ignores the systemic evils involved.

---

### Verse 68: "And the LORD shall bring thee into Egypt again with ships, by the way whereof I spake unto thee, Thou shalt see it no more again: and there ye shall be sold unto your enemies for bondmen and bondwomen, and no man shall buy you."

#### Commentary

The term "Egypt" is metaphorically used to represent a place of bondage and suffering. The "ships" have been interpreted as the slave ships in the Transatlantic Slave Trade. However, it's important to clarify that this verse should not be used to legitimize or rationalize the enslavement of African Americans. Rather, it serves as a cosmic warning against disobedience to divine law.

---

## Conclusion

Deuteronomy 28 is a complex chapter with deep cosmic symbols and mystic implications. While some have applied its verses to the African American experience, it's essential to approach these interpretations with scholarly rigor and ethical sensitivity. The text serves as a spiritual guide and should not be used to justify historical or systemic injustices.
